1.0
Biological Evidence — Blood
Detection · Presumptive Tests · Confirmatory Tests · Sample Collection
▼
1.1 Detection Methods
  • White light / low-power microscope — standard examination; inadequate on dark backgrounds.
  • Alternate Light Source (Polilight) — blood absorbs at 415 nm; useful on dark fabrics.
  • Infrared (IR) illumination — wavelength 800–1000 nm; needs IR-sensitive camera + software.
  • Luminol — chemiluminescence on oxidation by Hb; detects latent stains on large surfaces; NOT ideal as first choice (see limitations).
  • Fluorescein — fluoresces at 425–485 nm (blue light); observe in dark with yellow-filter goggles.
⚠ Luminol — Use as Last Resort
  • Only a presumptive test; stain too dilute for confirmatory testing if only luminol-visible.
  • False positives: copper ions, iron compounds, cobalt, potassium permanganate, bleach, ferricyanide, plant peroxidases.
  • Destroys several genetic markers.
  • Water-based — smears bloody impressions; further dilutes stains.
  • Must be used in total darkness.
1.2 Presumptive Blood Tests
  • Kastle–Meyer (Phenolphthalein) Test — based on peroxidase activity of Hb; colorless phenolphthalein → bright pink. Steps: swab stain → ethanol → KM solution → H₂O₂. Immediate pink = positive. Sensitivity: 1:10 dilution. False positives: cauliflower/broccoli peroxidases. Does not differentiate species.
  • Benzidine, Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB), Leucomalachite Green, Ortho-toluidine — catalytic peroxidase tests. Note: benzidine and ortho-toluidine are carcinogens.
  • Fluorescein test — Hb oxidizes fluorescein in H₂O₂; viewed under blue light (425–485 nm) in darkness.
1.3 Confirmatory Blood Tests
  • Takayama (Hemochromogen) Test — specific for Hb; forms pink needle/rhomboid crystals of pyridine ferroprotoporphyrin. NaOH releases heme; glucose reduces iron; pyridine combines with it. Warm slide → view microscopically. Positive = crystals present. Note: absence of crystals does NOT confirm absence of blood.
  • RSID™-Blood Strip Test — uses two monoclonal antibodies in lateral flow format to detect human glycophorin A (specific to RBCs). RSID Reader uses CCD imaging for pixel density analysis → positive/negative result. Calibrated from 800+ samples.
  • ABAcard® HemaTrace® — detects human Hb via antigen-antibody reaction. Two pink lines (T + C) = positive; one pink line (C only) = negative; no C line = invalid. False positives with ferret blood.
  • Hexagon OBTI, Heme-Select — immunological; confirm human Hb. May cross-react with primate blood.
ABAcard® Procedure Summary
Cut ~3 mm² sample → add 300 µL extraction buffer → vortex → incubate 1–5 min → add 150 µL to well "S" → read at 10 minutes.
1.4 Individualization After Confirmation
  • DNA profiling — current gold standard for source identification.
  • ABO typing / Hematrace cards — species of origin.
  • Ouchterlony — older immunodiffusion test for species origin.

2.0
Blood Spatter Analysis (BSA)
Types · Velocity · Angle of Impact · Point of Origin · Height Calculation
▼
2.1 Overview

BSA (Blood Stain Pattern Analysis) determines: number of blows, victim/attacker position, weapon type, distance from origin, direction of movement, and type of force. All BSA findings are circumstantial evidence. Key figure: Paul Kirk (1902–1970), UC Berkeley; instrumental in Sam Sheppard case.

2.2 Three Main Categories of Bloodstains
1. Passive
  • Gravity acting alone: drops, flows, pools.
  • Open wound dripping while victim moves.
  • Size ≥ 4 mm diameter (low force).
2. Transfer
  • Wet bloody surface contacts secondary surface.
  • Swipe: wet blood transferred to clean surface.
  • Wipe: object moves through wet bloodstain.
  • Examples: bloody shoe prints, body drag marks.
3. Projected (Impact)
  • Arterial Spurt/Gush: blood under pressure from breached artery.
  • Cast-off: blood thrown from a moving blood-bearing object.
  • Impact Spatter: blood source receives blow; random dispersion; size depends on force.
  • Backspatter: blood directed back toward energy source (e.g., gunshot entrance wound).
2.3 Velocity Classification
TypeSpeedDiameterTypical Cause
Low Velocity (Passive)≤ 5 ft/sec≥ 4 mmGravity, dripping wound
Medium Velocity5–25 ft/sec2–4 mmBlunt force, stabbing
High Velocity> 25 ft/sec (often ~100)< 2 mm ("mist")Gunshot, explosion, collision
Important Note on "Velocity"
In BSA, "velocity" describes the force applied to blood, NOT the speed of the blood itself. Terminal velocity of a free-falling blood drop ≈ 25.1 ft/sec.
2.4 Factors Affecting Blood Drop Shape
  • Surface texture: smooth → smooth edges; rough → scalloping/spines and satellite droplets.
  • Height: shape unchanged if angle same; diameter and number of satellites increase with height.
  • Velocity: higher force → smaller diameter.
  • Angle of impact: 90° = circular; decreasing angle → increasingly elliptical/elongated; tail appears at ~30°.
2.5 Angle of Impact Calculation
Angle of Impact
sin⁻¹ (Width ÷ Length) = Angle in degrees

Rule: Do NOT measure the tail. Examples:

  • W = 9 mm, L = 18 mm → sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30°
  • W = 1.73 cm, L = 2 cm → sin⁻¹(0.865) ≈ 60°
2.6 Area of Convergence (Point of Origin)

Draw lines through the long axis of multiple bloodstains — they converge at the 2D area of origin (where the blow occurred). Use strings at crime scene for measurement.

2.7 Height / Point of Origin (3D)
Height Formula
tan(Angle of Impact) = Height ÷ Distance from convergence
→ Height = Distance × tan(Angle)

Example: angle 27°, distance 5.75 ft → Height = 5.75 × tan(27°) = 5.75 × 0.5095 ≈ 3.3 ft

  • Distance 10 ft, angle 70° → Height = 10 × tan(70°) = 27.5 ft
  • Distance 15 ft, angle 80° → Height = 15 × tan(80°) = 85.1 ft
  • Distance 10 ft, angle 75° → Height = 10 × tan(75°) = 37.3 ft
2.8 Key BSA Rules for Exams
  • A few bloodstains do NOT make a pattern — draw conclusions with reservations.
  • It is OK to admit insufficient evidence; no opinion > incorrect opinion.
  • Preponderance < 1 mm → high velocity (gunshot).
  • Preponderance ≥ 1 mm → medium velocity (beating/stabbing).
  • Blood projected upward to ceiling → usually gunshot with upward trajectory → often suicide.
  • Ceiling spatter → almost always gunshot; upward trajectory → more often suicide than homicide.
3.0
Saliva Evidence
Detection · Amylase · Components
▼
3.1 Key Points
  • Saliva cannot be rigorously identified in dried stains/swabs.
  • Classical marker = amylase enzyme (catalyzes starch hydrolysis via starch-iodine reaction: iodine turns deep blue with intact starch; not with hydrolyzed starch).
  • Amylase also found in: pancreatic juice, fecal material (limits specificity).
3.2 Composition of Saliva
Inorganic
  • Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺, HCO₃⁻, Mg²⁺, NH₃
Organic
  • Urea, uric acid, creatinine (body secretion products)
  • Putrescine, cadaverine, lipids (putrefaction products)
  • 400+ proteins: amylase, histatins, cystatins, lactoferrins, lysozymes, mucins, PRPs, albumin, sIgA, transferrin
3.3 Evidence Components
  • Enzymes: amylase
  • Genes: STATHERIN, HISTATIN 3
  • Immunoglobulins
  • Epithelial cells — provide DNA + mRNA
  • DNA cytosine methylation profiling
  • RSID™-Saliva strip (detects α-amylase in lateral flow format)
4.0
Semen Evidence
Composition · Presumptive & Confirmatory Tests · Sperm Survival
▼
4.1 Composition of Seminal Fluid
  • Seminal vesicle: 60% | Prostate: 30% | Epididymis + Bulbourethral glands: 10%
  • Physical: greyish-yellow, thick, jelly-like, characteristic odor. Quantity: 2–5 mL. Sperms: 60–150 million (90% motile). pH: 7.4
  • Vasectomized/oligospermic/aspermiac males still produce detectable seminal fluid.
4.2 Presumptive Tests for Semen
  • Visual Examination: dried semen = off-white to faint yellow; map-like outline; stiffens fabric.
  • UV / Alternate Light (Wood's Lamp / Crime Scope): semen fluoresces blue-yellow due to Flavin and Choline-conjugated proteins. Highly presumptive — many substances fluoresce similarly.
  • Acid Phosphatase (AP) Test (Brentamine Spot): prostate secretes AP at 500–1000× other body fluids. AP + Alpha-Naphthyl acid phosphate + Brentamine Fast Blue → dark purple (within 60 sec). Positive up to 36 hours. Remains presumptive (vaginal secretions also contain AP).
  • Barbario's Test: picric acid + stain → yellow needle/rhombic crystals of spermine picrate. Depends on prostatic secretions. Screening test.
  • Creatine Phosphokinase (CPK): sperm contain high CPK (normal 385–1400 units/mL). >400 units = diagnostic. More stable than AP.
  • Chemical/Fluorescent Test: stain + 10% HCl → glass slide → fluorescence solution (KI, iodine, water) → rhombic crystals of choline iodide.
4.3 Confirmatory Tests for Semen
  • Christmas Tree Stain (most reliable): Picroindigocarmine (green-blue neck/tail) + Nuclear Fast Red (red head, pink acrosomal cap). Tails deteriorate first. At least one unbroken sperm = proof of semen.
  • RSID™-Semen Strip Test: detects semenogelin (seminal vesicle-specific antigen). Unique to semen — no cross-reactivity with other bodily fluids or animal semen. Works even on degraded samples.
  • PSA / P30 / ABAcard Test: detects Prostate-Specific Antigen. Note: also found in small amounts in female urine, breast milk, sweat, some female periurethral gland secretions. Use with caution without sperm confirmation.
  • Immunological: FISH (fluorescent in-situ hybridization) — detects male epithelial cells in vagina up to 1 week post-coitus.
4.4 Sperm Motility & Survival Timeline
ConditionDuration
Full motility (room temp)3 hours

Sperms better preserved outside the body (clothing) than in the vagina (subject to phagocytosis, lysis, agglutination, degeneration). Human sperm: 50–55 µm long; head 5 µm; short tapering tail.

5.0
Vaginal Fluid Evidence
Detection · PAS Stain · Limitations
▼
  • Vaginal secretions = mixture of fluid through vaginal walls + cervical mucus + residual urine.
  • Composition changes throughout the menstrual cycle — making identification difficult.
  • Periodic Acid Schiff (PAS) Reagent: most common method — detects glycogenated epithelial cells stained magenta. Principle: periodic acid oxidizes glycol groups → dialdehydes → react with Schiff's reagent → magenta complex. Intensity ∝ cell concentration.
  • Limitation: post-menopausal or pre-pubescent females lack glycogenated cells → test useless.
  • Older methods: vaginal peptidase, lactate dehydrogenase isoenzymes — not widely used.
  • Research into RNA profiling / bacterial profiling — no reliable method yet.

Hair Evidence
Structure · Life Cycle · Racial Differences · DNA Analysis · Animal vs Human
▼
6.1 Structure
  • Cuticle: transparent outermost overlapping scales; points proximal → distal; human scales are flattened and narrow (imbricate).
  • Cortex: thickest layer; contains melanin pigment granules (denser near cuticle).
  • Medulla: central canal; not always present. Types: continuous, intermittent, fragmented. Key for species ID.
6.2 Life Cycle

Medullary Index = diameter of medulla ÷ total hair diameter. Humans < 0.33; animals > 0.50.

6.5 Treated Hair
  • Bleaching: disturbs cuticle scales, removes pigment → brittle, yellowish; sharp demarcation from natural (vs sun-bleached = gradual).
  • Timing last coloring: hair grows ~1.3 cm/month. Measure naturally colored section ÷ 1.3 = months since last coloring.
  • Dyeing: colors both cuticle and cortex.
6.6 DNA from Hair
  • Nuclear DNA (nDNA): from anagen root; highest discrimination power; from both parents. Preferred when available.
  • Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA): from hair shaft and all roots; inherited from mother only; many copies per cell; useful when nDNA unavailable. Limitation: same mtDNA within maternal lineage.
  • Microscopy comparison should ALWAYS precede DNA analysis (DNA is destructive).
  • Telogen hairs without follicular tissue → often insufficient for nDNA → use mtDNA.
  • Postmortem root band = dark band near root in decomposing remains.
6.7 Testing Hair for Substances
  • Neutron Activation Analysis (NAA): identifies up to 14 elements in 2 cm of hair; probability of two people matching 9 elements ≈ 1 in a million. Detects: antimony, argon, bromine, copper, gold, Mn, Ag, Na, Zn.
  • Chemical dissolution for drugs/toxins/heavy metals (arsenic, lead) — timeline possible (1.3 cm/month).
Part B — Sexual Offences
7.0
Sexual Offences — Overview & Classification
Definitions · Natural vs Unnatural · Legal Context
▼
7.1 Definitions
  • Sexual offense: any sexual act against another without consent, including when unable to consent.
  • Non-forcible sex offense: unlawful, non-forcible sexual intercourse (incest, statutory rape).
  • Natural offense: vaginal penetration by penis.
  • Unnatural offense: sexual intercourse not involving vaginal penetration by penis; no procreation potential.
  • Sexual perversion: sexual excitement/orgasm associated with unusual, abnormal, or deviant acts or imagery.
  • Sexual harassment: physical contact/advances; unwelcome sexual overtures; verbal or physical sexual harassment. Punishable by imprisonment.
7.2 Classification
  • Natural offenses: Rape, Statutory rape, Incest, Adultery, Defilement
  • Unnatural offenses: Sodomy, Tribadism/Lesbianism, Bestiality, Buccal/Oral Coitus
  • Other: Indecent assault, Sexual harassment, Paraphilias/Deviations
8.0
Rape — Examination, Evidence & Law
Definition · Victim Exam · Accused Exam · Incest · Adultery · Kenyan Law
▼
8.1 Definition of Rape

Unlawful sexual intercourse by a man with a woman. Constituted by:

  • Penile penetration of vagina, mouth, urethra, or anus.
  • Insertion of object/body part into vagina, mouth, urethra, or anus.
  • Manipulation of body causing penetration.

Circumstances: against will, without consent, consent by fear/fraud/intoxication/unsound mind, statutory rape (under 18), inability to communicate consent.

Key Legal Quote
"A charge of rape is easy to make, hard to prove, harder still to disprove."
8.2 Kenyan Law — Sexual Offences Act 2006
  • Rape = intentional, unlawful penetration with genital organs without consent or by force/threat. Sentence: minimum 10 years, may be enhanced to life imprisonment.
  • Attempted rape: minimum 5 years, up to life.
  • Gang rape: minimum 15 years, up to life.
8.3 Objectives of Medical Examination
  • Evidence of recent sexual intercourse.
  • Injuries suggesting violent struggle.
  • Age of victim (if needed).
  • Whether victim was intoxicated or drugged.
Important
Rape is a legal Dx, NOT a medical Dx. Medical opinion states only whether there is "evidence of sexual intercourse" — never use the word "rape" in a medical report.
8.4 Precautions
  • Authorization: written order from police/court required.
  • Identification: by relatives, police, ID marks.
  • Female nurse present throughout.
  • No delay — examine as early as possible.
8.5 Examination of Victim — Sequence
  • Preliminary data: reference letter, name/age/address, brought by/identified by.
  • Consent: age >12 yrs → witnessed, written, informed consent required.
  • History: menstrual history, marital status, obstetric history, venereal diseases, resistance offered, bath/washing done.
  • Clothes examination: confirm unchanged; remove on white paper; check for tears, missing buttons, stains (mud, grass, blood, semen). Seal, label, send for chemical analysis.
  • General examination: general build, gait, mental condition (intoxicated/drugged), age findings, nail damage, stains, injuries.
  • Injuries: petechiae (face/conjunctiva), injuries around mouth/neck, wrists, arms, thighs, legs, buttocks, back, breasts, bite marks.
  • Genital examination: vulva, labia majora/minora, hymen, vagina, perineum — document injuries, tenderness, inflammation, presence/absence of semen.
8.6 Specimens from Victim
  • Clothes (if stained) — air dry, send intact.
  • Blood: plain (grouping) + anticoagulated (alcohol/drugs).
  • Urine: drugs screening.
  • Nail scrapings: blood/skin of accused (DNA).
  • Body stains: dry → scrape into envelope; moist → swab in distilled water.
  • Pubic hair: comb for foreign hairs; cut 20 pubic hairs for comparison.
  • Vaginal swabs: (a) introitus/perineum swab, (b) low vaginal swab, (c) high vaginal swab, (d) cervical mucus swab (if >24h after alleged rape).
  • Colposcopy: detect injuries.
  • Toluidine blue: stains injured areas.
  • UV light: seminal stains.
8.7 Examination of Accused
  • Consent: if refused, reasonable force may be used.
  • Clothes and undergarments, foreign hairs/fibers, fingernail scrapings, scalp hair, bite mark swabs, pubic hair comb + clippings, urethral swabs, glans swabs.
  • Blood: serology, drugs/intoxicants, DNA profiling, STDs, seminal fluid.
  • Genital exam: development, anomalies (rule out impotency), injuries (abrasions, frenulum tears).
  • Vaginal secretions on glans: moist filter paper + Lugol's iodine (blue = glycogen from vaginal cells); or Papanicolaou stain for vaginal epithelial cells.
  • Smegma assessment: cheesy sebaceous secretion on corona glandis — comment on presence/absence.
8.8 Complications of Rape
  • Haemorrhage and shock
  • Death (assault, suffocation, strangulation, intoxicants, suicide)
  • Mental agony; Rape Trauma Syndrome
  • STIs/STDs
  • Pregnancy
8.9 Incest

Sexual intercourse between persons within prohibited degrees of relationship (daughter, granddaughter, sister, stepsister, aunt, etc.). Types: Consanguinal, Affinial, Quasi-relative. Electra complex (father-daughter), Oedipus complex (mother-son), Pharaonic (brother-sister).

Kenyan Law: Minimum 10 years; life imprisonment if victim is under 18. Consent is immaterial.

Circumstances: family disorganization, low socioeconomic status, overcrowding, lack of parental supervision, alcohol, cerebral disease.

8.10 Adultery

Voluntary sexual intercourse between a married person and someone other than their spouse. Not a crime in Kenya but grounds for divorce (Marriage Act 2013).

9.0
Unnatural Sex Offences
Sodomy · Lesbianism · Bestiality · Buccal Coitus
▼
9.1 Sodomy (Buggery / Greek Love)

Anal intercourse between two males (homosexual) or male and female (heterosexual). Pederasty = with a boy (catamite); Gerontophilia = with an adult passive agent.

Non-habituated Passive Agent
  • Pain and tenderness
  • Lubricant/foreign pubic hair in/around anus
  • Perianal abrasions and bruising
  • Anal lacerations (triangular, based at external sphincter)
  • Fresh/dried semen around anus
Habitual Passive Agent
  • No blood/pain typically
  • Perianal hair often shaved
  • Perianal skin thickened/keratinized
  • Lateral traction test positive
  • Funnel-shaped depression toward anus
  • Anus: dilated, patulous, loss of rugosity; fissures/sinuses
  • Rectum: mucosal prolapse; STD evidence
Only Proof of Sodomy
The presence of semen in the anus. All other signs may disappear within 1–2 days.

Kenyan Law (Cap 165): Gross indecency between males = 5 years. With consent → both punishable. Without consent → only active agent. Not legalized in Kenya. Ground for divorce under Marriage Act.

Samples: swab from perianal region, swab from anal canal, foreign pubic hair, undergarments.

9.2 Lesbianism / Tribadism

Female homosexuality; gratification of sexual desire between women; clitoris is the primary organ. Tribadism = form of mental aberration; seen in women with repulsion toward men or nymphomania.

Kenyan Law (Cap 162): Carnal knowledge against order of nature = 14 years; 21 years if without consent or consent obtained by force/threats.

9.3 Bestiality / Zoophilia

Sexual intercourse with animals (vaginal, anal, or oral). Sheep used by males; dogs/cats by females. True coitus uncommon — usually oral manipulation by animal.

Evidence in accused: dung/blood stains on clothes, body injuries, penile injury, fecal matter/vaginal secretion/animal hair on penis, transmitted infections.

Evidence in animal: genital injuries, presence of human spermatozoa (sure evidence).

Kenyan Law (Cap 162): 14 years (same section as unnatural carnal knowledge). Also: cruelty to animals under Cap 360 (fine ≤ 3,000 KES or ≤ 6 months or both).

9.4 Buccal / Oral Coitus (Sin of Gomorrah)

Fellatio = oral stimulation of penis. Cunnilingus = oral stimulation of female genitalia. Now considered normal sexual foreplay.

  • Active partner findings: abrasions; amylase detected on penile swabs.
  • Passive partner findings: spermatozoa in oral cavity.
  • Victim evidence: buccal swabs within 8 hours of oro-penile contact reveal seminal traces (unless teeth cleaned or hot drinks consumed).
  • Deaths: aspiration of semen; impaction of penis in oropharynx.
  • Dx: semen in respiratory tract/stomach.

Kenyan Law: No specific law outlawing consensual oral coitus; becomes criminal if without consent or by force/threats. If non-consensual and repetitive → ground for divorce.

Part C — Pregnancy, Abortion & Infanticide
11.0
Pregnancy
Signs · Duration · Tests · Medicolegal Importance · Special Pregnancies
▼
11.1 Key Definitions & Duration
  • Pregnancy: implantation of fertilized ovum in uterine endometrium.
  • Average: 280 days | Maximum: 354 days | Minimum: 180 days
  • Zygote → Embryo (after implantation to 8th week) → Fetus (after 8th week)
  • Viable age: capability to sustain life independently. Average = 210 days (7 months); Earliest = 180 days (6 months).
11.2 Signs of Pregnancy
CategorySigns
PresumptiveAmenorrhoea, morning sickness, breast changes, Jacquemier's sign (vaginal color → violet-blue, 4th wk), skin pigmentation, sympathetic disturbances, urinary frequency, constipation, linea nigra, striae, altered food cravings, quickening (multip: 16–18 wk; primip: 18–20 wk)
ProbableProgressive abdominal enlargement, uterine height, Hegar's sign (cervical softening, 7–10 wk), Palmer's sign (intermittent uterine contractions, 4–8 wk), Goodell's sign (extreme cervix softening + blue, ~4 mo), uterine souffle (end of 4th month), ballottement, pregnancy test
Positive/CertainFetal movement (after 20 wk), fetal heart sounds (18–20 wk, 120–160 bpm), palpation of fetal parts, USG (6th wk), radiology (after 3rd month), MRI

Braxton Hicks: intermittent painless contractions lasting ~1 min, relaxation 2–3 min. Probable sign.

Chadwick's/Jacquemier's sign: vaginal mucosa turns pink → violet/blue due to venous obstruction.

11.3 Pregnancy Tests
  • Biological: Aschheim-Zondek test, Frank test, Friedman test, Hogben/Xenopus test, Galli-Mainini (male frog test)
  • Immunological/Serological: agglutination-inhibition, haemagglutination-inhibition, RIA, ELISA
  • Imaging: USG, radiology, MRI

11.6 Premature vs Post-mature Baby
Premature (<265 days)
  • Smaller size, lower birth weight (<2.5 kg)
  • Undescended testes, eyes more closed
  • Less developed nails
Post-mature (>295 days)
  • Oversize (>20 inches), over-weight
  • Hair >7 inches; nails beyond fingers
  • Radiology: ossification in upper tibia and cuboid bone

12.1 Signs of Recent Delivery (Living)
  • General: pale, exhausted, ill-looking.
  • Breasts: full, enlarged, tender, knotty; colostrum/milk present; dark areola, enlarged nipple.
  • Abdomen: pendulous, wrinkled, striae gravidarum.
  • Uterus: contracted, hard muscular tumour feel.
  • Vagina: smooth, relaxed, capacious; may have recent tears (heals by day 7); rugae reappear by week 3.
  • Labia: tender, swollen, bruised/lacerated.
  • Cervix: soft, dilated; internal os begins closing within 24h.
  • Lochia: Lochia rubra (4–5 days) → Lochia serosa (4–5 days) → Lochia alba (up to 2–3 weeks).
  • Perineum: sometimes lacerated.
  • Intermittent uterine contractions.
12.2 Signs of Remote Delivery
  • Abdomen: white/multiple scars on lateral aspects.
  • Breasts: lax, soft, pendulous; enlarged nipples, darkened areola.
  • Vulva: partially open (labia don't fully close).
  • Cervix: irregular scars; external os irregular/fissured.
  • Uterus (PM): larger (10×6×2.5 cm), heavier (80–100 g); concave inner walls; wider cavity; placental attachment scar; transverse slit external os.

13.1 Definition & Types

Premature expulsion of fetus from uterus at any stage before full term. Spontaneous (natural/accidental) occurs mostly at 2nd–3rd month (~15–20% of pregnancies).

TypeDescription
Natural (Spontaneous)Chromosomal defects, developmental defects, Rh incompatibility, uterine anomalies, hormonal deficiency, trauma, syphilis, DM, HTN, etc.
Legal (Therapeutic / MTP)With proper indication under law
CriminalInduced illegally without lawful indication
13.2 MTP (Medical Termination of Pregnancy) — Indications
  • Medical: active TB, chronic nephritis, CCF, malignant HTN, severe thyroid disorder with cardiac issues, uncontrolled DM, ulcerative colitis, multiple sclerosis, chorea.
  • Surgical: malignancy (breast, cervix, ovary), chronic pyloric stenosis.
  • Obstetric: toxemia, threatened abortion with severe hemorrhage, repeated C/S, uterine fibroids, irreducible prolapse.
  • Psychiatric: schizophrenia, severe depression.
  • Fetal conditions: ectopic pregnancy, dead fetus, hydatidiform mole, placental detachment/disease, acute hydramnios, gross malformation.
  • Humanitarian: pregnancy as result of rape.
  • Social: widow, contraceptive failure.
13.3 Methods of Therapeutic Abortion
  • Dilatation & Curettage (D&C) — early pregnancy under GA
  • Dilatation & Oxytocin infusion
  • Low rupture of membranes
  • Utus paste (contains mercury)
  • Vacuum aspiration
  • Amniotic replacement with hypertonic glucose/saline
  • Prostaglandin preparations (local/oral/IV)
  • Laparotomy
13.4 Methods of Criminal Abortion
  • Abortifacient drugs: Ecbolics (ergots, quinine, strychnine), Emmenagogues (oestrogen, borax), Purgatives (castor oil, senna), Irritants (cantharides, oil of turpentine).
  • General violence: severe abdominal pressure/blows/kicks, heavy exercise (jumping, jolting, riding), cupping, very hot/cold hip bath.
  • Local violence: hot/cold vaginal douches, objects introduced into uterine cavity, insertion of foreign bodies into uterus, syringe aspiration, utus paste.
13.5 Complications of Criminal Abortion
  • Local injuries: excoriation, lacerations/perforations (vagina, cervix, uterine wall).
  • Immediate: shock & hemorrhage, air embolism, reflex vagal inhibition.
  • Delayed: sepsis/septicaemia/pyaemia, toxaemia, peritonitis, tetanus.
  • Remote: jaundice, renal failure, bacterial endocarditis, pneumonia, DIC, DVT → pulmonary embolism.
  • Drug effects: poisonous effects of abortifacients.

13.7 Feticide & Stillbirth
  • Feticide: destruction of fetus before birth by unlawful means.
  • Stillbirth: fetus >28 weeks gestational age that did not breathe or show any sign of life after complete birth. Death occurs during labour. Causes: complicated labour, flooding lungs with liquor amnii, bronchial desquamation. MLI: question of feticide, abortion, infanticide.
  • Live birth: takes a breath or shows any sign of life after birth.
  • Dead birth: died in utero before birth; may show rigor mortis, maceration, or mummification at birth.

14.1 Infanticide

Definition: Killing of a newly born baby within 15 days of birth.

Motives: illegitimate child (widows, unmarried girls, wives with absent husbands), inheritance, poverty, family disharmony, dowry system, family pride, custom.

Modes: Commission (mechanical violence: suffocation, strangulation, drowning, head fracture; poisoning) OR Omission (failure to ligate cord, provide nutrition, protect from extremes of weather).

14.2 Causes of Infant Death
Natural
  • Prematurity, asphyxia, congenital malformation
  • Haemolytic disease (Rh incompatibility)
  • Neonatal infection, SIDS
  • Early placental separation, pre-eclampsia
Unnatural
  • During birth: prolonged labour, cord prolapse/knot, injuries, maternal death
  • After birth: suffocation, precipitate labour
  • Criminal: infanticide

14.4 Hydrostatic Test

Based on decreased specific gravity of lungs after air distension during breathing. Undistended lungs = heavier than water (sink); distended = lighter than water (float). Positive = respiration occurred after birth.

Fallacies of Hydrostatic Test
Emphysema from putrefactive gases, artificial respiration, consolidation, edema, congenital tumour, asphyxia neonatorum, partial breathing, stillbirth with decomposition.
14.5 Precipitate Labour

Sudden, rapid delivery especially in multipara women with roomy pelvis without mother's knowledge. Types: True and Pseudo/False. MLI: infanticide, injury to fetus.

Complications: suffocation (fall into lavatory pan), head injury/skull fracture, hemorrhage (torn cord end), drowning.

14.6 Viable Age Features (7th Month)
  • Length: 13–16 inches | Weight: 2–4 lbs
  • Skin: raw-looking with thick vernix caseosa
  • Eyelids: non-adherent; eyelashes present; membrana pupillaris avascular (atrophying)
  • Nails: well-developed but don't reach finger tips
  • Caecum in right iliac fossa; meconium through large bowel
  • Testes may be at internal inguinal ring
  • Cerebral convolutions forming
  • Ossification centers: talus, 2nd & 3rd segments of sternum
14.7 SIDS (Cot/Crib Death)

Sudden infant death unexpected by history; thorough autopsy fails to reveal adequate cause of death.

  • Age: 2 weeks – 2 years; peak 2nd–3rd month
  • Slight male preponderance
  • More common in winter; low socioeconomic conditions; single/young/poor parents
  • Suspected factors: hypersensitivity to cow's milk, infection, prematurity
14.8 Battered Baby Syndrome (Caffey Syndrome)

Multiple injuries not accidental in nature; inflicted by parents/foster parents.

  • Age: <1 year; male preponderance; eldest or youngest; unwanted/illegitimate child
  • Parents: younger, isolated, low education/economic status, family disharmony, criminal record, unemployed
  • Precipitants: crying, refusal to be quiet, soiling
  • Injuries: soft tissue injuries, abrasions/bruises/lacerations of different ages; head/neck/face injuries
  • History: discrepancies; delay in seeking medical attention
14.9 Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy (MBPS)

Rare form of child abuse — exaggeration or fabrication of illness by a primary caregiver (usually mother); child brought repeatedly for induced/alleged symptoms. Frequency: weeks to 21 years of age.

Diagnostic Criteria
  • Illness produced, alleged, or both by a parent.
  • Repeated medical care → multiple procedures.
  • Parental denial of cause of symptoms.
  • Symptoms resolve when child separated from parent.

Methods: adding blood to urine, closing child's nose and mouth, using pillow/towel to obstruct airway, giving insulin, inducing vomiting/diarrhea.

Motive: need for attention/sympathy from medical professionals; satisfaction from deceiving authority figures.
