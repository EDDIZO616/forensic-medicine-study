Unit 01

Injuries & Wounds

1.1

Definitions

Definition — Wound

A disruption of the continuity of tissues produced by external mechanical force. Legally, a wound requires breach of the full thickness of the skin (both epidermis and dermis), or a break of the inner lining of the lip. Bruises and abrasions are therefore NOT wounds by this definition. Neither are internal injuries such as fractures or contusions of the brain.

Definition — Injury

Used synonymously with wound but is wider in scope — includes damage from heat, cold, chemicals, electricity, and radiation. Defined as "a hurt or damage to the body."

Definition — Lesion

Originally meant an injury; now broader — any area of injury, disease, or local degeneration causing a change in tissue function or structure (Latin: laesio = a hurt).

1.2

Classification of Injuries

By Causative Factor

1. Mechanical / Physical
Abrasion, bruise, laceration, incised wound, stab wound, firearm wound, fracture/dislocation.

Blunt force → abrasion, contusion, laceration, fracture
Sharp force → incised & stab wounds
2. Thermal
Heat: general (heat cramps, heat stroke) or local (burns, scalds).
Cold: general (hypothermia) or local (frostbite, trench foot).
3. Chemical
Irritation (weak acids/alkalis) or Corrosion (strong acids/alkalis).
4. Miscellaneous
Electrical, Radiation (X-ray, UV, radioactive), Lightning, Blast injuries. (Categories 2–4 = non-kinetic injuries.)

By Other Criteria

BasisCategories
Severity (Legal)Simple · Grievous
Nature (Medico-legal)Suicidal · Homicidal · Accidental · Defense wounds · Fabricated / self-inflicted
Time of inflictionAntemortem (recent / old) · Postmortem
Key Principle

For any given amount of force: the greater the area over which it is delivered, the less severe the wound — this applies to both blunt and sharp trauma.

1.3

Abrasions

Definition

Injury involving only the outer layers of the skin — does not penetrate the full thickness of the epidermis. Clinically minor but forensically valuable: unlike a bruise, it always indicates the precise point of impact.

Causative agents: Blow, fall, fingernail, thorn, tooth bite.

Types of Abrasions

#TypeMechanismForensic Notes
i Scratch / Linear Sharp/pointed object moving across skin (fingernails, thorns, pins). Surface epidermis collects in front → clean start, tags at end. Fingernail abrasions seen in throttling, sexual assault, child abuse.
ii Graze / Sliding Horizontal friction between skin and hard rough surface. Parallel longitudinal lines; epidermis heaped at opposite end shows direction of force. Most common type. Glass, gravel, dirt may be embedded. Seen in RTAs.
iii Pressure / Crushing Direct impact or linear pressure of rough object; slight inward movement crushes superficial cuticle. E.g., noose or ligature marks in hanging/strangulation.
iv Imprint / Patterned Force applied perpendicular to skin; cuticle crushed bearing the object's imprint. Slightly depressed. Becomes brownish and parchmentized on drying. Radiator grill, tyre thread, teeth bite marks. UV light can reveal hidden patterns.

Circumstances of Abrasions

  • Usually seen in accidents and assaults.
  • Abrasions on face/body of an assailant → evidence of struggle.
  • Heart attack collapse: abrasions on forehead/nose/cheek, but no injuries on upper limbs (unconscious, cannot protect).
  • Conscious fall: abrasions on palmar surface of hands (person protects themselves).
  • Alcoholics: tend to fall backward → occiput injuries.
  • Hysterical women: may fabricate abrasions over accessible areas (front of forearm, face) to allege assault.
1.4

Bruises / Contusions

Definition

Haemorrhage into the skin, subcutaneous tissues, or deeper tissues due to rupture of small vessels from blunt force, resulting in a purplish mark on the skin. Blood infiltrates surrounding tissues under the pumping action of the heart. When blood collects in a mass beneath skin → haematoma.

Classification by Location

i. Intradermal
In the immediate subepidermal layer. Made by impact with patterned object. Haemorrhage is sharply defined.
ii. Subcutaneous
Situated in subcutaneous fatty layer. Edges blurred. Most common type from blunt object. Appears soon after as dark red swelling.
iii. Deep Bruise
Deeper than subcutaneous tissue. Takes hours to 1–2 days to surface (delayed bruising). Re-examine at 24–48 h. Infrared photography may help.

Dating / Age of Bruise

Important Caveat

Reliable microscopic dating is NOT possible. Colour changes are NOT always reliable indicators of age. Only state whether the bruise is recent or old.

Healing process: Extravasation → inflammatory reaction → vasodilation → macrophages break down haemoglobin to biliverdin → enzyme biliverdin reductase converts to bilirubin (yellow). Iron released → combines with ferritin → haemosiderin. Colour change starts at the periphery and extends inwards.

Methods used to date bruise: Histology (postmortem only) · Colour changes (visual) · Calorimetry · Spectrophotometry.

1.5

Lacerations

Definition

Tearing or splitting of skin, mucous membranes, muscles, or internal organs caused by a shearing or crushing force (blunt force applied to a broad area). Extensive bruising + laceration of deeper tissue = crush injury.

Types

TypeMechanismSites / Notes
SplitSoft tissue sandwiched between hard underlying structure and force. E.g. scalp between skull and hard object.Scalp, forehead, eyebrow, zygoma, iliac crest, perineum, shin
StretchHeavy forceful frictional impact — localised "pressure with pull." Flapping of skin indicates direction of force.Run over by vehicle, kicking, compound fractures
Avulsion / GrindingShearing force at oblique/tangential angle tears off skin (flaying). Amputation and decapitation are extreme forms.RTAs — rotating wheel tears skin off legs
TearsTearing from localised impact against hard, irregular objects.Car door handle, broken glass
Cut lacerationHeavy sharp-edged weapon causing deep wide cut.Deep tissue

Characteristics of Laceration

  • Margins: Ragged, irregular, uneven; "shallow tails"; tissue tags between edges.
  • Site: Most common over bony prominences where skin is fixed and easily torn.
  • Bruising and abrasion: Seen around margins.
  • Depth: Shows bridges of irregularly torn fibrous tissue, blood vessels, nerves.
  • Soiling: Mud, glass, paint, hair, fibres — great medico-legal importance.
  • Hair bulbs: Crushed.
  • Haemorrhage: Less than incised wounds (arteries crushed, retract, clot readily — except scalp where temporal arteries bleed freely as they cannot contract).
  • Bevelling: Tangential blow → undermining on one side, abrasion + bevelling on other → indicates direction of blow.
  • Healing: Produces permanent scar. Antemortem lacerations show bruising of margins, vital reaction, eversion, gaping.

Dating of Laceration

12–24 hMargins swollen, red, covered by dried blood clots and lymph
3–5 daysMargins strongly adherent, covered by dried crust
6–7 daysCrust/scab falls off; soft reddish tender scar

1.6

Incised Wounds

Definition

Caused by sharp cutting instruments (knives, razors, glass shards, sharp tin edges) in slashing movements. Criminal circumstances imply intent. Suicidal incised wounds are often multiple, parallel, tentative, superficial, on front of forearm/wrist or front/sides of neck.

Characteristics

FeatureDetails
MarginsClean cut, well-defined, usually everted (inverted only where thin muscle is adherent to skin, e.g. scrotum)
WidthGreater than edge of weapon — due to retraction of divided tissues
LengthGreater than width and depth; no relation to cutting edge; can extend any distance
ShapeSpindle-shaped (greater retraction at centre); gaping more if Langer's lines cut transversely
DepthUsually deeper at commencement ("head of wound") — suicidal: may have hesitation cuts at beginning. Progressively shallow towards end ("tailing").
HaemorrhageMore — vessels cut cleanly and bleed freely
Bevelled cutsOblique blade entry → visible tissue at one margin, undermining at other. Near-horizontal → flap wound.
1.7

Stab Wounds

Definition

Produced when force is delivered along the long axis of a narrow/pointed object (knife, dagger, chisel, sword, sickle). Depth is greater than length and width — key distinguishing feature.

Types

Penetrating Wounds

  • Weapon enters body cavity or viscus
  • Does not exit

Perforating (Through & Through) Wounds

  • Weapon enters one side, exits other
  • Entry: larger, inverted edges
  • Exit: smaller, everted edges

Characteristics

FeatureDetails
MarginsClean, inverted; everted in fatty areas (abdomen, gluteal); no abrasion/bruise unless full penetration; may be abraded/ragged if cutting edge is blunt
LengthSlightly less than width of weapon (skin stretches)
WidthGaping wounds brought together → estimate maximum possible knife width
DepthGreater than external injury dimensions; usually ≤ blade length; may be greater on yielding surfaces (anterior abdominal wall)

Factors Affecting Depth

  • Sharpness of tip, thickness of knife
  • Tissue/organ resistance
  • Clothing
  • Speed of thrust
  • Skin tension (stretched vs lax)

Context (Medicolegal)

SuicidalHomicidalAccidental
Accessible body parts; direction upwards, backward, to the right; variable depthMost stab wounds are homicidal — especially inaccessible areasRare

Complications

  • Marked internal haemorrhage or visceral injury
  • Foreign material infection
  • Air embolism (neck stab → jugular vein → negative pressure sucks air)
  • Pneumothorax
  • Asphyxia (inhalation of blood)
1.8

Firearm Wounds (Ballistic Trauma / GSW)

Ballistics

Internal Ballistics
Path of bullet inside the gun barrel (ignition → propellant combustion → barrel length). Longer barrels → higher velocity, more stability.
External Ballistics
Bullet path from muzzle to target. Rifling → gyroscopic stability. Air drag decelerates; gravity causes bullet drop. Ricochet = deviation off intermediate object → deformity, loss of velocity & stability.
Wound Ballistics
Bullet path inside human body; energy transfer → tissue damage. KE = ½mv². Velocity more important than mass for wounding power. ~50 m/s to penetrate skin; ~65 m/s to fracture bone.

Types of Bullet Injury

  • Surface wound: Entry and exit wounds — most informative. Exit wound may be absent if bullet lodges inside.
  • Permanent cavity: Direct crushing of tissues by bullet track. Expanding (hollow point) bullets create larger cavity. Fragmented bullets → multiple cavities.
  • Temporary cavity: Radial shock wave from kinetic energy transfer → damages tissue not directly crushed.

Shotgun Injury Classification by Range

TypeRangeCharacteristicsMortality
Type I<5 mPellets strike as single mass; massive KE transfer; tissue avulsion85–90%
Type II5–12 mSignificant pellet dispersal & energy loss; may penetrate deep fascia; fractures rare; ocular injuries; Lead pellet embolisation15–20%
Type III>12 mUsually only skin penetrated0–5%

Entry Wound vs Exit Wound

Entry Wound

  • Abrasion collar / contusion ring (always present)
  • Grease collar
  • Possible burning, singeing, blackening, tattooing (at close range)
  • Cherry red tissues (CO at contact range)
  • Wads in wound (shotgun)
  • Circular with abrasion rim (most common)

Exit Wound

  • No burning, blackening, tattooing, abrasion/contusion collar
  • Edges everted, torn
  • Contused haemorrhagic subcutaneous fat protrudes
  • Shapes: stellate, circular, cruciate, elliptical, crescent/star (scalp)
  • May be larger/irregular if bullet tumbles, deforms, or fragments

Rifle Entry Wound by Range

Mnemonic — Suicidal Firearm (5 S's)
  • Site — Classical: temple, heart, mouth, chin
  • Single shot
  • Scene — Calm, locked inside, no disturbance
  • Suicidal note
  • Spasm — Cadaveric spasm (hallmark finding)
Shored Exit Wound

When a firm object (belt, waistband, brassiere, collar) presses against the skin at the exit site, or when the body leans against a hard surface — the exit wound becomes circular with an abrasion margin, mimicking an entry wound. Context and trajectory analysis differentiate.

Unit 02

Medico-Legal Autopsy

2.1

Definitions & Types of Autopsy

Forensic Pathology

Investigation of sudden, unexpected, and/or violent deaths — determining cause of death and circumstances of how death occurred.

Autopsy

Systematic examination of a dead person for medical, legal, and/or scientific purposes.

TypePurposeConsent / Authority
AcademicDissection by anatomy studentsGovernment authority; unclaimed body
Pathological / ClinicalDiagnose cause of death or confirm a diagnosisConsent from next of kin required; physicians cannot order without consent
Medico-legal / ForensicEstablish cause and manner of death in sudden, suspicious, unnatural, litigious, or criminal deathsOfficial order from police or Magistrate — NO next-of-kin consent needed
AnatomicalVoluntarily donated body for dissectionConsent from legal heirs prior to dissection
2.2

Objectives of Medico-Legal Autopsy

The autopsy answers: Who, When, Where, Why, How, and What.

  1. Identify the deceased (decomposed, burnt, mutilated, or unidentified body)
  2. Determine cause of death (natural or unnatural) and interpret significance of disease
  3. Approximate time of death, mode of death, age of injuries, and place of death
  4. Determine manner of death (accidental, suicidal, or homicidal)
  5. Identify poison or weapon responsible in homicide
  6. Assess volitional activity possible after trauma and survival time
  7. Document extent of external and internal injuries
  8. Determine whether injury was likely to cause death in ordinary course of nature
  9. Determine whether treatment was received before death
  10. In homicide: number of persons involved; trace evidence; other related offences; multiple methods/weapons; whether body was displaced; relative positions of victim and assailant
  11. In newborns: live birth and viability
  12. In mutilated/skeletal remains: whether human; how many persons; probable cause and time since death
2.3

Consent & Authority

Key Rule

Medico-legal autopsy requires no consent from relatives — only a legal requisition (authorisation from police or Magistrate) is required.

2.4

Procedure for Medico-Legal Autopsy

  1. Scene visit: If feasible, valuable in murder, poisoning, traffic accidents, firearm injuries, and sexual offences. Can exclude crime in favour of accident, suicide, or natural causes.
  2. Authorisation: Only conducted on official order from police or Magistrate.
  3. Personnel: All registered medical practitioners in government service. Two doctors for suspicious death of a female within 7 years of marriage (burns or other). Board of doctors for custodial deaths, on-table deaths, and second autopsy — odd number for majority decisions.
  4. Privacy: No unauthorized person present.
  5. Location & timing: Must be in an authorized centre. Body should NOT be embalmed before autopsy. Should be performed in daylight (colour changes, bruises, jaundice, PM staining cannot be appreciated in artificial light). Not after 5 PM unless emergency or District Magistrate instruction.
  6. Decomposed bodies: Still perform autopsy — important lesions may survive. Forensic entomologist can assist with time of death.
  7. Documentation: Read inquest report and requisition before starting. Sign all papers. Remember: inquest report may be misleading. Both positive and negative findings must be recorded. Nothing erased — all alterations initialled.
  8. Night receipt: Body preserved at 4°C. Preliminary external examination done at night (temperature, PM staining, rigor mortis). Full autopsy next day.
  9. Identification: A police officer/authorized person and two relatives must identify the body. Names recorded. For unidentified bodies: identification marks, race, religion, sex, age, dental formula, photographs, fingerprints.
  10. Chain of evidence: Essential. Absolute control of specimens removed. All articles (clothes, jewellery, bullets) listed, labelled, sealed, mentioned in report, and handed to police constable on receipt.
  11. Restoration: Body stitched, washed, restored to best cosmetic appearance, then handed to IO.
2.5

External Examination

Observation and documentation of all external characteristics of the decedent.

Clothing

  • Number, labels, laundry marks, design, stains, tears, loss of buttons, cuts, holes, blackening from firearm discharge (with dimensions)
  • Trace evidence: hair, fibres, paint chips, glass, vegetation, insects → collected, labelled, preserved
  • Pockets: medication, drugs of abuse, papers for identification and medical history
  • Remove carefully without tearing. If can't be removed intact, cut away from bullet/cut areas, along seams
  • After autopsy: wet clothing air-dried, packed and sealed in paper bags, handed to police

Documentation of External Injuries

Each injury characterised by: type/nature · size (L×B×D) · shape · site (two external anatomical landmarks) · direction of force · margins/edges/base · distance from heel · time of infliction (inflammatory and colour changes) · vital reaction · foreign materials.

Radiology Indications

Identification/dentistry · Mutilated/charred remains · Gunshot wounds · Air embolism · Sharp force wounds · Barotrauma · Explosives · Child abuse · Decomposed body

2.6

Internal Examination (Evisceration)

All three major body cavities — skull, thorax, abdomen — are opened and examined as routine.

Order of Opening

  • Suspected head injury: skull first, then thorax and abdomen
  • Suspected asphyxial deaths (neck compression): skull and abdomen first, then neck (allows blood drainage for a cleaner dissection)
  • All other cases: thorax and abdomen first, then skull

Skin Incisions

TypeDescriptionWhen Used
I-shapedChin straight down to symphysis pubis (avoiding umbilicus)Most common
Y-shapedMidline from xiphisternum to pubis; forks to chest towards acromion processesStandard alternative
Modified YMidline from suprasternal notch to symphysis; extends over clavicle and up behind earsDetailed neck organ study (hanging, strangulation)
T / "bucket handle"Transverse from acromion to acromion + midline to pubis → calyx/U-shapeSpecific anatomical requirements

Evisceration Methods

En Masse (Letulle)
Most organs removed in one sweep. Rapid evisceration but longer dissection. Leaves all attachments intact.
Virchow's Method
Individual organs removed one by one with immediate dissection. Useful for single-organ pathology.
En Bloc (Ghon)
Compromise between the above two. Most widely used in UK. Relatively quick, preserves inter-organ relationships.
In Situ (Rokitansky)
Organs dissected in place with minimal evisceration. For highly transmissible diseases.

Organ Description

  • Size: Measuring tape; tense capsule → enlargement; loose capsule → shrinkage
  • Shape: Any deviation from normal
  • Surface: Normally delicate, smooth, glistening, transparent serosa — note thickening, roughening, dullness
  • Consistency: Softness/firmness by finger pressure
  • Cohesion: Resistance of cut surface to tearing/pressure/pulling
  • Cut surface: Colour and structural details; hollow structures cut open; solid organs "bread-loafed"
2.7

Report Writing

Report Guidelines
  • Written in duplicate/triplicate on the same day
  • Computerised copy can be shared with police / website
  • In poisoning: opinion reserved until Chemical Examiner's report received; viscera preserved
  • If laboratory tests needed: provisional report first, then supplementary
  • Both positive AND negative findings recorded
  • If cause of death cannot be ascertained: "undetermined" (cause) / "unknown" (manner)
Critical — Opinion Quality

The word "probably" should be avoided. Cause and manner of death are opinions, not facts. A poor opinion is far worse than no opinion — it misleads rather than informs. Opinions can legitimately differ between surgeons.

Unit 03

Postmortem Artefacts

3.1

Definition & Classification

Definition — Artefact

From Latin: arte (skill) + fact (something made). A change or feature that was NOT present in the body (physiologically or pathologically) before death, but was caused or introduced into the body after death — leading to confusion about its nature and cause, and resulting in misinterpretation of medicolegally significant findings.

Classification by Causative Agent

i. Due to Postmortem Changes
Arising naturally from the process of decomposition and other postmortem phenomena.
ii. Third Party Artefacts
Introduced by animals, insects, humans, or other external agents after death.
iii. Environmental Artefacts
Caused by the environment (heat, cold, submersion, soil, chemicals).

Classification by Time of Introduction

Period: Death → AutopsyPeriod: During Autopsy
1. Agonal artefacts1. Postmortem fractures
2. Resuscitation artefacts2. Opening of skull cap
3. Embalming artefacts3. Examination of meninges and brain
4. Exhumation artefacts4. Dissection artefacts
5. Handling of body artefacts5. Preservation artefacts
6. Rigor mortis related6. Miscellaneous
7. Postmortem lividity related
8. Decomposition artefacts
9. Third party artefacts (animal & insect activity)

Agonal Artefact

"Agonal" = the struggle that precedes death. The most common agonal artefact is regurgitation and aspiration.

Antemortem Agonal Reaction

  • Stomach contents aspirated into lower respiratory tract
  • Mixed with fluid and mucus → choking → death

Postmortem Agonal Artefact

  • Never reaches lower respiratory tract
  • Only passive pooling

Neurogenic shock / cardiac arrest before death may prevent any significant bleeding.

Embalming = chemical treatment of dead body to protect from decay.

Key Rule

The body should never be embalmed before autopsy — histopathological examination becomes impossible, and toxicological analysis is compromised.

3.4

Exhumation (Internment & Exhumation Artefacts)

Fungal Growth

  • Fungus on injuries and natural orifices
  • May obscure or simulate antemortem injuries

Digging-Related

  • Digging process itself may produce fractures of bones
  • May be mistaken for perimortem injuries

Undertaker's Fracture

A postmortem fracture — occurs due to careless handling of the dead body by undertakers (or anyone handling the body). Pathologists extending the head for neck structure removal can also cause C6–C7 fractures. May mimic antemortem cervical injury.

3.6

Artefacts Due to Postmortem Changes

Rigor Mortis Related

  • Breaking rigor mortis to move the body → asymmetrical rigor mortis → misinterpretation of Time Since Death
  • Heat stiffening, cold stiffening, and gas stiffening can all simulate rigor mortis
FeatureRigor MortisHeat StiffeningCold Stiffening
CauseNormal postmortem processSurrounding temperature >65°CExposure to freezing temperatures
DurationPasses off in ~36 hoursRemains until muscles/ligaments soften by decompositionUntil body thaws in warm water
IntensityNormalMuch more marked than rigor mortisFirm (body fluid freezing + subcutaneous fat solidification)
Gas Stiffening vs Rigor Mortis — Differentiating Features
  • Putrefactive changes: inflation of tissue spaces by gases (not seen in heat or cold stiffening)
  • No particular order of appearance or disappearance (unlike rigor mortis)
  • Joints are not tight → can be moved easily
  • No muscle shortening (as in heat stiffening)
  • Stiffening disappears by incisions over the affected areas

Hypostasis (Postmortem Lividity) Related Artefacts

  • Localised hypostasis may be mistaken as bruise
  • Hypostasis in dependent heart → resembles myocardial infarction
  • Hypostasis in dependent lung → resembles pneumonic or broncho-pneumonic consolidation
  • Hypostasis in dependent intestine → suggests corrosion or strangulation
  • Hypostasis in dependent stomach → looks like poisoning
  • Hypostasis in nails → sometimes confused with cyanosis

Postmortem vs Antemortem Clot

Antemortem Clot

  • Cannot be wiped off
  • Has fibrin content
  • Firmly adherent

Postmortem Clot

  • Can be wiped off easily
  • No fibrin
  • Jelly-like consistency

Special Decomposition Processes

Mummification
Dehydration of tissues in dry, hot conditions. Body becomes hard and leathery. May obscure injuries or cause skin to contract — simulating ligature marks.
Saponification (Adipocere)
Conversion of body fat to a soap-like substance in humid/aquatic environments. Body found submerged. Preserves body form but alters tissue characteristics.

Pugilistic Attitude

In severe burns, the body assumes a characteristic boxer's stance — arms flexed, fists clenched, legs bent — due to coagulation and shortening of muscle proteins. This is a postmortem heat artefact and must NOT be mistaken for a defensive posture or evidence of a struggle.

Kennedy Phenomenon Surgical cleaning/debridement of gunshot wounds removes gunpowder, changes overall character of wound (as in JF Kennedy murder case). Surgical suturing or alteration makes medico-legal evaluation of firearm injury difficult. Range of fire, type of weapon may become impossible to determine

Criminal artefacts (general) Dismemberment and mutilation of body parts after death; alteration of antemortem injuries to cause confusion to the autopsy surgeon Obstructs identification and manner-of-death determination

Autopsy Surgeon Tips
  • When removing neck organs: care not to fracture thyroid cartilage or hyoid bone
  • In elderly/debilitated persons: unusual fragility may cause postmortem fractures, especially ribs
  • Postmortem fractures lack vital reaction (no haemorrhage, no marrow extrusion, no periosteal reaction) — histology distinguishes antemortem from postmortem fracture
3.12

Medico-Legal Importance of Artefacts

Consequences of Misidentifying Artefacts
  • Wrong conclusion as to cause of death
  • Wrong conclusion as to mode of death
  • Wrong conclusion as to nature of death
  • Wrong determination of whether a wound was antemortem or postmortem
  • Unjustified suspicion of foul play
  • Misguided direction to the investigating officer
  • Miscarriage of justice — imprisonment of innocent persons
Take Home Message

Interpretation of findings over a dead body depends entirely upon the ability, dedication, knowledge, and wisdom of the autopsy surgeon. Artefacts, if misidentified, may ultimately lead to the imprisonment of an innocent person. Recognition of artefacts is not optional — it is the foundation of just forensic practice.
