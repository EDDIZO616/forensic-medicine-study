UNIT I Examination of Trace Evidence
1.1 — Blood: Overview & Detection
Key Concept

Blood is the most common body fluid detected at violent crime scenes. Detection proceeds through three stages: visualization → presumptive testing → confirmatory testing.

Visualization Methods
  1. Naked eye / White light: Standard examination of clothing. Insufficient on dark backgrounds.
  2. Alternate Light Source (Polilight): Blood has absorption maximum at 415 nm. Does not fluoresce but absorbs visible light.
  3. Infrared (IR) Illumination: Wavelength 800–1000 nm with an IR-sensitive camera. Used when blood is on dark fabrics.
  4. High-intensity white light: Should be the first choice at any crime scene — diluted blood leaves brownish stains, flows into cracks, carpet padding, and baseboards.
🔬 Presumptive vs. Confirmatory

Presumptive tests — based on peroxidase activity of hemoglobin (Hb). Include Luminol, Fluorescein, Kastle-Meyer, Benzidine, TMB, LMG.

Confirmatory tests — immunological. Include Takayama crystal test, RSID-Blood, ABAcard HemaTrace.

1.2 — Luminol & Fluorescein Tests
Luminol Test
  • Chemiluminescence when oxidized by Hb
  • Detects latent/cleaned-up bloodstains
  • Requires a dark room to observe
  • Water-based — can smear impressions
  • Can cause DNA degradation
  • Should be the last resort
Fluorescein Test
  • Oxidation of fluorescein in presence of Hb + H₂O₂
  • Requires light at 425–485 nm
  • Viewed in dark with yellow filter goggles
  • Less commonly used than luminol
⚠️ Problems with Luminol (4 Key Points)
  1. Only a presumptive test — if stain is only visible by luminol, no further analysis is possible.
  2. False positives: copper ions, iron compounds, cobalt ions, potassium permanganate, bleach, plant peroxidases.
  3. Causes loss of several genetic markers.
  4. Water base smears latent impressions and further dilutes stains past DNA detection limits.
1.3 — Kastle-Meyer (Phenolphthalein) Test
Principle

Peroxidase-like activity of Hb catalyzes the oxidation of colorless reduced phenolphthalein into bright pink phenolphthalein.

Materials Required
  • Kastle-Meyer solution (reduced phenolphthalein)
  • 70% ethanol, distilled water, 3% hydrogen peroxide
  • Cotton swabs, dropper/pipette
Procedure (Steps)
1
Moisten swab with water → touch to dried blood sample (small amount only).
2
Add 1–2 drops of 70% ethanol (exposes Hb for increased sensitivity; alcohol does not react).
3
Add 1–2 drops of Kastle-Meyer solution. Should be colorless or pale yellow. If pink → solution is old/oxidized; discard and restart.
4
Add 1–2 drops of 3% H₂O₂. Immediate pink = positive for blood. (Delayed pink after ~30 sec = H₂O₂ oxidizing phenolphthalein — not a true positive.)
⚠️ Limitations
  • Sensitivity: detects dilutions as low as 1:10
  • Negative result = reliable absence of heme
  • False positives: oxidizing agents (peroxidases in cauliflower, broccoli)
  • Does NOT differentiate species — cannot tell human vs. animal blood
  • Separate species-origin test required (ABO typing, Hematrace cards)
1.4 — Takayama Confirmatory Test (Hemochromogen)
Principle

NaOH releases heme from globin (alkaline hydrolysis) → glucose reduces heme iron → pyridine combines to form pyridine ferroprotoporphyrin — insoluble pink, needle/rhomboid-shaped crystals.

Reagents
  • Saturated aqueous glucose (100 mL)
  • 10% aqueous NaOH (100 mL)
  • Takayama reagent: 10 mL glucose + 10 mL NaOH + 10 mL pyridine + 20 mL deionized water
  • Store refrigerated in dark bottle
Equipment
  • Hotplate
  • Compound microscope
  • Glass slides & coverslips
  • Disposable pipets
Procedure
1
Place small portion of suspected blood on microscope slide.
2
Add a drop of Takayama reagent; cover with coverslip.
3
Warm slide on hotplate — a characteristic reddish color appears around the sample.
4
Observe microscopically for pink needle or rhomboid-shaped crystals.
Interpretation

Positive = pink needle/rhomboid crystals formed → confirms presence of blood. Absence of crystals does not confirm absence of blood. Must test against positive control (bloodstain) before each use.

1.5 — RSID-Blood & ABAcard® HemaTrace®
RSID™-Blood (Rapid Stain Identification)
  • Uses two monoclonal antibodies in lateral flow format
  • Detects human glycophorin A — abundantly expressed in red blood cells
  • One antibody conjugated to colloidal gold → migrates to Test line (T)
  • Control line (C) = anti-mouse IgG (internal positive control)
  • RSID Reader: hand-held, touch-screen, CCD imaging; compares pixel density to calibration data from 800+ samples
  • Also available: RSID-Saliva (α-amylase), RSID-Semen (semenogelin), RSID-Urine (Tamm-Horsfall glycoprotein)
ABAcard® HemaTrace® Procedure
  1. Place ~3 mm² cutting or ¼ swab into 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube.
  2. Add 300 µL extraction buffer → vortex briefly.
  3. Incubate 1–5 min at room temperature.
  4. Remove device from sealed pouch; label appropriately.
  5. Add 150 µL (4 drops) to sample well S.
  6. Read result after 10 minutes.
Reading Results

Two pink lines (T + C) = Positive. One line (C only) = Negative. No line in C = Invalid test. Note: false positives may occur with ferret blood.

1.7 — Blood Spatter Analysis (BSA / BPA)

Blood spatter analysis (bloodstain pattern analysis) is a forensic specialty determining the location of victim/attacker, weapon type, and sequence of events.

Key Principles
  1. Patterns classified as: Passive, Projected, and Transfer.
  2. Shape/size of drops indicates angle of impact and distance fallen.
  3. Direction of travel determined from the shape of the stain (pointed end = direction of travel).
  4. Area of convergence = the 2D origin on a surface; point of origin = 3D source height.
  5. A few stains ≠ a pattern. Always draw conclusions with qualifications.
  6. Stains <1 mm diameter → high velocity (most often gunshot).
  7. Stains ≥1 mm diameter → medium velocity (beating/stabbing).
  8. Blood on ceiling with upward trajectory → almost always a gunshot (often suicide).
1.8 — Spatter Calculations
Angle of Impact Impact Angle = arcsin (Width ÷ Length)
Drop Height (Point of Origin) Height = Distance × tan(angle of impact)
Angle of Impact: Key Rules
  • 90° = perfectly round drop
  • More acute angle → more elongated stain
  • At ~30° → stain begins to produce a tail
  • Do NOT measure the tail when calculating angle
Worked Examples
Example Calculations
  1. Width 9 mm, Length 18 mm: arcsin(9/18) = arcsin(0.5) = 30°
  2. Width 1.73 cm, Length 2 cm: arcsin(1.73/2) ≈ 60°
  3. Angle 27°, Distance 5.75 ft: Height = 5.75 × tan(27°) = 5.75 × 0.5095 ≈ 2.93 ft
  4. Angle 70°, Distance 10 ft: Height = 10 × tan(70°) = 27.5 ft
  5. Angle 80°, Distance 15 ft: Height = 15 × tan(80°) = 85.1 ft
  6. Angle 75°, Distance 10 ft: Height = 10 × tan(75°) = 37.3 ft
1.9 — Categories of Bloodstains
1. Passive Stains

Created by gravity alone. Includes drips, pools, and flows. Circular drops at 90°.

2. Transfer Stains

Wet bloody surface contacts a secondary surface. Can show recognizable pattern (bloody handprint, footwear).

3. Projected Stains (3 subtypes)
  1. Arterial Spurt/Gush: Blood exits under arterial pressure.
  2. Cast-off Stains: Blood thrown from a blood-bearing object in motion (e.g., swinging weapon).
  3. Impact Spatter: Blood source receives a blow — random dispersion. Velocity determines pattern.

1.10 — Saliva
Identification Markers
  • Classical marker: enzyme amylase (high quantities in saliva).
  • Amylase also found in: pancreatic juice, fecal material → cannot be sole indicator if contaminated.
  • Other enzymes: histatins, cystatins, lactoferrins, lysozymes, mucins.
  • Epithelial cells of oral mucosa provide DNA evidence.
Inorganic Components
  • Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺, HCO₃⁻, Mg²⁺, NH₃
Organic Components
  • Urea, uric acid, creatinine
  • Putrescine, cadaverine, lipids
  • 400+ types of protein
  • sIgA, albumin, transferrin
Evidentiary Markers (for forensic identification)
  1. Enzyme amylase (activity test)
  2. Genes: STATHERIN and HISTATIN 3
  3. Total proteins and mineral content
  4. Immunoglobulins
  5. Epithelial cells — DNA, mRNA
  6. DNA cytosine methylation patterns
1.11 — Semen
Composition
  • ~60% from seminal vesicle glands
  • ~30% from prostate gland
  • ~10% from epididymis + bulbourethral glands
  • Vasectomized/oligospermic males can still produce detectable seminal fluid (PSA, semenogelin)
Presumptive Tests for Semen
  1. Visual and Alternate Light Tests: Dried semen is off-white to faint yellow. Under blue/UV light (Wood's Lamp, Crime Scope) → fluoresces blue to yellow due to Flavin and choline-conjugated proteins. Highly presumptive only — many substances fluoresce similarly.
  2. Acid Phosphatase (AP) Test: Prostate secretes high AP. In presence of Alpha-Naphthyl acid phosphate + Brentamine Fast Blue → dark purple color in <1 minute. AP activity detectable in dried samples years later; degrades with moisture/heat within days. Post-coital AP markedly decreases after 24h, diminishes by 48h.
  3. Prostate Specific Antigen (PSA / P30 test / ABAcard): High amounts from male prostate. Also found in trace amounts in fecal material, sweat, female urine, breast milk. Caution when interpreting positive PSA not confirmed by sperm presence.
Confirmatory Tests for Semen
  1. Christmas Tree Stain: Most reliable — positive visual identification of spermatozoa.
    • Picroindigocarmine → sperm neck/tail: green/blue
    • Nuclear Fast Red (Kernechtrot) → sperm heads: red; acrosomal cap: pink
    • Sperm tails degrade first — analyst must distinguish sperm heads from mucosal/epithelial cells (nuclei also stain red)
    • Intact sperm can be recovered from vaginal cavity and surfaces/fabrics if dried quickly
  2. RSID-Semen Strip Test: Detects semenogelin (seminal vesicle-specific antigen). Unique to semen — no cross-reactivity with other fluids or mammalian semen. Works even in unfavorable storage conditions. Similar format to a pregnancy test strip.
1.12 — Vaginal Fluid

Consists of fluid secreted through vaginal walls + cervical mucus + residual urine. Chemical composition changes throughout the menstrual cycle — makes identification difficult.

Detection Method

Periodic Acid Schiff (PAS) reagent — detects glycogenated epithelial cells, staining them magenta. Glycol groups of carbohydrates oxidized by periodic acid → dialdehydes → combine with Schiff's reagent → magenta complex.

Major Limitation

Post-menopausal or pre-pubescent females may lack glycogenated cells → test useless. No reliable method currently available. Older methods (vaginal peptidase, LDH isoenzymes) not widely used.

1.13 — Hair: Structure, Types & Racial Differences
Cuticle (Outermost Layer)
  • Transparent, overlapping scales
  • Scales point from proximal (root) → distal (tip)
  • Human scales: flattened and narrow = imbricate
  • Cat/seal/mink: spinous (petal-like)
  • Rodents/bats: coronal (crown-stacked)
Cortex (Thickest Layer)
  • Contains most pigment granules (melanin)
  • Distribution of pigment varies by race
  • Usually denser near the cuticle in humans
Medulla (Central Canal)
  • Not always present in human hair
  • Classified as: continuous (unbroken), intermittent (regular intervals), fragmented (irregular)
  • Human: thin (<⅓ of hair diameter) or absent
  • Animal: thick (>½ of hair diameter)
  • Medullary index = diameter of medulla ÷ total hair diameter
Hair Life Cycle
Anagen (Active Growth)

~80–90% of all hair. Lasts ~1000 days. Cells around follicle divide rapidly. Best for nuclear DNA extraction.

Catagen (Transition)

~2% of hair. Hair changes; growth slows.

Telogen (Dormant)

10–18%. Follicle dormant — shed hairs. May lack sufficient nDNA; may use mtDNA.

Human vs. Animal Hair Differentiation
Human Hair
  • Pigment denser toward the cuticle
  • Thin/absent medulla (<⅓ diameter)
  • One consistent color along length
  • Imbricate (flattened narrow) scale pattern
Animal Hair
  • Pigment denser toward medulla
  • Ovoid bodies or dense pigment masses
  • Thick medulla (>½ diameter)
  • Banded color patterns possible
Treated Hair Analysis
  • Bleaching: Disturbs cuticle scales, removes pigment → brittle and yellowish. Artificial: sharp demarcation line. Sun-bleached: gradual.
  • Timing of last coloring: Hair grows ~1.3 cm/month. Measure naturally colored section ÷ 1.3 = months since coloring. (e.g., 2.5 cm ÷ 1.3 = ~1.9 months ago)
  • Dyeing: Colors both cuticle and cortex.
Cast Mount Procedure (for cuticle scale examination)
1
Place a drop of latex (or thin layer of nail polish) on clean slide.
2
Tilt second slide over first at ~30° angle, edges touching.
3
Slowly pull tilted slide until it touches the latex drop.
4
Push tilted slide back along first to spread latex into thin film.
5
Place hair strands on film. Allow 10–15 min for latex to harden (nail polish: until tacky-dry).
6
Use forceps to pull hair off. Examine with low-power objective only (do NOT use high-power for scale casts).
7
Note: whether scales surround shaft, general scale shape, and whether exposed edge is smooth or jagged.
1.14 — Hair in Investigation & DNA Profiling
Collection Methods
  • Plucking, shaking, or scraping surfaces
  • Vacuum (large amounts), tape lifts
  • Compared to hair from 6 major body regions of suspect/victim
Microscopic Analysis
  • Initial: low power → examine cuticle. Macroscopic: length, color, curliness.
  • Microscopy: medulla pattern, cortex pigmentation, cuticle scale type. Magnification: 40–400×
  • Comparison microscope: examines two samples simultaneously side by side.
  • Phase contrast + fluorescence microscopy: detects dye/treatments.
  • Electron microscope: highest detail.
Testing Hair Shaft for Substances
  • Hair dissolved → checked for poisons (lead, arsenic) or drugs.
  • Timeline developed by analyzing sections from root = time since exposure.
  • Neutron Activation Analysis (NAA): Identifies up to 14 elements in 2 cm of hair. Hair bombarded with high-energy neutrons → gamma radiation signals for each element. Identifies Sb, Ar, Br, Cu, Au, Mn, Ag, Na, Zn. Probability of two people having same concentration of 9 elements ≈ 1 in 1,000,000
DNA Analysis of Hair
nDNA vs. mtDNA
  • Nuclear DNA (nDNA): Inherited from both parents. Higher discrimination power. Found in anagen roots (actively dividing cells).
  • Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA): Inherited from mother only. Many copies per cell — useful when nDNA is absent. Found in roots and hair shafts of telogen hairs.
  • Telogen hairs without follicular tissue: often lack nDNA → use mtDNA.
  • DNA analysis is destructive — consumes portions of hair.
Critical Sequence: Microscopy BEFORE DNA Analysis
  1. Always perform full microscopical comparison BEFORE DNA analysis (cannot be done afterwards if hair is consumed).
  2. Microscopy and DNA are complementary — fragmentary hairs excluded microscopically may not need DNA.
  3. People of the same maternal line share mtDNA types → microscopy may provide additional discrimination.
Preparing Hair for DNA Analysis
  • Unmounted hair: Place in clean container directly.
  • Temporary mounting medium: Remove hair, clean with solvent, dry, place in container.
  • Semi-permanent mounting medium: Soak in solvent until coverslip can be removed, or rapid-chill (liquid nitrogen/dry ice). Remove hair, rinse, place in container.
  • Always reinspect slide and container to confirm complete transfer.
Biological & Environmental Alteration of Hair
  • Collect known hair standards contemporaneously with questioned hairs.
  • Head hairs most affected by biological fluctuations and environment.
  • A period of months to years can detract from head hair comparisons.
  • Pubic hairs less influenced — several years may not severely impact comparisons.
  • Hair from decomposing bodies: postmortem root band — dark band near root.
UNIT II Sexual Offences
2.1 — Introduction & Classification
Definition

Sexual offence = any sexual act directed against another person without consent, including when the person is unable to consent.

Classification of Sexual Offences
  1. Natural Offences: Committed in order of nature (penile penetration of vagina).
  2. Unnatural Offences: Against the order of nature (no potential for procreation) — sodomy, lesbianism, bestiality, oral coitus.
  3. Non-Forcible Sex Offences: Unlawful but non-forcible — incest, statutory rape.
  4. Sexual Perversions / Paraphilias: Sexual excitement associated with unusual/abnormal imagery or acts.
  5. Other Sex-linked Offences: Indecent assault, sexual harassment.
Important Medico-Legal Principle

Rape is a LEGAL diagnosis — not a medical diagnosis. Medical opinions must never include the word "rape." Opinion should state whether there is evidence of sexual intercourse and whether injuries are consistent with force.

2.2 — Rape: Definition, Elements & Law
Definition (from Latin rapere: to seize by force)

A man commits rape when he does any of the following acts under specified circumstances:

  1. Penetrates his penis into vagina, mouth, urethra, or anus.
  2. Inserts any object or body part (not his penis) into vagina, mouth, urethra, or anus.
  3. Manipulates any part of her body causing penetration into vagina, urethra, or anus.
Circumstances Constituting Rape
  1. Against her will.
  2. Without her consent.
  3. With consent obtained by: fear of death/hurt; impersonation (man pretends to be her husband); unsoundness of mind or intoxication or stupefying substances (unable to understand consequences).
  4. With or without consent — when victim is under 18 years (statutory rape).
  5. When she is unable to communicate consent.
Exceptions
  • Medical intervention/procedure does not constitute rape.
  • Sexual intercourse by a man with his wife (not under 18) is not rape.
Kenyan Law — Sexual Offences Act 2006: Penalties
  1. Rape: Minimum 10 years; may be enhanced to life imprisonment.
  2. Attempted Rape: Minimum 5 years; may be enhanced to life.
  3. Gang Rape: Minimum 15 years; may be enhanced to life.

"A charge of rape is easy to make, hard to prove, harder still to disprove."

2.3 — Medical Examination of Victim
Precautions (Mandatory)
  1. Authorization: Written order from police or court required.
  2. Identification: By relatives, police, or ID marks.
  3. Female nurse: Must be present throughout the entire examination.
  4. No delay: Examination should be done as early as possible.
Objectives of Examination
  • Evidence of recent sexual intercourse
  • Injuries suggesting violent struggle
  • Age of victim (if required)
  • Intoxication or drugging
Consent Rules
  • Age >12 years: witnessed, written, informed consent required.
  • In absence of consent, examination cannot be undertaken.
History to Obtain
  • Menstrual history, marital status, obstetric history
  • History of venereal diseases
  • Resistance offered by victim
  • Whether bath or local washing was done (critical for sample integrity)
Examination of Clothes
  1. Confirm clothes have NOT been changed.
  2. Ask victim to remove them on a large white sheet of paper (to collect trace evidence).
  3. Examine for tears, lost buttons (signs of violence/struggle).
  4. Look for stains: mud, grass, blood, semen — record position, size, character.
  5. Seal, label, and send for Chemical Analysis.
General Body Examination
  • General build, gait, mental state (intoxicated/drugged?)
  • Dental exam and secondary sexual characters (for age)
  • Nails: damage, foreign bodies (suspect's tissue)
  • Stains on body: blood, saliva, semen, mud
  • Injuries: restraint marks, violence marks
Injuries — Key Locations & Significance
Pattern of Injuries
  1. Face/conjunctiva (petechiae): Partial asphyxia — forcible restraint over neck.
  2. Mouth and neck: Prevent calling for help.
  3. Face: Violence to subdue victim.
  4. Wrists/arms, thighs/legs: Holding injuries.
  5. Thighs and buttocks: During intercourse.
  6. Back: From ground friction.
  7. Breasts: Rough handling.
  8. Bite marks: Face, breasts, genitalia.
Examination of Genitalia

Examine: Vulva (labia majora), labia minora, hymen, vagina, perineum. Record injuries, tenderness, inflammation, presence/absence of semen.

2.4 — Specimens to be Collected (Victim)
Biological Specimens
  1. Clothes: Air dry; send intact.
  2. Blood (plain): For grouping.
  3. Blood (anticoagulated): For alcohol/drugs.
  4. Urine: For drugs.
  5. Nail scrapings: Blood/epidermis of accused; DNA.
Hair Specimens
  1. Pubic hair combing: Foreign hairs for analysis.
  2. Matted pubic hair: Cut and send.
  3. 20 pubic hairs cut for comparison.
Vaginal Swabs — Critical Sequence
  1. 1 swab from introitus and perineum
  2. 1 low vaginal swab
  3. 1 high vaginal swab
  4. 1 cervical mucus swab — ONLY if alleged rape occurred ≥24 hours prior

Spermatozoa in the vagina = definite evidence of sexual intercourse.

Special Examination Tools
  • Colposcopy: Detects injuries not visible to naked eye.
  • Toluidine Blue dye: Stains injured areas blue.
  • UV Light: Detects seminal stains (fluorescence).
Stains on Body
  • Dried stains: scrape into envelope.
  • Moist stains: swabs in distilled water.
Complications / Dangers of Rape
  1. Haemorrhage and shock
  2. Death (assault, suffocation, strangulation, intoxicants, suicide)
  3. Mental agony
  4. Rape Trauma Syndrome
  5. Sexually Transmitted Infections
  6. Pregnancy
2.5 — Examination of the Accused
Key Difference from Victim Examination

If the accused refuses consent for examination, reasonable force may be used.

Local Examination (Genitalia)
  • Examine for development and congenital anomalies (rule out impotency)
  • Record all injuries: abrasions, frenulum tears
Demonstrating Vaginal Secretions on Accused
  1. Rub moist filter paper on glans → add Lugol's iodine → blue color = positive for vaginal secretions (rich in glycogen).
  2. Smear from glans + Papanicolaou stain → examine for vaginal epithelial cells.
Smegma Assessment

Comment on presence/absence of smegma (cheesy sebaceous secretion + desquamated epithelial cells on corona glandis). Absence may suggest recent washing.

Samples to Collect from Accused
  1. Clothes and undergarments
  2. Foreign hair/fibers
  3. Fingernail scrapings
  4. Scalp hair
  5. Swab from teeth/bite marks
  6. Combing of pubic hair
  7. Pubic hair clippings
  8. Urethral swabs
  9. Swabs from glans penis
  10. Blood: serology, drugs/intoxicants, DNA profiling
  11. STD testing
2.6 — Seminal Fluid Analysis (Forensic)
Physical Properties of Semen
  • Greyish-yellow, thick, jelly-like, sticky with characteristic odor (fresh)
  • Quantity: 2–5 mL per ejaculation
  • Sperm count: 60–150 million (90% motile)
  • pH: 7.4
Sperm Motility Timeline
ConditionMotility
At room temperature — 3 hoursFull motility
8 hours50% motile
24 hours10% motile
26 hours in vaginaComplete sperm present
Victim deadDestroyed by decomposition
Chemical Tests for Semen
  1. Fluorescent Test: Stain + 10% HCl → glass slide + coverslip + fluorescence solution (KI + iodine + water) → rhombic crystals of chlorine iodide. Used as screening/presumptive test.
  2. Barbario's Test: Stain + glass picric acid → yellow needle-shaped rhombic crystals of spermine picrate (depends on prostatic secretions).
  3. Acid Phosphatase Test: 500–1000× more AP in semen than any other body fluid. 340–360 Bodansky units. Positive reaction up to 36 hours. >1,000 Bodansky units with non-motile sperm = ejaculation within 12 hours.
  4. Creatine Phosphokinase: High concentration in spermatozoa. Normal level: 385–1400 units/mL. >400 units = diagnostic. More stable enzyme than AP.
  5. Immunological Methods: PSA (P30), MHS-5 (seminal vesicles), Mab 4E6 (sperm cells), FISH (fluorescent in-situ hybridization — detects male epithelial cells in vagina for up to 1 week).
Microscopic Examination of Semen
Staining Method
  1. Stained fabric + 1% HCl or 3% acetic acid → incubate 30–60 min.
  2. Rub fabric on slide → air dry → fix with methyl/ethyl alcohol.
  3. Stain with methylene blue + eosin.

Appearance: Posterior ⅓ of head = pink; anterior ⅔ = unstained. Sperm better preserved outside body (clothes) than inside vagina.

Human sperm dimensions: Length 50–55 µm; head 5 µm.

Proof of semen: At least one unbroken sperm; OR electrophoretic LDH isoenzyme detection.

2.7 — Incest

Sexual intercourse by a man with a woman closely related by blood or by marriage (prohibited degrees of relationship): daughter, granddaughter, sister, stepsister, aunt.

Types
  • Consanguinal: Between blood relatives
  • Affinal: Between adoption/marriage relatives
  • Quasi-relative: Foster parent, live-in partner in parental role
Circumstances Predisposing to Incest
  1. Family strife and disorganization
  2. Low socioeconomic status
  3. Overcrowding
  4. Lack of parental supervision
  5. Low morality and delinquency
  6. Alcohol removing natural inhibitions
  7. Cerebral diseases (general paralysis, senile degeneration)
  8. Siblings separated in childhood who meet as strangers
Effects on Victim
  • Behavior problems, delinquency, anxiety, regressive behaviors
  • Nightmares, withdrawal from normal activities
  • Internalizing/externalizing disorders, cruelty and self-injury
  • PTSD, poor self-esteem, age-inappropriate sexual behavior
Kenyan Law — Sexual Offences Act 2006 (Incest)

Any male committing an indecent act or act causing penetration with daughter, granddaughter, sister, mother, niece, aunt, or grandmother → minimum 10 years. If female victim <18 years → life imprisonment. Consent is immaterial if victim <18. Attempted incest → minimum 10 years.

Medico-Legal Aspects
  • May lead to progression of genetic defects from close-relative mating.
  • Valid ground for divorce.
  • Prohibited by religious laws in many countries.
2.8 — Unnatural Sex Offences

Sexual intercourse against the order of nature — not involving penile penetration of a woman's vagina by a man's penis.

UNIT III Pregnancy, Abortion & Infanticide
3.1 — Pregnancy: Definition, Duration & Signs
Key Definitions & Timelines
  • Zygote: Fertilized ovum
  • Embryo: After implantation to 8th week
  • Fetus: After 8th week onward
  • Average duration: 280 days (40 weeks)
  • Maximum duration: 354 days
  • Minimum duration: 180 days
Viability
  • Viability = capability to lead independent life outside mother after birth.
  • Average viable age: 210 days (7 months)
  • Earliest viable age: 180 days (6 months)
Signs of Pregnancy — Three Categories
A. Presumptive Signs (subjective)
  1. Amenorrhoea
  2. Morning sickness
  3. Changes in the breast
  4. Jacquemier's/Chadwick's sign: vaginal mucosa changes from pink to violet to blue (venous obstruction) — after 4th week
  5. Pigmentation of skin, linea nigra, striae gravidarum
  6. Sympathetic disturbances, frequency of micturition, constipation
  7. Altered food choice
  8. Quickening: Multipara 16–18th wk; Primipara 18–20th wk
B. Probable Signs
  1. Progressive enlargement of abdomen; uterine height
  2. Hegar's sign: Softening of cervix — 7–10th week
  3. Palmer's sign: Intermittent uterine contractions — 4–8th week
  4. Goodell's sign: Extreme cervical softening + bluish discoloration — ~4 months
  5. Uterine souffle: soft blowing murmur synchronous with mother's pulse — end of 4th month
  6. Ballotment test
  7. Pregnancy test (biochemical)
  8. Braxton Hick's sign: Intermittent painless uterine contractions; each ~1 min, followed by 2–3 min relaxation
C. Certain / Positive Signs
  1. Fetal movement: after 20th week
  2. Auscultation of fetal heart: 18–20th week; rate 120–160 bpm
  3. Palpation of fetal parts
  4. Imaging: USG from 6th week; Radiology after 3rd month; MRI
Pregnancy Tests
  1. Biological tests: Aschheim-Zondek, Frank, Friedman, Hogben/Xenopus, Galli-Mainini/male frog tests.
  2. Immunological/Serological tests: Agglutination-inhibition, haemagglutination-inhibition, RIA, ELISA.
  3. Imaging: USG (6th week), radiology (after 3rd month), MRI.
Pseudocyesis (False/Phantom Pregnancy)

Woman shows most signs of pregnancy (enlarged abdomen, weight gain, nausea) but is NOT pregnant. Caused by psychic and hormonal disorder. Found mostly in childless women, women nearing menopause, or those with pelvic/abdominal tumors/fatty abdomen.

3.5 — Abortion: Types, Causes & Methods
Definition

Premature expulsion of the fetus from the uterus at any time before full term. Legally: termination of pregnancy before 20th week, with or without expulsion, spontaneous or induced.

Types of Abortion
  1. Spontaneous (Natural/Accidental): Occurs without intervention. Mostly 2nd–3rd month; 15–20% of pregnancies. Causes include chromosomal defects, implantation defects, uterine abnormalities, Rh incompatibility, systemic diseases, hormonal deficiency, trauma, drugs.
  2. Legal / Therapeutic (MTP — Medical Termination of Pregnancy): Indications include maternal medical/surgical/obstetric/psychiatric conditions, fetal abnormalities, rape (humanitarian), social conditions (widow, contraceptive failure).
  3. Criminal Abortion: Unlawfully induced abortion without proper indication.
MTP Indications — Categories
Maternal Indications
  • Respiratory: Active pulmonary tuberculosis
  • Renal: Chronic nephritis, nephrotic syndrome, PCKD
  • Cardiovascular: CCF, malignant HTN, Rh heart disease
  • Endocrine: Severe thyroid disorders, uncontrolled DM
  • GI: Ulcerative colitis
  • Neurological: Chorea, multiple sclerosis
  • Malignancy: Ca breast/cervix/ovary
  • Obstetric: Toxemia, persistent haemorrhage, repeated C/S
  • Psychiatric: Schizophrenia, severe depression
Fetal & Other Indications
  • Ectopic pregnancy, dead fetus
  • Hydatidiform mole
  • Gross malformation of fetus
  • Acute hydramnios
  • Humanitarian: Pregnancy from rape
  • Social: Widow, contraceptive failure
Methods of Therapeutic Abortion
  1. Dilatation and curettage (D&C) — early pregnancy under GA
  2. Dilatation and oxytocin infusion
  3. Low rupture of membrane
  4. Utus paste (containing mercury)
  5. Vacuum aspiration
  6. Amniotic replacement with hypertonic glucose or saline
  7. Prostaglandin preparations (local/oral/IV)
  8. Laparotomy
Methods of Criminal Abortion
A. Abortifacient Drugs
  1. Ecbolics (directly contract uterus): ergots, quinine, strychnine
  2. Emmenagogues (initiate menstrual flow): oestrogen, borax
  3. Purgatives: jalap, castor oil, senna
  4. Irritants: cantharides, oil of turpentine
B & C. Physical Violence Methods
  • General violence: Abdominal pressure, blows, kicks, tight banding, heavy exercise (jumping, jolting, riding), cupping, extreme hip baths (hot/cold)
  • Local violence: Hot/cold vaginal douches, objects inserted into uterine cavity, FB insertion, syringe aspiration, introduction of utus paste into uterus

3.7 — Feticide, Still-born, Live-born & Dead-born
Key Definitions
  • Feticide: Destruction of fetus at any time before birth by unlawful means.
  • Still-born: Fetus ≥28 weeks that did not breathe or show any sign of life after complete birth. Death occurs during labour.
  • Live-born: Child takes breath or shows any sign of life after birth.
  • Dead-born: Child died in utero before birth — may show rigor mortis, maceration, or mummification at birth.
Signs of Live Birth (at Autopsy)
  1. Degree of maturity
  2. Chest shape (arched/drum-shaped; circumference > abdomen; intercostal spaces wider)
  3. Diaphragm at level of 6th–7th rib (vs 4th–5th rib in still-born)
  4. Lungs (see table below)
  5. Changes in stomach and intestinal content (air swallowing)
  6. Middle ear changes
  7. Skin changes, caput succedaneum changes
  8. Umbilical cord changes
  9. Circulation changes

Hydrostatic Test (Lung Float Test)

Principle: Fetal lungs (unexpanded) are heavier than water and sink. After breathing, air expands alveoli → specific gravity decreases → lungs float. Positive test = proof that respiration occurred after birth.

Fallacies of Hydrostatic Test
  • False positive: Emphysema from putrefactive gases, artificial respiration
  • False negative: Consolidation, edema, congenital tumor, asphyxia neonatorum, partial breathing

  • Undescended testes
  • Eyes more closed
  • Less developed nails
Post-mature Baby (>295 days)
  • Oversize (>20 inches)
  • Long hair (>7 inches)
  • Overweight
  • Nails prominent beyond fingers
  • Radiology: ossification in upper tibia and cuboid
3.8 — Infanticide
Definition

Killing of a newly born baby within 15 days of birth.

Causes of Infant Death — Natural
  1. Prematurity
  2. Asphyxia
  3. Congenital malformation
  4. Haemolytic disease (Rh incompatibility)
  5. Neonatal infection
  6. Early placental separation, pre-eclampsia/eclampsia
  7. Haemorrhage from birth trauma
  8. SIDS
Causes — Unnatural
Accidental (During Birth)
  • Prolonged labour
  • Pressure on/prolapsed cord
  • Knotted/twisted cord around neck
  • Injuries; death of mother
Accidental (After Birth)
  • Suffocation
  • Precipitate labour (fall into lavatory pan, head injury from falling on hard surface, haemorrhage from torn cord, drowning)
Motives for Criminal Infanticide
  1. Illegitimate child (widows, unmarried girls, married women with absent husband)
  2. Inheritance disputes
  3. Poverty
  4. Family disharmony
  5. Dowry system
  6. Family pride
  7. Custom (e.g., female infanticide)
Modes of Infanticide
Acts of Commission
  1. Mechanical violence: suffocation, strangulation, throttling, drowning, skull fractures, penetrating wounds, twisting neck
  2. Poisoning
Acts of Omission
  • Failure to ligate umbilical cord
  • Failure to provide nutrition
  • Failure to protect from temperature extremes
  • Improper nursing care
Autopsy of Newborn — Objectives & Considerations
  1. Determine: dead-born / still-born / live-born
  2. Determine cause of death
  3. Determine manner of death
  4. Determine intrauterine age and viability
Special External/Internal Examination Features

External: development, skin, maceration, caput succedaneum, umbilical cord, placenta, lividity, FB in mouth/airway, natural orifices, injuries.

Internal: lungs, stomach, intestinal content, ossification centers.

3.9 — Precipitate Labour
Definition

Sudden and rapid delivery of fetus, especially in multipara women with roomy pelvis, without knowledge of the mother.

Types
  1. True precipitate labour
  2. Pseudo/False precipitate labour
Medico-Legal Importance & Complications
  1. Infanticide (key MLI)
  2. Injury to fetus
  3. Suffocation (falling into lavatory pan)
  4. Head injury/skull fracture (falling on hard object)
  5. Haemorrhage (from torn cord end)
  6. Drowning
Viable Age at 7th Month — Key Features
Features of 7-Month Viable Fetus
  1. Length: 13–16 inches
  2. Weight: 2–4 lbs
  3. Skin: raw-looking, thick vernix caseosa
  4. Eyelids: non-adherent; eyelashes present; membrana pupillaris avascular and undergoing atrophy
  5. Nails: well developed but don't reach fingertip
  6. Caecum lies in right iliac fossa
  7. Meconium: present throughout large bowel
  8. Testes may be at internal inguinal ring
  9. Cerebral hemisphere convolutions in formation
  10. Ossification centers: in talus; 2nd and 3rd sternum segments
3.10 — SIDS, Battered Baby & Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy
SIDS (Sudden Infant Death Syndrome / Cot Death / Crib Death)

Sudden death of an infant that is unexpected by history and in whom a thorough autopsy fails to reveal an adequate cause of death.

  1. Age range: 2 weeks – 2 years; peak 1–7th month (especially 2nd–3rd month)
  2. Sex: Slight male preponderance
  3. Season: More in colder/winter months
  4. Socioeconomic: Low income, single parent, poor conditions
  5. Risk factors: Prematurity, hypersensitivity to cow's milk, infection
Battered Baby Syndrome (Caffey Syndrome)

Multiple injuries in a child inflicted by parents or foster parents, not accidental in nature.

  1. Age: <1 year. Sex: Male predominance
  2. Position in family: Eldest or youngest; unwanted, illegitimate
  3. Perpetrator profile: Younger parents, isolated, lower education, family disharmony, criminal record, unemployed
  4. Precipitating factors: Crying, refusal to be quiet, soiling of nappies
  5. Injuries: Direct manual violence; soft tissue injuries; abrasions/bruises/lacerations of different ages; injury to head, neck, face
  6. Delay in seeking medical attention is a hallmark sign
Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy (MBPS)

A form of child abuse where a caregiver (usually the mother) exaggerates or fabricates illness in a child to gain medical attention/sympathy.

Diagnostic Criteria
  1. Illness is produced or alleged (or both) by a parent
  2. Repeated requests for medical care → multiple procedures
  3. Parental denial of knowledge of cause of symptoms
  4. Regression of symptoms when child is separated from parent
Methods of Illness Production
  • Adding blood to child's urine sample (finger prick)
  • Blocking airways (closed nostrils + pushed-up jaw / pillow over face)
  • Injecting insulin → hypoglycemia
  • Inducing vomiting; administering laxatives; salt poisoning for diarrhea
⚠️ Why MBPS is Hard to Diagnose

The parent/caregiver appears extremely caring and attentive, manipulating doctors and inducing symptoms. Diagnosis is made extremely difficult by this deception. Motivation: need for attention and satisfaction in deceiving authority figures.
