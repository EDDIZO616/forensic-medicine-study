TOPIC 01
Drowning Asphyxial death by submersion · Classification · PM findings · Medico-legal aspects
1.1 Definition & Overview ▼
Drowning is a form of asphyxial death due to aspiration of fluid into the air passages by submersion of the body in water or fluid medium.
★ Complete submersion is NOT necessary — submersion of just the nose and mouth is sufficient to cause drowning.
1.2 Circumstances of Body Found in Water ▼
  • Died from natural disease before falling into the water
  • Died from natural disease while already in the water
  • Died from injury before being thrown into the water
  • Died from injury while in the water
  • Drowned accidentally or by submersion (suicide/homicide)
1.3 Phases of Drowning ▼

Phase 1 — Breath Holding

  • Lasts for a variable length of time
  • CO₂ accumulates in blood
  • Stimulation of the respiratory centre in the brain
  • Inevitable inhalation of large volumes of water

Phase 2 — Swallowing of Water

  • Coughing, vomiting, progressive loss of consciousness
  • Escape of remaining air from lungs
  • Lungs replaced by water

Phase 3 — Terminal Phase

  • Profound unconsciousness and convulsions
  • Gasping
  • Respiratory standstill
  • Failure of heart
  • Irreversible changes in the brain
  • Death
Vicious Cycle: Deep inspiration → Water enters respiratory passage → Air driven out of lungs → Need for more air → Deep inspiration (repeat)
1.4 Classification of Drowning ▼
Typical Drowning
  • Obstruction of airways and lungs by inhalation of fluid = "Wet Drowning"
  • Subtypes: Fresh water & Salt water
Atypical Drowning
  • Very little or no fluid in air passages
  • Dry drowning
  • Immersion syndrome (vagal inhibition)
  • Submersion of the unconscious
  • Secondary/near-drowning

Fresh Water Drowning

  • Water crosses alveolar membrane into circulation → marked hypervolaemia
  • Red cells swell or burst → haemolysis → liberation of K⁺
  • Heart submitted to anoxia, hypervolaemia, K⁺ excess, and sodium deficit
  • Ventricular fibrillation due to anoxia and potassium excess within 4–5 minutes

Salt Water Drowning

  • Marked hypertonicity of inhaled water causes loss of fluid from circulation
  • Fulminating pulmonary edema and progressive hypovolaemia

Atypical — Dry Drowning

  • Intense laryngeal spasm due to entry of water into nasopharynx and larynx
  • Very little water enters the lungs
  • Best case for resuscitation

Atypical — Immersion Syndrome

  • Due to sudden impact with very cold water → cardiac arrest from vagal inhibition
  • Victims often young people with excess of alcohol
  • It also results from falling/diving feet first or duck diving by inexperienced swimmers
  • Loss of consciousness instantaneous; death in few minutes
  • Autopsy: none of the usual signs of drowning

Atypical — Submersion of the Unconscious

  • Victim suffers from epilepsy, heart disease, or is drunk/has head injury during fall

Secondary / Near-Drowning

  • Mainly due to infection from inhalation of contaminated water
  • Lung complications, oxygen deficiency, tired heart, cerebral edema
  • Victim may look alert and breathing, may respond to initial resuscitation
  • Late stage: respiratory distress, hypotension, cardiac arrhythmia may cause death
  • Ballooning of lungs may be absent; foam formation may be negligible
  • Complete picture of death by drowning not found

1.6 Internal (Post-Mortem) Findings ▼

Lungs & Respiratory Tract

  • Airways filled with froth, sand, weeds, etc. found in the water
  • Lungs are voluminous, edematous, doughy to feel with rib markings
  • Paltauf's haemorrhages seen
  • Oozing of blood-stained frothy fluid and ballooning on cut section

Other Internal Findings

  • Watery fluid transudates into pleural cavities during putrefaction
  • Hydrostatic haemorrhage: 2 metres depth — 20 min
  • Haemorrhages in the middle ear and mastoid air cells
  • Stomach may contain watery fluid or foreign material from the water (e.g. soil, sand)
  • Bilateral haemorrhages within the petrous temporal bones in drowning

Death from Natural Causes Before Entering Water

  • Myocardial infarct
  • Accidental or suicidal injuries may be inflicted
  • Trauma in the water is common
  • Sudden entry of cold water into pharynx and larynx (and nasal passages) can produce powerful stimulation of nerve endings in the mucosa
1.7 Diatoms — The Critical Evidence ▼
Diatoms are microscopic unicellular algae present in water. They have a silicaceous cell wall which resists acid digestion, heat and putrefaction.
  • Size: 10–80 microns
  • Only a live body with circulation can transport diatoms from lungs to brain or bone marrow
  • Isolation: acid digestion of tissue (commonly bone marrow)
  • Seen under dark-ground illumination
★ Finding diatoms in brain or bone marrow = definitive evidence of ante-mortem drowning (required circulation to distribute them).
1.8 Cause of Death & Diagnosis ▼
Causes of Death
  1. Asphyxia
  2. Ventricular fibrillation
  3. Laryngeal spasm
  4. Vagal inhibition
  5. Exhaustion
Diagnosis of Drowning
  • Froth at nose/mouth
  • Weeds & gravel/soil in hand
  • Voluminous lungs
  • Diatoms in tissues
1.9 Medico-Legal Aspects ▼

The key medico-legal questions to answer in a drowning case:

  1. Was the death due to drowning or another cause?
  2. Length of time the body was in water
  3. Was it accidental / suicidal / homicide?
Classic Case "Brides of the Bath" case — a famous case where drowning was used as a method of murder; illustrates homicide by drowning is possible.
TOPIC 02
Impotence & Sterility Definitions · Causes · Examination · Sterilization · Contraception · ART
2.1 Key Definitions ▼
Impotence: Inability of a person to perform sexual intercourse and achieve gratification (unable to copulate).
Erectile Dysfunction: Inability to develop and maintain an erection for satisfactory sexual intercourse in the absence of an ejaculatory disorder such as premature ejaculation.
Quod (impotence quoad hanc): A male may be impotent with one particular female but not with another.
Frigidity: Inability to initiate or maintain sexual arousal pattern in female (absence of desire or incapacity to achieve orgasm).
Sterility: Absolute inability of either male or female to procreate. In males: inability to make female conceive. In females: inability to conceive.
Infertility: Failure to conceive (regardless of cause) after 1 year of unprotected and regular intercourse.
★ A sterile person may or may not be impotent — and an impotent person may or may not be sterile. These are distinct concepts.
2.2 When the Question Arises (Legal Context) ▼
Civil Cases
  • Divorce
  • Adultery
  • Nullity of marriage
  • Disputed paternity and legitimacy
  • Claims for damages (loss of sexual function)
Criminal Cases
  • Adultery
  • Rape
  • Unnatural offences
  • (Impotence cited as defence)
Key Legal Rule Permanent impotence = ground for nullity of marriage/divorce (incapable of consummation). Sterility alone is NOT a ground for nullity/divorce.

2.5 Examination of Alleged Impotence / Sterility ▼
Organic vs Psychological Impotence: If the patient has NEVER had an erection → likely organic. If sometimes → could be organic or psychological.

General Principles

  • Examined only when asked by court or police
  • Informed consent is mandatory
  • Full history: illness, surgery, mental condition, sexual history, smoking, diet, obesity, medications
  • Psychosocial examination (interview + questionnaire); sexual partner may be interviewed

Male Examination

  • Complete medical exam including CNS
  • Pulse, BP, secondary sexual characteristics, site of urethral meatus, stenosis, penile sensitivity, deformities
  • Testicular size, epididymis, spermatic cord, varicocele
  • Bulbocavernosus reflex test: Squeeze glans → anus contracts if nerve intact

Male — Investigations

  • Semen analysis (essential in infertility)
  • Blood counts, blood sugar, urinalysis, lipid & thyroid profiles, creatinine, liver enzymes, PSA
  • Serum testosterone, LH, serum prolactin
  • PGE1 injection into corpora (erection if vasculature adequate)
  • Duplex ultrasonography (vascular function, atherosclerosis)
  • USG testes / transrectal USG (prostate)
  • Nocturnal penile tumescence testing: Normal = 5–6 erections during sleep (REM). Absence suggests organic defect.
  • Penile biothesiometry (electromagnetic vibration — sensitivity & nerve function)

Female Examination

  • Hair distribution, clitoris size, Bartholin glands, labia majora/minora, lesions
  • Vaginal mucosa (estrogen deficiency or infection)
  • Cervix: Pap smear + STD cultures
  • Postcoital (Sims-Huhner) test: Spermatozoa amount & motility in cervical mucus during preovulatory period
  • Bimanual examination: uterus size/position, adnexal masses, fibroids, endometriosis
  • HSG, pelvic USG, hysterosonogram, MRI
★ Opinion for impotence in males is given in double negative form: "there is nothing to suggest the person is incapable of sexual intercourse." A definitive opinion cannot be given unless there is gross deviation from normal.
2.6 Sterilization — Types & Medico-legal Aspects ▼
Sterilization: Process to render a person sterile without affecting potency or sexual functions.
Compulsory

Ordered by the State; performed on mentally/physically defective persons, sexual criminals (as punishment), or for eugenics.

Voluntary
  • Therapeutic: Prevent danger to health/life from future pregnancy
  • Eugenic: Prevent conception of physically/mentally defective children
  • Contraceptive: Family planning

Surgical Methods

  • Males: Vasectomy (dividing vas deferens). Chemical: ethanol, formaldehyde, AgNO₃.
  • Females: Tubectomy (ligation), hysteroscopy (electrocoagulation), laparotomy/minilap (Pomeroy, Madelener, Aldridge, cornual resection, fimbrectomy), laparoscopy (clips).

Chemical Castration

  • Antiandrogen cyproterone acetate, Depo-Provera, or antipsychotic Benperidol
  • Does NOT remove organs — not a form of sterilization
  • Reduces sexual drive, masturbation frequency, sexual fantasies
  • Treatment strategy for sex offenders (alternative to imprisonment/death penalty)

Medico-legal Aspects

  • No absolute guarantee of sterility; may prove irreversible. Man is NOT sterile immediately after vasectomy — additional protection needed for ~2–3 months (≥20 ejaculations).
  • Written consent of both husband and wife essential; doctor liable if performed without consent.
  • Desirable only in individuals above 30 years with at least 2 children (one male).
  • Healthy unmarried or married persons without children should NOT be permanently sterilized even if they volunteer.
  • Failure of male contraceptive → suspicion of wife's infidelity → litigation (divorce, illegitimacy, disputed paternity).
2.7 Contraception ▼

Steroidal Contraception

  • OCP: Progestins (levonorgestrel, norethisterone, desogestrel) + estrogens (ethinyl-estradiol or mestranol)
  • Injectable: DMPA (Depo medroxyprogesterone acetate), NETEN (norethisterone enanthate)
  • Implants: Norplant (levonorgestrel), Implanon (desogestrel)

Permanent

  • Males: Vasectomy / chemical sclerosing agents
  • Females: Tubectomy, hysteroscopy, laparotomy/minilap, laparoscopy with clips

Temporary / Natural

  • Rhythm method (abstinence during fertile period)
  • Coitus interruptus (withdrawal before ejaculation)
  • Breastfeeding
  • Barrier: diaphragm (female), condom (male), spermicidal agents
  • IUDs: Copper T 200, Cu T 380A, Multiload 250/375, levonorgestrel IUS, Progestasert, Lippes loop
2.8 Artificial Insemination (AI) ▼
AI: Introduction of semen from husband or donor by instruments into vagina/uterus to achieve pregnancy not attainable by sexual intercourse.

Types

TypeDescription
AIHArtificial insemination homologous/husband
AIDArtificial insemination donor
AIHD'Pooled' — donor semen + husband's semen; technical possibility husband is father

Routes (in order of common use)

  • IUI — intrauterine (most common; highest success rate)
  • ICI — intracervical
  • IVI — intravaginal (low success rate)
  • ITI — intratubal (most invasive, higher infection risk)

Procedure

  • Semen obtained by masturbation after 1 week abstinence; 1 ml deposited just above internal os at ovulation (day 14)
  • Semen 'washed' in Ham's F10 media (no L-glutamine) at 37°C — removes mucus and non-motile sperms
  • Success rate: 5–30% depending on type; affected by stress, egg and sperm quality

Medico-legal Aspects of AI

  1. Litigation: Doctor may be sued after birth of defective child. Donor must be screened for genetic defects.
  2. Divorce: AI for sterility = NOT a ground for divorce. AI for impotence = IS a ground. AID without husband's consent = ground + doctor liable (cruelty).
  3. Legitimacy: AIH = legitimate child. AID = technically illegitimate but husband accepted as father in practice.
  4. Adultery: No physical coitus = NOT adultery.
  5. Incest: Risk of incestuous relationship between AI offspring and donor's children.
  6. Unmarried/widow: No legal bar. Child of single woman through AID = legitimate. AID should be done only on married woman with husband's written consent.
  7. Deceased husband: Child born through AIH with stored sperm of deceased husband = legitimate.
  8. Kenya: AID has NOT been legalized; should only be undertaken at infertility centres after counselling.
2.9 Assisted Reproductive Technology (ART) ▼
ART: Any fertility treatment in which gametes (sperms and eggs) are manipulated outside the body; gametes/embryos are replaced back to establish pregnancy.

Female infertility: 1/3 · Male infertility: 1/3 · Combined: 15% · Unexplained: remainder

#ProcedureKey Features
1IVF (In Vitro Fertilization)Controlled ovarian hyperstimulation → transvaginal USG-guided oocyte retrieval → fertilization in culture (or ICSI) → transcervical transfer of zygotes (day 3–5). Most common ART.
2GIFT (Gamete Intrafallopian Transfer)Laparoscopic transfer of unfertilized eggs + sperms into fallopian tube; fertilization inside body.
3ZIFT (Zygote Intrafallopian Transfer)Day 1 fertilized eggs (zygotes) laparoscopically transferred into fallopian tube.
4ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection)One sperm directly injected into egg; indicated in male factor infertility.
5Ovum DonationDonor IVF for poor egg numbers/quality; eggs fertilized by husband's sperm → transferred to patient's uterus.
6Oocyte FreezingOvum preserved at -196°C; takes 2–4 weeks (hormones → stimulate ovulation → egg retrieval). Used by women delaying pregnancy.
7MicromanipulationZona drilling, partial zona drilling.
TOPIC 03
Paternity Testing Legal framework · Fertility examination · Blood groups · DNA · Anthropological signs
3.1 Legal Framework (Kenya) ▼
  • LawChildren Act CAP 141 (2012) — governs child status in family.
  • Section 81: Child custody = parental rights/duties relating to possession of child.
  • Article 53(1)(e) — Constitution: Child has right to equal responsibility from both parents.
  • Section 76(3)(f): Court considers financial ability of each parent. Both parents swear affidavit of means.

Presumption of Paternity

  • Father = person married to mother from conception to birth (or any portion thereof)
  • Nullification of marriage does NOT affect paternity status
  • Presumed date of conception: day 182–300 (counted back from date of birth)

If Mother Was Not Married

Father = the man who:

  1. By complete and open declaration acknowledges the child as his, or
  2. Is declared father by valid court judgment, or
  3. Marries the mother after birth of the child under existing legal conditions
Important There is no legal provision to establish paternity in cases of artificial insemination.
3.2 Methods of Excluding Paternity ▼
  1. Examination for fertility (andrological)
  2. Establishment of time of conception and maturity of newborn
  3. Examination of hereditary characteristics:
    • Blood group examination (ABO, MN, Rh)
    • Examination of enzyme systems (isoenzymes)
    • Hereditary serum factors (haptoglobin, Gm, Gc)
    • HLA system
    • Hereditary anthropological signs
    • Chromosomal examination
3.3 Fertility Examination (Andrological) ▼
  • Opinion of andrologist requested
  • History (parotitis, TB, varicocele — diseases damaging spermiogenesis)
  • Genital inspection: hypo/epispadias, induration penis plastica, irreducible scrotal hernia
  • Examine testes: undescended (severe spermiogenesis disturbance), cysts, tumours, varicoceles

Semen Analysis

  • Produced by masturbation — NOT from brought sample (motility/pH changes rapidly; condom spermicides damage cells)
  • Visual: greyish-white, sticky, 3–6 ml; fresh pH ~7.2; becomes fluid after 20–30 min
TermDefinition
Normospermia3–6 ml; sperm count >40 million; ≥60% motile
Hypospermia<3 ml; sperm count 10–20 million/ml
AzoospermiaNo sperm in ejaculate
NecrospermiaSperm do not react to intervention (supravital staining, warming)

Testicular Biopsy Findings

  • Hypoplasia of germinal epithelium with sperm precursors + normal Leydig cells
  • Tubular obstruction with normal spermatogenesis + azoospermia
  • Damage to germinal cells — development only to spermatocyte level, no spermatids/mature sperm
  • Only Sertoli cells visible; occasional Leydig cell damage
  • Hyaline degeneration of tubular epithelium → unequivocal sterility
  • Leydig cell tumour
★ Hyaline degeneration + testicular atrophy on biopsy = unequivocal sterility. The biopsy provides indication of previous fertilization capability over years.
3.4 Calculation of Time of Conception ▼
  • Presumed conception: day 182–300 counted back from date of birth
  • Father must have been living with mother during this period
  • WHO: Mature baby born between 37th–42nd week (day 259–293); weight ≥2500 g; body length 53 cm; cranial circumference 36 cm
  • Wide variation in weight; cranial circumference and body length are fairly stable
  • Newborn measurements can narrow down the conception window → pivotal in paternity
3.5 Blood Group Systems for Paternity ▼

Blood group antigens on red cells are hereditarily determined; their genetics are among the best studied in heredity.

Bernstein's Principles

  1. A child CANNOT have a blood group antigen which neither parent possesses.
  2. If a trait is in homozygote form in offspring, neither parent can be homozygote for the opposite gene.
"Mater semper certa est" — certainty of motherhood is assumed; characteristics in child not found in mother must come from father.
SystemExclusion %Key Points
ABO17.6%3 alleles: A, B, O. A & B dominant over O. Subtypes of A (A₁, A₂, A₃) rare — valuable for proof. Cannot differentiate AO from AA by serology alone.
MNSs32.1%MM, NN homozygote; MN heterozygote. Combined with S and s: MS, Ms, NS, Ns. Non-dominant. One of the most powerful systems with 4-test sera.
Rh28%Linked loci: C-c, D-d, E-e. D = Rh positivity. Crossing over not observed. 36 genotypes possible; 5 clinically listed in European populations.
Duffy (FY)18%Fya, Fyb, and quiet gene in blacks. Most conspicuous racial difference among blood groups.
Kell-Cellano4.8%K dominant over k. Genotypes: KK, Kk, kk. Rare, so useful for positive proof of paternity.
Xg–X-linked dominant. Present in 66% men, 84% women. Women have allele pair (XX); men have one allele only (XY).
★ If paternity is excluded by blood groups, HLA, serum factors, or enzyme systems → further tests are superfluous.

3.7 HLA System ▼
  • HLA antigens found on leukocytes and other cells = transplantation antigens
  • Enormous polymorphism: theoretically >1 million genotype variations
  • Exclusion of non-fathers by phenotype examination alone: 96%
  • By this method alone, very powerful for both exclusion and proof of paternity
  • Used in serostatistical examinations as positive proof of paternity
3.8 Anthropological Examination ▼

Based on heritable morphological characteristics. Usually ordered by court alongside blood group examinations.

Morphological Signs Examined

  • Hair colour, skull form, facial form (anthropometric measurements)
  • Nose: form, size, shape of nasal openings
  • Brow shape, eye colour, iris variety
  • Mouth: lip curve, upper/lower lip position, corner of mouth, chin form
  • Ear: size/shape of ear lobes (tapering or hanging free), auricle position
  • Hands and nails

Dominantly Inherited Traits (High Value)

  • Arachnodactyly, brachydactyly, syndactyly
  • Hypoplasia of the enamel
  • Carabelli tubercle
Limitation Anthropological examinations alone DO NOT establish proof of paternity. They are complementary. A probability opinion only. Trait may skip a generation. Must be combined with blood group and serostatistical methods.
3.9 Chromosomal Examination ▼
  • One chromosome of each pair inherited from father, one from mother
  • Chromosomes examined in metaphase (cell division inhibited)
  • Two chromatids joined at centromere visible in this stage
  • Chromosomes numbered 1–22; sex chromosomes XX or XY
  • If chromosomal abnormality in child matches putative father (and is rare in population) = positive proof

Chromosomal Banding Techniques

  • G-banding, C-banding, Q-banding — characteristic dark/light band configurations
  • Results are confirmatory and unambiguous when alteration found in father is passed to child
3.10 Proof of Paternity & Statistical Methods ▼
  • Under optimal conditions, combined blood group + enzyme + protein examination can exclude non-fathers in >90% of cases
  • Paternity Index (PI): Probability statistics differentiating true father from non-father
  • Essen-Möller formula: Serostatistical calculation; basis of Hummel gene statistic data; applicable only to same ethnic groups
  • Gene frequency values crucial — calculations valid only within the same population group
  • Rare markers in both child and putative father greatly increase certainty of proof
  • Superfecundation (simultaneous fertilization by different fathers) must be considered in expanded pedigree analysis
★ Positive proof requires rare markers in both the child and supposed father that do not or very rarely appear in the general population. The more such shared rare markers, the higher the certainty.

When Chromosomal Examination is Especially Indicated

  • Two men with non-significantly different serostatistical values
  • Blood group does not exclude the named father but serostatistical result is uncertain
  • Anthropological examination is not convincing
