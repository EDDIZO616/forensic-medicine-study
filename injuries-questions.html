Unit 1 · Injuries & Wounds · SAQ 1–10

SAQ 015 MARKS
Define an abrasion and describe the four types of abrasions with examples of each.
Definition (1 mark): An injury involving only the outer layers of the skin, not penetrating the full thickness of the epidermis.

Four types (1 mark each):
  • Scratch/Linear: Caused by sharp/pointed object (fingernails, thorns, pins) moving across skin; clean start, tags at end. Seen in throttling, sexual assault, child abuse.
  • Graze/Sliding: Horizontal friction between skin and rough surface; shows parallel longitudinal lines indicating direction of force. Most common type — seen in RTAs.
  • Pressure/Crushing: Direct impact or linear pressure of rough object; crushes superficial cuticle. E.g., noose or ligature marks in hanging/strangulation.
  • Imprint/Patterned: Force applied perpendicular to skin; cuticle bears object's imprint; becomes brownish and parchmentized. E.g., tyre tread, radiator grill, bite marks.
SAQ 025 MARKS
Describe the healing process of a bruise and outline the methods used to estimate the age of a bruise.
Healing process (3 marks): Extravasation of blood is followed by an inflammatory reaction causing vasodilation. Macrophages break down haemoglobin to biliverdin (green). Biliverdin reductase converts this to bilirubin (yellow). Iron released from haemoglobin combines with ferritin → haemosiderin. Colour change starts at the periphery and extends inwards.

Methods of dating (2 marks):
  • Histology (only in postmortem context)
  • Colour changes (visual examination)
  • Calorimetry
  • Spectrophotometry
Important caveat: Reliable microscopic dating is not possible. Only state whether bruise is recent or old.
SAQ 035 MARKS
Compare and contrast the characteristics of incised wounds versus lacerations.
  • Causative agent: Incised — sharp cutting instrument; Laceration — blunt force (shearing/crushing)
  • Margins: Incised — clean-cut, well-defined, everted; Laceration — ragged, irregular, tissue tags, crushed hair bulbs
  • Haemorrhage: Incised — more (vessels cut cleanly); Laceration — less (arteries crushed and clot rapidly)
  • Depth: Incised — deeper at commencement, shallower at end (tailing); Laceration — variable with internal tissue bridges
  • Healing: Both produce permanent scars; vital reaction differentiates antemortem from postmortem
  • Forensic: Incised wounds imply intent (criminal context); suicidal incised wounds show hesitation cuts; lacerations commonly from RTAs and blunt assault
SAQ 045 MARKS
What are the characteristics of stab wounds? How does the medicolegal context help determine suicidal versus homicidal intent?
Characteristics (3 marks):
  • Depth greater than length and width (key feature)
  • Clean inverted margins; everted in fatty areas
  • Length slightly less than blade width (skin stretches)
  • May have abrasion/bruise at full penetration
  • Depth ≤ blade length; may exceed on yielding surfaces
Medicolegal context (2 marks):
  • Suicidal: Accessible body parts (chest, abdomen); direction upward, backward, to the right; hesitation wounds possible; variable depth
  • Homicidal: Inaccessible areas; clothing often involved; multiple wounds; no hesitation pattern
  • Accidental: Rare; history of accident clearly present
SAQ 055 MARKS
Describe the features that differentiate an entry wound from an exit wound in a firearm injury.
Entry wound features (3 marks):
  • Abrasion collar / contusion ring (always present)
  • Grease collar (from barrel lubricant)
  • Burning, singeing, blackening at close range
  • Powder tattooing at intermediate range
  • Cherry red tissues from CO at contact range
  • Inverted wound edges
  • Wads in wound (shotgun)
Exit wound features (2 marks):
  • No abrasion collar, burning, blackening, tattooing
  • Everted torn edges with protruding haemorrhagic subcutaneous fat
  • Shapes: stellate, circular, cruciate, elliptical, crescent (especially scalp)
  • May be larger or irregular if bullet tumbles, deforms, or fragments
SAQ 065 MARKS
What is a "shored exit wound"? How is it formed and why is it medicolegally important?
Definition (1 mark): An exit wound that has been supported by a firm surface or object at the time of exit, resulting in a circular or nearly circular defect with a marginal abrasion — mimicking an entry wound.

Formation (2 marks): Occurs when a firm object (belt, waistband, brassiere, collar, tie) is pressed against the skin at the exit site, OR when the body is leaning against a hard surface (wall, chair, floor). The support prevents the skin from tearing outward freely.

Medicolegal importance (2 marks):
  • Can be confused with an entry wound, leading to incorrect conclusion about direction of fire
  • May falsely suggest two entry wounds when there is actually one entry and one shored exit
  • The number of bullets remaining in the body may be incorrectly calculated
  • Context, trajectory, and detailed wound examination must be used to differentiate
SAQ 075 MARKS
List and briefly describe the five S's used as a mnemonic for diagnosing suicidal firearm deaths.
  • Site (1 mark): Classical suicidal sites — temple, heart, mouth, chin. These are accessible areas the individual can reach with their own hand.
  • Single shot (1 mark): Suicide typically involves a single gunshot. Multiple shots suggest homicide or accident.
  • Scene (1 mark): Scene is usually calm, locked from inside, with no evidence of struggle or disturbance.
  • Suicidal Note (1 mark): May be present — written note explaining intent. Absence doesn't rule out suicide, but presence strongly supports it.
  • Spasm — Cadaveric spasm (1 mark): This is the hallmark. The weapon remains firmly gripped in the hand at the moment of death due to cadaveric spasm — near impossible to simulate postmortem.
SAQ 085 MARKS
Describe the classification of firearm injuries from shotguns based on range, including wound characteristics and mortality at each type.
  • Type I — <5 metres (2 marks): All pellets strike as a single mass; results in massive kinetic energy transfer; tissue avulsion; single large circular wound. Mortality: 85–90%.
  • Type II — 5–12 metres (2 marks): Significant pellet dispersal and loss of kinetic energy; less tissue destruction; may penetrate deep fascia; fractures rare; ocular injuries possible; risk of lead pellet embolisation. Mortality: 15–20%.
  • Type III — >12 metres (1 mark): Usually only skin penetrated; diffuse pellet pattern; no significant deep tissue injury. Mortality: 0–5%.
SAQ 095 MARKS
What is the forensic importance of examining the clothing at a medico-legal autopsy?
  • Evidence of force/weapon (1 mark): Cuts, holes, tears, and the pattern of damage may correspond to the weapon used (knife, bullet, blunt object).
  • Trace evidence (1 mark): Hair, fibres, paint chips, glass fragments, vegetation, and insect activity can identify environment of death or contact with a suspect.
  • Range of fire (1 mark): Blackening, burning, and powder tattooing on clothing indicate contact or close-range firearm discharge — even when skin has been cleaned or treated.
  • Identity and history (1 mark): Labels, laundry marks, personal papers, medication in pockets help with identification and medical history.
  • Proper handling (1 mark): Clothing must be removed carefully without tearing; cut away from injury sites if intact removal is impossible; wet clothing air-dried and packed in paper bags (not plastic) to prevent artefactual mould growth.
SAQ 105 MARKS
Define the term "kinetic injury" and give examples. How does the area of force application affect wound severity?
Definition (1 mark): Injuries in category 1 (mechanical/physical) — caused by the application of physical force. Contrasted with non-kinetic (thermal, chemical, miscellaneous).

Examples (2 marks): Abrasion, bruise/contusion, laceration, incised wound, stab wound, firearm wound, fracture/dislocation of bone or joint.

Area vs severity (2 marks): For any given amount of force, the greater the area over which it is delivered, the less severe the wound. This applies to both blunt and sharp trauma.
  • Example — blunt force: a broad-surfaced club causes a bruise/laceration; a narrow-tipped weapon concentrates force → deeper penetration
  • Example — sharp force: a razor-sharp edge concentrates force on a thin line → deeper incision than a blunt edge applying the same force over a wider area

Unit 2 · Autopsy · SAQ 11–20

SAQ 115 MARKS
List five objectives of a medico-legal autopsy.
  • 1 mark each — any five of the following:
  • Identify the deceased (decomposed, burnt, mutilated, or unidentified body)
  • Determine cause of death (natural or unnatural) and interpret significance of any disease
  • Approximate time of death, mode of death, age of injuries, and place of death
  • Determine manner of death (accidental, suicidal, or homicidal)
  • Identify poison or weapon responsible for death in homicide
  • Assess volitional activity possible after trauma and survival time
  • Document extent of external and internal injuries
  • Determine whether injury was likely to cause death in ordinary course of nature
  • In homicide: determine number of persons involved, trace evidence, other related offences
  • In newborns: determine live birth and viability
SAQ 125 MARKS
Describe the procedure to be followed when a body is received at night for a medico-legal autopsy.
  • Preservation (1 mark): Body preserved at 4°C after noting the date and exact time of receipt.
  • Preliminary examination (2 marks): A preliminary external examination is conducted to document: external appearances; rectal (body) temperature; extent of postmortem staining (lividity); presence and degree of rigor mortis.
  • Identification (1 mark): The police officer accompanying the body and identity details are documented; identification by relatives if possible.
  • Full autopsy (1 mark): The actual full postmortem examination is deferred to the next day in daylight, to allow for accurate assessment of colour changes (jaundice, bruise changes, PM staining).
SAQ 135 MARKS
Why is it important that the body should never be embalmed before a medico-legal autopsy? What problems does prior embalming create?
  • Histopathological examination becomes impossible (1 mark): Chemical fixation from embalming fluid alters tissue architecture and cellular morphology, making microscopic examination unreliable.
  • Toxicological analysis is compromised (1 mark): Embalming fluids (formaldehyde, methanol, glycerine) interfere with toxicological tests and may cause false positive or false negative results for drugs and poisons.
  • Bruises may be altered (1 mark): Embalming fluid pressure can extravasate blood from vessels, creating false bruises, or enlarging minor bruises — leading to misinterpretation of assault.
  • Wound tracks are distorted (1 mark): Trochar insertion creates new wounds (potentially mimicking stab/gunshot wounds), and existing wound tracks may be distorted, making it impossible to determine direction of fire or type of weapon.
  • Chain of evidence is broken (1 mark): Evidence within the body (e.g., undigested tablets, retained bullets, trace materials) may be washed out or disturbed during the embalming process.
SAQ 145 MARKS
Describe the four types of skin incisions used in autopsy and state the specific indication for each.
  • I-shaped (1 mark): Incision from chin straight down to symphysis pubis, avoiding umbilicus. Most common method used.
  • Y-shaped (1 mark): Straight line from xiphisternum to pubis, with forks running to the chest towards the acromion processes. Standard alternative to I-shaped.
  • Modified Y-shaped (2 marks): Midline from suprasternal notch to symphysis, extending over the clavicle and upward over the neck behind the ears. Used specifically when a detailed study of neck organs is required — such as in cases of hanging or strangulation.
  • T-shaped / "bucket handle" (1 mark): Transverse incision from acromion to acromion along the clavicle line + midline to pubis creating a calyx/U-shape. Used for specific anatomical requirements or when the standard incisions are not appropriate.
SAQ 155 MARKS
Compare the four evisceration methods used in autopsy, describing their advantages and disadvantages.
  • En masse — Letulle (1 mark): Most organs removed in one sweep. Advantage: rapid evisceration; leaves all attachments intact. Disadvantage: lengthiest subsequent dissection.
  • Virchow's method (1 mark): Individual organs removed one by one with immediate dissection of each. Advantage: quick and effective for single-organ pathology; good for targeted cases. Disadvantage: inter-organ relationships are disrupted.
  • En bloc — Ghon (2 marks): Compromise between the above two — most widely used in the UK. Advantage: relatively quick; preserves most important inter-organ relationships. Disadvantage: requires more skill than Virchow's.
  • In situ — Rokitansky (1 mark): Organs dissected in place with little evisceration. Advantage: protects staff from highly transmissible diseases; method of choice in such cases. Disadvantage: technically demanding; rarely performed.
SAQ 165 MARKS
What is "chain of evidence" in the context of medico-legal autopsies? Why is it important and how is it maintained?
Definition (1 mark): The unbroken documentation and control of all specimens, samples, and items of evidence from the moment of collection at autopsy to their final use in court proceedings.

Importance (2 marks): Any break in the chain of evidence renders the specimen inadmissible in court or open to challenge by the defence. Without it, there is no guarantee that the specimen tested was the one collected from the deceased, and evidence of homicide may be dismissed.

Maintenance (2 marks):
  • Identifying the body by authorized persons before autopsy begins
  • All articles (clothes, jewellery, bullets, trace materials) are listed, labelled, sealed, and mentioned in the report
  • Specimens handed to the police constable/IO against a signed receipt
  • All documentation signed; alterations initialled; nothing erased
  • Photographic documentation of major findings
SAQ 175 MARKS
What information can be obtained from examining the eyes during the external examination at autopsy?
  • Petechial haemorrhages (1 mark): In the conjunctiva — strongly suggests asphyxial death (throttling, compression asphyxia, strangulation). Also seen in cases of high venous pressure (cardiac failure, severe coughing).
  • Pupil state and colour (1 mark): Dilated fixed pupils suggest death; pin-point pupils may suggest opioid poisoning before death; lens or corneal opacities can indicate underlying disease or time since death.
  • Scleral icterus (1 mark): Yellow discolouration of sclera → jaundice (liver disease, haemolytic conditions).
  • Softening of the eyeball (1 mark): Indicates time since death; occurs as intraocular pressure drops postmortem.
  • Contact lenses (1 mark): May aid in identification; their presence may also contribute to accidents (e.g., distorted vision in road traffic cases).
  • Artificial eyes: Important for identification purposes.
SAQ 185 MARKS
Describe the post-autopsy reconstruction of the body and explain why it is important.
Importance (1 mark): Post-autopsy reconstruction must be of a high standard so that the body does not leak and can be viewed by the next of kin without distress. It upholds dignity of the deceased and is a professional obligation.

Steps (4 marks):
  • Fluid removal: Residual blood, ascitic/pleural fluid, and bowel contents are aspirated and dried from cavities to prevent leakage.
  • Wadding: Cavities lined with cotton wool or adequate wadding to absorb remaining fluids.
  • Viscera containment: Organs placed in biohazard/clear plastic bags, returned to cavities on top of the wadding. Foreign objects (gloves, syringes) disposed of separately.
  • Skull: Skull cap secured with notches to prevent slippage; brain and dura placed in plastic bag inside cranial cavity; wadding added.
  • Suturing: Continuous or baseball suture using nylon (or cotton for cost-effectiveness). Body then washed with water and disinfectant detergent (hot water avoided to prevent skin damage).
SAQ 195 MARKS
What are the indications for a panel (board) of doctors at a medico-legal autopsy? How does the composition of the panel affect its conclusion?
Indications for a panel (2 marks):
  • Custodial deaths (deaths in police or prison custody)
  • Deaths on the operation table (intraoperative deaths)
  • Second autopsies (re-examination where the first autopsy result is contested)
Composition rules (2 marks):
  • Two doctors are required for suspicious death of a female within 7 years of marriage (burns or other suspicious cause)
  • A panel should ideally have an odd number of members so that a majority decision can be reached in case of disagreement
Significance (1 mark): When autopsy surgeons disagree on the cause or manner of death, the majority opinion guides the legal conclusion — an odd-numbered panel always allows for a deciding vote, preventing a deadlock in high-stakes cases such as homicides.
SAQ 205 MARKS
Why should the word "probably" be avoided in an autopsy report? What is the recommended approach when the cause or manner of death cannot be established?
Why avoid "probably" (2 marks):
  • Cause and manner of death are opinions, not absolute facts — but they should be stated with professional confidence. The word "probably" introduces ambiguity that can be exploited in legal proceedings.
  • A poor, uncertain opinion is far worse than no opinion — it misleads legal authorities rather than informing them. An experienced autopsy surgeon should give the most supportable opinion based on all available evidence.
Recommended approach (3 marks):
  • If the cause of death cannot be ascertained: state cause of death as "undetermined"
  • If the manner of death cannot be established: state manner of death as "unknown"
  • Preserve viscera, await laboratory results, and submit a supplementary/provisional report if further investigations are pending
  • In suspected poisoning: reserve the final opinion until the Chemical Examiner's report is received

Unit 3 · PM Artefacts · SAQ 21–30

SAQ 215 MARKS
Define a postmortem artefact and classify them by causative agent, giving examples.
Definition (1 mark): A change or feature that was not physiologically or pathologically present in the body before death, but was caused or introduced after death, leading to confusion about its nature and causing misinterpretation of medicolegally significant findings.

Classification by causative agent (3 marks — 1 each):
  • Due to postmortem changes: Rigor mortis variants (heat/cold/gas stiffening), lividity changes, decomposition blisters, marbling, adipocere, mummification
  • Third party artefacts: Animal activity (insect/rodent/bird), criminal mutilation (Kennedy/Rayalaseema), surgical alteration, embalming, handling during transport
  • Environmental artefacts: Post-mortem corrosion by gasoline, burns/heat effects, submersion changes, exhumation damage
Medicolegal consequence (1 mark): Misidentification can lead to wrong conclusions about cause/manner of death and ultimately the imprisonment of innocent persons.
SAQ 225 MARKS
Describe the artefacts that can be produced by CPR and other resuscitation measures, and how they might be misinterpreted.
  • Injection sites (1 mark): Multiple injection marks from IV access/medications may be mistaken for drug abuse marks. History and case sheet essential.
  • Defibrillator marks (1 mark): Paddle/pad burns on the chest simulate antemortem contact abrasions or thermal burns.
  • CPR injuries (2 marks): Rib and sternal fractures; haemothorax; haemopneumothorax from fractured ribs; fat embolism from bone marrow release; lacerations of spleen, liver, and diaphragm from vigorous compressions — all can simulate blunt force assault.
  • Positive pressure ventilation (1 mark): Emphysema and sub-pleural air — may simulate spontaneous pneumothorax or blast injury pathology.
  • Endotracheal intubation: Injuries to larynx and adjacent tissues by inexperienced intubators may simulate throttling.
SAQ 235 MARKS
How does postmortem lividity (hypostasis) create medicolegal artefacts? Give examples of how different hypostasis patterns can mislead an autopsy surgeon.
Mechanism (1 mark): After death, blood gravitates to dependent parts due to gravity. When localised or differently coloured due to environment or poisoning, this can simulate pathological conditions.

Examples of misinterpretation (4 marks — 1 each):
  • Localised lividity → bruise: Localised pooling of blood in one area can be mistaken for a blunt force contusion
  • Heart hypostasis → myocardial infarction: Blood pooling in the dependent posterior wall of the heart simulates ischaemic discolouration
  • Lung hypostasis → pneumonia: Dependent consolidation from lividity mimics bronchopneumonic consolidation
  • Stomach/intestine hypostasis → poisoning/strangulation: Discolouration of intestine suggests infarcted bowel; stomach discolouration suggests corrosive poisoning
  • Pink lividity in refrigerated body → CO poisoning: The pale pink colour of hypostasis in a refrigerated body can be confused with the cherry-red lividity of carbon monoxide poisoning
SAQ 245 MARKS
Differentiate gas stiffening from true rigor mortis. What other conditions mimic rigor mortis, and how are they distinguished?
Gas stiffening vs Rigor mortis (2 marks):
  • Gas stiffening: caused by putrefactive gases inflating tissue spaces; no particular order of appearance/disappearance; joints NOT tight (can be moved easily); stiffening disappears when areas are incised; associated with other decomposition changes
  • Rigor mortis: predictable cephalocaudal order; joints firmly fixed; resolves in ~36 hours in normal conditions; no tissue inflation
Heat stiffening (2 marks): Occurs at surrounding temperature >65°C; much more marked than rigor mortis; remains until muscles/ligaments soften with decomposition; associated with burns/fire; rigor mortis does NOT occur simultaneously with heat stiffening.

Cold stiffening (1 mark): Caused by freezing of body fluids and solidification of subcutaneous fat; resolves when body is placed in warm water to thaw; easily distinguished by context (frozen environment).
SAQ 255 MARKS
Describe the artefacts associated with embalming and their medicolegal significance.
  • Trochar insertion wounds (1 mark): The embalmer's trochar creates wounds from the neck and abdomen that can mimic stab wounds or gunshot wound entry/exit points.
  • Wound track distortion (1 mark): Existing wounds (especially gunshot tracks) can be distorted, and false tracks can be created — making determination of direction of fire impossible or misleading.
  • Bruise simulation/exaggeration (1 mark): High-pressure embalming fluid can extravasate blood from vessels, creating false bruises or enlarging small, pre-existing bruises — leading to over-estimation of assault severity.
  • Toxicological interference (1 mark): Embalming chemicals (formaldehyde, methanol, glycerine) contaminate tissue samples, potentially causing false positive/negative results for drugs, poisons, and alcohol.
  • Histological damage (1 mark): Chemical fixation destroys cellular detail needed for vital reaction assessment, making it impossible to determine whether wounds were inflicted ante- or postmortem by histological means.
SAQ 265 MARKS
What are animal-induced postmortem artefacts? How do wounds produced by different animals differ, and how can they be distinguished from antemortem injuries?
Key distinguishing feature (1 mark): Animal-induced postmortem wounds show NO bleeding, no blood clots, and no vital reaction — their absence is the fundamental differentiator from antemortem wounds.

Patterns by animal (3 marks):
  • Rats/rodents: Nibbled pale white wounds with gnawing marks; superficial, irregular, scalloped margins
  • Dogs/jackals: Deeper tooth impressions with tearing; may resemble superficial stab injuries
  • Ants/insects: Extensive superficial tissue damage in sandy/moist areas; brown parchmentised areas mimicking abrasions
  • Birds/vultures: Clean circular holes from beaks — highly similar to gunshot entry wounds
  • Fish/crabs/aquatic animals: Damage to soft areas (lips, genitalia, fingers, toes) in drowned bodies
Additional differentiation (1 mark): Pattern of involvement, location on body, context of discovery, absence of haemorrhage/coagulation, and forensic entomology can all aid in distinguishing animal from human-inflicted wounds.
SAQ 275 MARKS
Describe the Kennedy Phenomenon and Rayalaseema Phenomenon. How do they affect forensic investigation?
Kennedy Phenomenon (2 marks): Medico-legal evaluation of a firearm injury made difficult or impossible by surgical alteration or cleaning of the wound. Surgical debridement of gunshot wounds removes gunpowder residue, alters the wound margins, and changes its overall character — as occurred in the J.F. Kennedy assassination case. The original entry/exit characteristics and range estimation become unreliable.

Rayalaseema Phenomenon (2 marks): Deliberate criminal mutilation of a firearm injury to conceal its nature. Examples include cutting or stabbing through a gunshot wound to make it appear as a stab wound, or inserting a bullet into a stab wound to suggest a gunshot. This is an intentional act by criminals to obstruct justice and confuse the autopsy surgeon.

Key difference (1 mark): Kennedy = medical/surgical (unintentional alteration); Rayalaseema = criminal/deliberate (intentional mutilation). Both compromise the medicolegal investigation of firearm cases.
SAQ 285 MARKS
List the artefacts that can be introduced during the postmortem examination itself, and how the autopsy surgeon can avoid or minimise them.
  • Visceral organ injuries (1 mark): Accidental cuts/tears during dissection may be mistaken for antemortem injuries. Prevention: careful technique; annotate clearly as postmortem artefact in report.
  • Skull fracture extension (1 mark): Saw artefacts during skull opening may extend existing antemortem fractures, distorting the fracture pattern. Prevention: careful sawing; photograph fractures before any cutting.
  • Hyoid/thyroid fracture (1 mark): Forceful extraction of neck organs can fracture the hyoid or thyroid cartilage — simulating manual strangulation. Prevention: extreme caution when removing neck structures; consider in-situ dissection of neck in strangulation cases.
  • Blood oozing into neck (1 mark): Blood from vessels may seep into neck dissection planes during evisceration, simulating antemortem haemorrhage. Prevention: open the skull and drain blood from neck vessels before dissecting the neck.
  • Rib fractures in elderly (1 mark): Osteoporotic ribs may fracture during chest opening or body handling, being mistaken for blunt trauma fractures. Prevention: note bone quality; gentle technique; comment on bone density in report.
SAQ 295 MARKS
How can decomposition artefacts cause misinterpretation in a medico-legal autopsy? Give five specific examples.

1 mark each for any five well-explained examples:

  • Postmortem blisters → antemortem burns: Gas-filled blisters from putrefactive gases under the epidermis look identical to thermal burn blisters; histology differentiates (PM blisters lack vital reaction)
  • Blood-stained froth from nose/mouth → pulmonary oedema or head injury: Decomposition gases force bloody fluid upward; context (history, scene) and other findings needed
  • False groove on neck from tight collar → strangulation: A tight shirt collar/neck tie can leave a groove in the neck during decomposition that mimics a ligature mark; absence of ecchymosis in the groove helps differentiate
  • Rupture of oesophagus/stomach from gastric juices → corrosive acid poisoning: Postmortem self-digestion (autolysis) by gastric acid can perforate the stomach wall; absence of vital reaction in the margins and chemical analysis clarify
  • Loose flaccid vaginal introitus with blood-stained discharge → sexual assault: Normal decomposition changes in female genitalia; full forensic workup (swabs, histopathology) required before any conclusion
  • Marbling → bruising: The greenish-black network pattern from haemolysed blood in decomposing vessels follows vessel anatomy; bruises are irregular and non-vascular in distribution
SAQ 305 MARKS
What are the medicolegal consequences of failing to recognise postmortem artefacts? What qualities in an autopsy surgeon minimise this risk?
Medicolegal consequences (3 marks):
  • Wrong conclusion as to cause of death (e.g., declaring natural death when it was homicide, or vice versa)
  • Incorrect determination of manner of death (accidental vs suicidal vs homicidal)
  • Misidentifying antemortem vs postmortem wounds — falsely implicating injury in death
  • Unjustified suspicion of foul play — police investigating innocent persons
  • Misguided direction to the investigating officer, wasting investigative resources
  • Ultimate consequence: Imprisonment of an innocent person — miscarriage of justice
Qualities that minimise risk (2 marks):
  • Ability and knowledge: Deep understanding of normal postmortem phenomena, decomposition patterns, and the full spectrum of artefacts
  • Dedication: Thorough, systematic examination — not taking shortcuts
  • Wisdom: Correlating gross findings with history, scene evidence, case sheet, laboratory results, and clinical context before reaching any conclusion
  • Intellectual humility: Willingness to state "undetermined" rather than force an uncertain conclusion

CASE STUDY 0120 MARKS
Scenario: A 45-year-old male is found dead on a roadside. He has multiple injuries. On examination of the body: there is a 4 cm × 3 cm area of dark brown parchment-like change on the forehead; a 6 cm × 4 cm purple discolouration on the right shoulder (margins blurred, situated in subcutaneous fatty layer); a 5 cm ragged wound on the scalp with irregular margins, tissue tags, crushed hair bulbs, and minimal bleeding; and an area of skin showing parallel longitudinal lines with epidermis heaped at one end on the left arm.
Part A (4 marks)Identify and classify each of the four injuries described.
Part B (4 marks)Describe the medicolegal significance of each injury finding in the context of determining manner of death.
Part C (4 marks)The shoulder discolouration shows colour changes to yellow at the periphery. Explain the biochemical basis of this colour change and what it tells you about the age of the injury.
Part D (4 marks)Describe how the scalp wound should be examined, documented, and what complications of such wounds are known.
Part E (4 marks)What questions should the autopsy surgeon ask when evaluating whether these injuries are consistent with a road traffic accident?

Part A — Injury Identification (4 marks)

  • Forehead: Antemortem abrasion — specifically an imprint/pressure abrasion (dark brown parchment-like = characteristic of antemortem abrasion with dried crust)
  • Right shoulder: Subcutaneous bruise/contusion — blurred edges and subcutaneous fatty layer location are characteristic features
  • Scalp wound: Laceration (split type) — ragged margins, tissue tags, crushed hair bulbs, minimal bleeding are hallmarks of blunt force laceration
  • Left arm: Graze abrasion (sliding/scrape) — parallel longitudinal lines with epidermis heaped at one end indicating direction of force; most common in RTAs

Part B — Medicolegal Significance (4 marks)

  • Parchment abrasion on forehead: Indicates the precise point of impact; direction of force can be inferred; antemortem (dark brown, parchment) vs postmortem (pale yellow) helps confirm injuries occurred before death
  • Subcutaneous bruise: Confirms blunt force trauma occurred before death; subcutaneous location means it appeared soon after impact; absent vital reaction would indicate postmortem
  • Scalp laceration: Indicates significant blunt force applied to the head; tissue tags and crushed hair bulbs confirm blunt (not sharp) force; antemortem vital reaction (bruising at margins) to be confirmed histologically
  • Graze abrasion: Epidermis heaped at one end indicates direction of drag; strongly consistent with RTA where the body slides across the road surface; embedded glass or gravel can confirm vehicular involvement

Part C — Biochemical Basis of Colour Change (4 marks)

The bruise heals through the following sequence: Haemoglobin released from lysed red cells → broken down by macrophages to biliverdin (green) → converted by biliverdin reductase enzyme to bilirubin (yellow). Separately, iron released from haemoglobin combines with ferritin → haemosiderin (brown). Colour change starts at the periphery and extends inwards. The peripheral yellow colour (bilirubin) indicates the bruise is in the resolving phase — generally several days to a week old, though exact timing is unreliable. Only "recent" vs "old" should be stated formally.

Part D — Examination and Documentation of Scalp Wound (4 marks)

  • If injuries are obscured by hair, the area should be shaved before detailed examination
  • Document: type/nature, size (L×B×D), shape, site (two anatomical landmarks), direction of force application, margins/edges/base, distance from heel, vital reaction
  • Deep or penetrating wounds should NOT be probed until the body is internally examined (to preserve wound track)
  • Photograph with measuring scale and unique autopsy number before any dissection
  • Complications: Haemorrhage is profuse in scalp because temporal arteries are firmly bound and cannot retract; risk of intracranial injury (skull fracture, epidural/subdural haematoma); risk of infection with permanent scarring

Part E — RTA Consistency Assessment (4 marks)

  • Are the injuries consistent with the pattern of a vehicle impact? (Primary: contact injuries from the vehicle; secondary: fall to the ground; tertiary: dragging over the road surface)
  • Is there embedded glass, gravel, or paint material in the wounds matching the scene?
  • What is the distribution of injuries — are they consistent with the alleged mechanism? (e.g., bilateral or multiple injuries from rolling suggest dragging)
  • Is there any injury to the lower limbs consistent with bumper contact at a specific height?
  • Are there any injuries inconsistent with RTA that suggest assault before the body was placed on the road?
  • Scene examination: blood, skid marks, vehicle paint transfer, position of body on road
CASE STUDY 0220 MARKS
Scenario: A 30-year-old male is brought to the mortuary. He was found in his locked bedroom with a gunshot wound to the right temple. A revolver is found tightly clasped in his right hand. The wound at the temple is circular with an abrasion collar, grease collar, and evidence of burning and blackening of surrounding skin. On the left side of the head there is a larger, irregular wound with everted, torn edges. No suicide note was found.
Part A (5 marks)Describe the features of the right temple wound and classify it. At what range was the shot fired? Justify your answer.
Part B (5 marks)Describe the left-sided head wound. What is it? What variations in its shape can occur and why?
Part C (5 marks)Using the "5 S's" mnemonic, assess whether this death is consistent with suicide. What is the significance of the revolver being tightly clasped?
Part D (5 marks)If this case is taken to court and the defence argues that the surgical team cleaned the wound at a hospital before the patient died, what phenomenon does this describe? How does it affect the forensic investigation?

Part A — Right Temple Wound Classification & Range (5 marks)

Classification: Entry wound (2 marks) — Features present: circular hole with abrasion collar (epidermis scraped as bullet enters), grease collar (from barrel lubricant), burning and blackening of surrounding skin.

Range: Contact to close range (within 15 cm) (3 marks) — The presence of burning and blackening indicates the muzzle was within approximately 6–15 cm of the skin. At tight contact range there would also be muzzle imprint and cherry red tissues; blackening without tattooing suggests slightly farther than true contact but within close range. Powder tattooing alone (without blackening) would indicate 15–30 cm. At distances >40 cm, only the abrasion collar remains.

Part B — Left Head Wound (5 marks)

Classification: Exit wound (1 mark)

Characteristics (2 marks): Larger and more irregular than entry wound; everted, torn edges; haemorrhagic subcutaneous tissue may protrude; absence of abrasion collar, burning, blackening, tattooing.

Variations in shape and reasons (2 marks):

  • Stellate/cruciate — common on the scalp due to splitting forces as bullet exits through the rigid skull
  • Larger/more destructive — if bullet tumbled inside and exits non-nose-first
  • Multiple fragments — if bullet breaks up inside, creating multiple small exit points
  • Shored exit wound — circular with abrasion margin if the scalp was pressed against a hard surface at exit
  • Elliptical — if bullet exits at an oblique angle

Part C — Assessment Against 5 S's (5 marks)

  • Site (1 mark): Right temple — classical suicidal site. Consistent with suicide.
  • Single shot (1 mark): Only one entry wound described. Consistent with suicide.
  • Scene (1 mark): Found in a locked bedroom with no disturbance described — strongly consistent with suicide.
  • Suicidal note (0.5 mark): Absent — does not exclude suicide; approximately 25–30% of suicides do not leave notes.
  • Spasm — Cadaveric spasm (1.5 marks): The revolver tightly clasped in the right hand is the hallmark finding. Cadaveric spasm occurs at the instant of death, locking the hand in its final position. It cannot be simulated postmortem by placing a weapon in a relaxed hand — the grip strength pattern in genuine cadaveric spasm is different from postmortem placement.

Overall assessment: All five of the S's are satisfied (the absent suicide note is not disqualifying). The findings are highly consistent with a suicidal gunshot wound.

Part D — Kennedy Phenomenon (5 marks)

Phenomenon: Kennedy Phenomenon (1 mark) — Medico-legal evaluation of a firearm injury made difficult by surgical cleaning, debridement, or alteration of the wound.

How it occurs (2 marks): Surgical debridement removes gunpowder residue (soot, unburnt particles) from the wound margins and surrounding skin. Suturing closes and distorts the wound opening. The original wound dimensions, shape, and range-indicating features (blackening, tattooing, burning) are partially or entirely destroyed.

Effect on forensic investigation (2 marks):

  • Range estimation becomes unreliable or impossible — defence can argue it was a distant shot (homicide) rather than close-range (suicide)
  • Entry/exit distinction may be obscured after suturing
  • Histological vital reaction in the wound margins is altered by surgical manipulation
  • Named after the J.F. Kennedy assassination case where surgical intervention at Parkland Memorial Hospital altered the wound characteristics, leading to decades of forensic controversy
  • Autopsy surgeon must note in the report that the wound was surgically altered and qualify all range estimates accordingly

CASE STUDY 0320 MARKS
Scenario: A 28-year-old female is found dead at home. On external examination: there is a ligature mark around the neck; multiple small crescent-shaped abrasions are present on the neck; there is petechiae in the conjunctivae bilaterally; the face appears cyanosed. Her husband states she died from a fall down the stairs. When the body is brought for autopsy, it is noted the body had been embalmed by the family before the police could intervene.
Part A (5 marks)Classify the abrasions on the neck. What type of violence do they suggest, and what is their significance in determining manner of death?
Part B (5 marks)Describe the significance of conjunctival petechiae and cyanosis in this case. What mechanism produces these findings?
Part C (5 marks)The body has been embalmed. List the specific problems this creates for this particular case, and what the autopsy surgeon can still document.
Part D (5 marks)During autopsy, the neck organs are being extracted and a thyroid cartilage fracture is found. How do you distinguish whether this is antemortem (caused by strangulation) or a postmortem artefact introduced during the autopsy?

Part A — Neck Abrasion Classification (5 marks)

Type of abrasion (2 marks): Crescent-shaped abrasions are scratch/linear abrasions caused by fingernails (nails have a curved shape producing crescent marks). These are a specific sub-type of scratch abrasion.

Violence suggested (2 marks): Fingernail abrasions on the neck are characteristic of manual strangulation (throttling). They can be produced by the victim's own nails (trying to remove the assailant's grip) or by the assailant's nails during grip.

Significance in manner of death (1 mark): These are highly specific indicators of assault and are inconsistent with a fall down the stairs. They indicate external application of force to the neck — strongly supporting homicide.

Part B — Petechiae and Cyanosis (5 marks)

Mechanism (3 marks): In strangulation, external compression of the neck causes: (1) obstruction of venous outflow (jugular veins compressed) while arterial inflow continues briefly → rapid rise in venous/capillary pressure above the level of constriction → rupture of small capillaries → petechiae. Cyanosis results from failure of oxygenated blood to reach the brain and facial tissues combined with venous congestion → deoxyhaemoglobin accumulation → blue discolouration.

Significance (2 marks): Bilateral conjunctival petechiae in the context of a neck ligature mark and fingernail abrasions strongly support asphyxial death from strangulation. Petechiae are also seen in other forms of asphyxia (hanging, smothering, choking) and occasionally in severe cardiac failure or coughing — but in this context, the combination of findings is specific. A fall down the stairs would not typically produce this pattern.

Part C — Problems from Embalming (5 marks)

Specific problems in this case (4 marks):

  • Bruise alteration: Embalming fluid pressure can extravasate blood, creating false bruises on the neck or enlarging any existing petechiae/bruising — making it impossible to accurately map the strangulation grip
  • Toxicological interference: If the husband had administered poison (possible in suspected domestic violence), embalming chemicals now contaminate all visceral samples
  • Histological damage: Vital reaction in wounds and petechiae cannot be reliably assessed histologically after embalming — impossible to determine if injuries were truly antemortem
  • Wound track distortion: Trochar insertion creates new neck wounds potentially obscuring the original injury pattern

What can still be documented (1 mark): The ligature mark pattern (if clearly defined), macroscopic findings of petechiae (if still visible), external injury pattern, and any retained foreign trace evidence. Careful documentation with photography of all remaining findings is essential.

Part D — Antemortem vs Postmortem Thyroid Fracture (5 marks)

Antemortem fracture (strangulation) features (3 marks):

  • Haemorrhage into the fracture site and surrounding soft tissues (vital reaction) — even if embalming has occurred, large haematomas may still be grossly visible
  • Petechial haemorrhages in surrounding laryngeal mucosa
  • Histology: inflammatory cells, red cell extravasation into fracture margins (though compromised by embalming)
  • Surrounding muscle oedema and haemorrhage

Postmortem artefact features (2 marks):

  • No haemorrhage at the fracture site (vessels do not bleed postmortem without cardiac pumping)
  • Clean fracture margins without tissue reaction
  • Fracture pattern consistent with the direction of neck organ removal (pathologists extend the head during neck dissection → force applied posteriorly at C6–C7 and indirectly to the larynx)
  • Note: In elderly individuals, even minimal dissection force can fracture calcified thyroid cartilage — especially important to document bone quality
CASE STUDY 0420 MARKS
Scenario: An unidentified severely decomposed body is found in a shallow grave in a forest area 3 weeks after a person was reported missing. On exhumation, the body shows greenish-black vessel outlines visible through the skin, bloating with gas, and blistering of the skin. Multiple wounds are found: some are pale white nibbled areas on the face; one is a 2 cm circular hole on the scalp with blackened edges; and bone fragments are scattered around. Insects are present throughout.
Part A (5 marks)Identify the decomposition features described and explain what each indicates about the postmortem interval and environment.
Part B (5 marks)Examine the wounds: classify the pale white nibbled areas, the circular scalp wound, and the scattered bone fragments. What are their possible origins and what artefacts must be considered?
Part C (5 marks)What are the specific challenges of performing an autopsy on this decomposed exhumed body? Describe the precautions and special techniques used.
Part D (5 marks)How can forensic entomology assist in this case? What specimens should be collected and what information can they provide?

Part A — Decomposition Features (5 marks)

  • Greenish-black vessel outlines through skin — Marbling (2 marks): Caused by haemolysis of red cells; haemoglobin from lysed cells tracks along blood vessels, staining skin in a network pattern. Indicates early-to-mid putrefaction; develops within days to 1–2 weeks depending on temperature. This is a decomposition artefact — must not be mistaken for bruising or trauma.
  • Bloating / gas distension (2 marks): Putrefactive bacteria produce gases (H₂S, CO₂, methane) that inflate the body cavities and tissue spaces. The body in early stages may appear obese (swollen oedematous corpulent) — must not be mistaken for antemortem obesity. Gas in tissues creates "gas stiffening" which can simulate rigor mortis.
  • Skin blistering (1 mark): Postmortem blisters form as putrefactive gases accumulate under the epidermis, separating it from the dermis. Must not be confused with antemortem burn or scald blisters — differentiated by histology (absence of vital reaction in PM blisters).

Part B — Wound Classification (5 marks)

  • Pale white nibbled areas on face (2 marks): Animal-induced artefacts — most consistent with rodent activity (rats) which produce nibbled pale white wounds with gnawing marks. Alternatively, insect/arthropod activity at moist areas. Key feature: no bleeding, no blood clots, no vital reaction. These must not be interpreted as antemortem assault injuries.
  • Circular scalp hole with blackened edges (2 marks): Could be: (1) gunshot entry wound — would require careful examination for abrasion collar even in decomposed state; OR (2) postmortem vulture/bird activity — birds create circular holes from their beaks that closely resemble gunshot entries; OR (3) maggot/putrefaction-produced hole with darkened rim from decomposition. Differentiating features: abrasion collar, bone fracture pattern, retained bullet/fragments — radiological examination mandatory.
  • Scattered bone fragments (1 mark): Could be from exhumation (digging damage), animal scattering, or perimortem blunt force trauma. Exhumation artefact — digging may produce postmortem bone fractures that must be distinguished from perimortem injuries (perimortem fractures show specific green bone characteristics).

Part C — Autopsy Challenges and Techniques (5 marks)

  • Identification challenges (1 mark): Standard identification methods unavailable — must use dental records, DNA, fingerprints (if fingerprint preservation is possible), anthropological assessment, jewellery/clothing, implants/prosthetics
  • PPE and biohazard precautions (1 mark): Full PPE (gloves, mask, face shield, coveralls) required; decomposed bodies carry significant infection risk; autopsy should be performed in well-ventilated/negative-pressure environment
  • Artefact recognition (1 mark): All changes must be carefully assessed — the autopsy surgeon must have comprehensive knowledge of decomposition changes to separate genuine injuries from artefacts (marbling, blistering, gas stiffening, skin slippage)
  • Radiological examination (1 mark): Mandatory in this case (suspected gunshot wound, bone fragments); full body X-ray before any incision to locate bullets, foreign bodies, identify perimortem fractures and decomposition-related changes
  • Scene and anthropological expertise (1 mark): Forensic anthropologist to assess skeletal remains; forensic entomologist for insect evidence; detailed documentation of body position, burial depth, orientation, associated evidence, soil samples

Part D — Forensic Entomology (5 marks)

Role (1 mark): Forensic entomology uses insect succession patterns on decomposing bodies to estimate the postmortem interval (PMI) — how long ago the person died. Insects colonise bodies in a predictable sequence depending on species, season, and environment.

Specimens to collect (2 marks):

  • Live larvae (maggots) of all sizes from different body regions — preserved in 75–80% ethanol for identification AND kept alive for rearing to adult for species identification
  • Adult insects present at the scene
  • Pupae and pupal casings (indicate previous fly generations)
  • Soil samples from beneath the body — contain puparia of earlier colonisers that have already completed their cycle
  • Record ambient temperature, soil temperature, maggot mass temperature, and GPS coordinates

Information provided (2 marks):

  • PMI estimation: the oldest insect species identified indicates the minimum time since death (first colonisers arrive within hours of death)
  • Whether the body was moved postmortem: presence of insect species inconsistent with the location suggests the body was moved after initial colonisation elsewhere
  • Season/environment of death: species composition reveals whether death occurred indoors vs outdoors, in a different geographic region, or under different seasonal conditions
  • Whether the body was covered/disturbed: delayed colonisation patterns suggest wrapping or burial occurred soon after death
CASE STUDY 0520 MARKS
Scenario: A 55-year-old male is found dead in his home. He lives alone. Police describe the scene as a "locked room." The body is supine on the floor. Part A — Cheek Abrasion Analysis (4 marks)

Classification: Postmortem abrasion (2 marks) — Pale yellow colour with no parchment-like darkening and no scab formation are the hallmarks of a postmortem abrasion. An antemortem abrasion would show dark brown parchment-like appearance (from drying and vital reaction), scab formation in older injuries, possible bleeding surface if fresh, and healing evidence. In postmortem abrasions, there is no vital reaction, no healing, and no scab — just pale yellow desiccation.

Medicolegal implication (2 marks): The pale yellow postmortem abrasion on the right cheek was likely caused after death — possibly from the body sliding or being moved, or contact with the floor during decomposition. This injury cannot be attributed to an assailant as a cause of the death. Police speculation of "attack" based on this finding is incorrect — it is a postmortem artefact from handling or body position.

Part B — Cherry-Red Lividity Interpretation (5 marks)

Significance of cherry-red colour (2 marks): Cherry-red postmortem lividity is characteristic of carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning. CO binds haemoglobin with 240× the affinity of oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb) which is bright cherry red in colour. This colour is retained in lividity even after death.

Differential — refrigeration (1 mark): Pink lividity can also occur in refrigerated bodies — but this body has been dead for 3 days at room temperature, making refrigeration-related pink colour very unlikely. Cherry red (vs pale pink) and the context strongly point to CO.

Gas boiler connection (2 marks): A faulty gas boiler is a major source of CO — incomplete combustion of natural gas produces CO. A locked room with a faulty boiler creates a closed environment where CO accumulates to fatal levels. This is entirely consistent with accidental CO poisoning. The boiler must be inspected, tested for CO emission levels, and documented as part of the investigation.

Part C — Forearm Wound Analysis (5 marks)

Classification: Incised wound (1 mark)

Characteristics consistent with description (2 marks): Clean-cut with well-defined margins (characteristic of sharp weapon); greater length than depth; deeper at one end ("head of wound") and shallowing progressively toward the other end ("tailing of wound").

Medicolegal context (2 marks):

  • Suicidal: Location on the forearm is consistent with suicide (accessible area). The pattern of deeper at commencement with tailing is a characteristic of voluntary self-infliction. Hesitation cuts (multiple tentative wounds) may also be present. This is a common site for suicidal incised wounds.
  • Homicidal: Less likely — homicidal incised wounds are typically in inaccessible areas; they tend to be more random; the forearm is a classic suicidal site not a homicidal target site. However, defence wounds on the forearm would be more irregular.
  • Accidental: Unlikely to produce a single clean incised wound with tailing on the volar forearm.

Part D — Manner of Death Assessment (3 marks)

Most likely manner: Accidental CO poisoning with possible concurrent suicide attempt

  • Cherry-red lividity + faulty gas boiler + locked room + found supine = classic accidental CO poisoning scenario
  • The incised forearm wound may represent a concurrent suicide attempt — but CO poisoning may have caused death before blood loss from the wound became fatal
  • Additional investigations (1 mark): Carboxyhaemoglobin level in blood (COHb >50% is typically fatal); toxicology screen for drugs and alcohol; gas boiler CO emission testing; scene investigation for CO detector absence; histology of forearm wound for vital reaction (to establish whether cut was made before or at the time of death)

Part E — Incised Wound vs Injection Marks (3 marks)

  • Shape and margins (1 mark): Incised wounds are elongated with clean-cut, well-defined margins and measurable length and depth; injection marks are puncture wounds — round to oval, small (1–3 mm diameter), with no significant length
  • Surrounding changes (1 mark): Chronic injection sites show surrounding fibrosis, hyperpigmentation, track marks (linear fibrotic cords along veins), and collapsed veins; single incised wounds do not. Fresh injection sites may show bruising around the puncture.
  • Distribution (1 mark): Injection marks are typically multiple and clustered along vein distributions (antecubital fossa, dorsum of hand, groin); suicidal incised wounds are typically on the volar forearm or wrist, parallel or slightly diagonal, often with multiple shallow hesitation cuts alongside a deeper definitive cut
