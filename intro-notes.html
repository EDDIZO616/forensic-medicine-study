Module One

Introduction to Forensic Medicine

1.1

Definitions & Terminology

Forensic Medicine

Application of medical knowledge for the purposes of law and administration of justice. Also called Legal Medicine or State Medicine.

Forensic Science

Application of science to criminal and civil laws enforced by police agencies — suitable for courts and the purpose of law.

Medical Jurisprudence

Deals with legal aspects of medical practice and knowledge. Brings doctors into contact with law.

State Medicine

Application of medical knowledge in disease prevention. Defines a doctor's duty regarding births, deaths, notifiable diseases, and food poisoning.

Etymology: "Forensic"

From Latin forensis — meaning "forum" or "of the court of law." The forum was where civil and legal matters were discussed.

1.2

History & Key Milestones

3rd Century — China
Unknown Coroner
First forensic case: wife poisoned husband and burned body. Coroner confirmed death by simulating crime with live and dead pigs. Chinese were also first to recognize potential in fingerprints.
44 BC
Antistius — First Autopsy
Roman physician who performed the first known autopsy. Reported 23 knife wounds on Julius Caesar; identified the fatal stroke between the 1st and 2nd ribs.
AD 1247
Sung Tzu — First Forensic Murder Case
Solved a murder case using forensics. Found victim was killed with a sickle — had farmers stand in sun; flies were drawn to blood residue on the guilty sickle. Origin of forensic entomology.
1547
Ambroise Paré — First Forensic Pathologist
French military surgeon who studied effects of violent trauma on vital organs. Wrote Reports in Court, first manual on writing legal medical reports.
1658
Thomas Browne
Identified adipocere — the waxy result of decay of fatty tissue. One of the first steps toward recognizing processes of decomposition.
1784
John Toms — First Ballistic Conviction
First individual convicted of murder using ballistic evidence. Pistol paper recovered from the victim was matched to newspaper found in Toms' pocket.
1814
Mathieu Orfila — Father of Modern Toxicology
Identified arsenic as a poison; developed techniques to detect it. Also developed tests for blood presence in forensic contexts. First forensic toxicologist.
1839
James Marsh — Scottish Chemist
First to introduce chemical evidence of arsenic in a body during a court trial.
1822–1911
Francis Galton — Father of Fingerprinting
Developed fingerprinting as a method to uniquely identify individuals.
1858–1946
Albert Osborn — Father of Document Examination
His work led to acceptance of documents as scientific evidence by courts.
1880s
L. Atcherley — First Forensic Psychologist
Coined the term "Modus Operandi" (mode of operation) — used to identify distinctive criminal signatures.
1882
Alphonse Bertillon — Father of Anthropometry
Police officer who measured criminals for accurate identification. Developed anthropometry — an identification system based on metric measurements. No longer in use.
1887–1954
Leone Lattes — Father of Bloodstain Identification
Developed a procedure for determining blood type (A, B, AB, or O) from dried blood stains.
1890
Edward Henry
Developed the first fingerprint classification system, sorting prints by physiological characteristics. Used until the 1990s.
Early 1900s
Karl Landsteiner & Leone Lattes
Identified human ABO blood groups. First time blood could be used to include or exclude individuals as suspects.
1929
Calvin Goddard — First Ballistic Crime Solution
Mobsters dressed as police executed seven men in Chicago. Goddard's analysis showed bullets did not come from police-issued firearms, leading to arrests.
1930s
J. Edgar Hoover — Father of the FBI
Organized a national forensic laboratory to offer services to all US law enforcement. Directorship spanned 48 years and 8 presidential administrations.
1.3

The Scientific Method in Forensics

  • 01
    Identify the Problem Crime is discovered; police arrive at the scene.
  • 02
    Background Research Observe and collect physical evidence found at the crime scene. Done by police and investigators.
  • 03
    Construct Hypothesis Police attempt to answer: what happened, how did it happen, and when? The crime-scene investigator does not form a hypothesis — sends evidence to the lab instead.
  • 04
    Establish Procedures Send physical evidence to the lab for testing. E.g. hair is tested for color, texture, shape.
  • 05
    Analyze Results E.g. toxicology report shows no drug use + no follicular tag means hair will likely not be linked to anyone. Tests help reduce the number of potential suspects.
  • 06
    Draw Conclusion After all evidence is processed, a conclusion is drawn. If a suspect can be linked to the crime, the suspect is apprehended.
1.4

Disciplines of Forensic Science

01
Forensic Pathology
Autopsy-based investigation of sudden, unexpected, or violent deaths. Autopsy takes place in pathology department; police may attend to gather evidence.
02
Forensic Anthropology
Examination of skeletal remains. Answers questions on species, gender, age, race, stature, nutritional status, disease, and skeletal trauma.
03
Forensic Odontology
Dental identification of human remains and evaluation of bite-marks. Most used for badly burned or decomposed bodies.
04
Forensic Entomology
Studies insect succession patterns in decomposing bodies. Larvae analysis allows scientific estimation of time of death and sometimes location of death.
05
Forensic Toxicology
Identification and quantification of drugs and poisons in body tissues. Requires both a qualitative screening test and a confirmatory quantitative test for forensic validity.
06
Forensic Psychiatry
Evaluates mental state of criminals. Forms psychiatric profiles of murder suspects. "Psychological autopsy" used for determining state of mind of suicide victims.
07
Computer Forensics
Investigates digital and electronic crimes — identity theft, credit card fraud, child pornography. May analyze a deceased person's computer to determine method of suicide.
08
Forensic Artistry
Draws or sculpts images of suspects or victims based on witness accounts. Facial reconstruction over skulls; computer aging of missing persons.
09
Forensic Engineering
Examines collapsed structures, vehicle collisions, engine defects, electrocutions. Called upon when a specific death relates to engineering failures.
1.5

Case Study — The Menendez Brothers (1989)

Real-World Application · Multiple Forensic Disciplines
Menendez Brothers v. State of California
  • August 20, 1989: Parents Jose and Kitty Menendez found shot dead. No forced entry, nothing stolen — suggested victims knew their attackers.
  • No gunshot residue tests administered at scene. Brothers not initially considered suspects.
  • By year end, brothers spent over $1 million of $2 million inheritance — drew police suspicion.
  • Investigators searched firearms records and found two shotguns sold on August 18, 1989 — signature on purchase did not match the named buyer.
  • Court ordered handwriting samples from both brothers. Erik refused — key investigative detail.
  • Forensic evidence used: ballistics, handwriting analysis, financial records, firearms registration.
  • March 1990: brothers arrested. First trial (1993) resulted in mistrial.
  • Second trial (1995): convicted of first-degree murder and conspiracy. Sentenced April 17, 1996 to life without parole.

Module Two

Trace Evidence

2.1

Definition & Locard's Exchange Principle

"Trace evidence encompasses virtually anything that can be touched, broken, pulverized, transferred, shed, and left behind during a crime — if a piece of it, no matter how small, can be found during the investigation."
PRINCIPLE 01

Every contact leaves a trace.

PRINCIPLE 02

The exchange of traces is a two-way process. The more violent the contact, the more likely transfer will occur.

Sir Edmond Locard (1877–1966) — In sexual and physical assault, biological material transfers from assailant to victim, victim to assailant, and both to the scene.
2.2

Classification of Trace Evidence

Biological Evidence
  • Body fluids — blood, saliva, semen, sweat, vomit
  • Body tissues
  • Hair, nail clippings, bone, teeth
  • Stains (e.g. paint from body contact)
Non-Biological Evidence
  • Personal items — clothing, spectacles, purse, wristwatch
  • Environmental materials — carpet fibres, soil, vegetation
  • Crime objects — bullets, pellets, weapons, bottles, tablets
2.3

General Collection Methods

Method How It Works Key Notes
Lifting Adhesive tape rolled over surface; placed on clear plastic sheeting Protects against contamination; allows easy viewing
Scraping Spatula dislodges trace materials onto clean paper Best conducted in controlled lab environment
Vacuum Sweeping Vacuum with filter trap; filter preserved after use Filters, traps, and parts must be changed between uses to avoid cross-contamination
Combing Clean comb or brush through hair of individual Comb and debris packaged together
Clipping Nail clipping and scraping under fingernails Right and left hands packaged separately
The collector must know: what to collect (source), from where (site), how much (quantity), and how (procedure). Proper collection = proper material + proper site + proper quantity + proper method.
2.4

Collection Phases

01
Overview Phase
Review case history, study documents, visit scene. Note body position, object locations, overall layout.
02
Detailed Discovery
Walk through area end-to-end twice. Move objects. Look under the body. Focus on areas of interest.
03
Recovery Phase
Collect each specimen. Identify and note its location. Photograph where possible.
04
Review Phase
Go through all prior phases. Ensure nothing is left — evidence not collected now is lost forever.
Forms of specimens: Dry → scrape or wet-swab then air-dry  |  Wet → dry first then scrape or swab  |  Mixed → cut and separate the relevant portion
2.5

Packaging, Preservation & Labelling

Type of Analysis Container Lid Preservative
Chemical Analysis Plastic wide-mouth jar Plastic lid Saturated saline
Histopathology Glass (sized to organ) Glass lid 10% formal saline or 95% alcohol
Serology Sterilized glass test tube Glass or rubber stopper Anticoagulant — Ca. Oxalate or Heparin
Each container label must include:
  • Jar number (1, 2, 3…)
  • Specimen name and site of collection
  • Preservative used
  • PMR / MLC number
  • Date and time of collection
  • Name of collector
  • Examination required
  • Doctor's name, signature, and official seal
Forwarding letter must include:
  • Copy of MLR / PMR
  • Copy of FIR (First Information Report)
  • Copy of inquest report
  • Specimen of the seal
  • Request letter with bio-data and specimen name
  • Examination required
All containers must be sealed with wax to maintain integrity and a secret chain of custody. Specimens should be dispatched immediately after autopsy. Stored by lock and key in freezers if lab is closed.
2.6

Chain of Custody

"Any and all who handle the specimens at a particular instance should be able to certify the extent of handling."
What must be documented:
  • What was collected
  • Why it was collected
  • To whom it was handed
  • When it was handed over
  • How long it remained in custody
Principles:
  • Shorter chain = better
  • Fewer persons involved = surer
  • Every surrender must be on written receipt
  • Maintained until specimens are presented in court
  • Handling must be certified in writing
2.7

Blood Evidence

Liquid Blood Collection
  • Collected from blood pools or clothing using gauze pad or sterile cotton
  • Must be refrigerated or frozen and delivered to lab as quickly as possible
  • Sample dried at room temperature first; after 48 hours the sample may be useless
  • If mailed: completely air-dry before packaging in brown paper bag or box
  • One item per container; never use plastic (moisture promotes microorganisms)
Dried Bloodstain Collection
  • Small object: send entire object to lab after packaging and labelling
  • Large transportable object: cover stain with paper, tape to object to avoid contamination
  • Non-transportable surface: cut out stained area (provide control sample separately) OR tape-lift using fingerprint tape, OR scrape flakes into paper packet
  • Clothing: air-dry entire article, package separately, send to lab
  • Hard surface dry stain: scrape off with scalpel, collect on glazed paper
  • Blood on plaster or wood: cut that portion or chip off plaster
  • Blood in soil: collect sufficient amount of surrounding soil
Special CaseCollection Instruction
Disputed PaternityPeripheral vein; anticoagulant only (no other preservative); collected in front of all contesting parties; cross-signed by opposing party
PoisoningCollected directly from heart or large veins at autopsy
CO PoisoningLayer of liquid paraffin placed over blood to prevent evaporation of CO
Alcohol IntoxicationDo NOT clean collection site with spirit — would contaminate the sample
Quantity: In living persons — 10–15 ml. At autopsy — 50–100 ml.
2.8

Hair Evidence

What hair can reveal:
  • Racial background
  • Use of drugs, toxins, heavy metals
  • Nutritional deficiencies
  • DNA identification (if follicle is present)
Microscopic examination:
  • Macroscopic: length, color, curliness
  • Magnification: 40–400×
  • Examines cuticle scale pattern
  • Cortex pigmentation and medulla pattern
  • Comparison microscope to match two samples simultaneously
Collection of questioned hair:
  • Photograph hair in place before touching
  • Wear new, unused, clean latex or nitrile gloves
  • Use cleaned or disposable tweezers; take care not to crush or break the hair
  • Place on clean sheet of paper or Post-it note (adhesive holds lightly without damage)
  • Place in labeled envelope: case number, date, exhibit number, description, collector's name
  • Alternative: tape-lifting (wrap clear packing tape sticky-side-out over roll; roll over item; lay on clear plastic) or vacuuming with filter unit
Known hair exemplars (for comparison): Collect 25 pulled + 25 combed/naturally shed hairs from various areas of the head and/or pubic region. Head and pubic hairs are the only human hairs suitable for comparison; beard hairs may also be suitable.
2.9

Other Evidence Types

Fibres 2.9.1
  • Remove with forceps or gloved fingers
  • Place in paper evidence fold, glassine envelope, or on Post-it note adhesive edge
  • Do NOT crush or bend; do NOT use high-tack tape (no duct tape, packing tape, lint rollers, masking tape)
Semen 2.9.2
  • Composition: 60% seminal vesicle, 30% prostate, 10% epididymis/bulbourethral
  • Average ejaculate: ~3.5 ml; 10–50 million sperm/ml
  • Coagulates immediately; liquefies after ~15 minutes
  • Oligospermia, aspermia, vasectomy — seminal fluid still forensically detectable
  • External: fabric stain — cut portion, shade-dry, preserve
  • Internal: 3 pre-hymenal swabs + 2 post-hymenal swabs; patient in lithotomy position
  • Slides: swab rubbed on middle third; two slides air-dried and face-to-face with matchstick spacers
  • Pipette method: instil 20 ml saline into vagina; aspirate with bulbed pipette
Saliva 2.9.3
  • Classical marker: amylase enzyme (also in pancreatic juice — not fully specific)
  • ~80% of people are secretors — secrete blood group substances in saliva, semen, tears, vaginal secretions
  • Found on cups, envelopes, bitten food, and bite marks on skin
  • True bites: dental impression for suspect ID; Love bites: suction hemorrhages (neck, breasts, medial thighs)
  • Single swab technique: classical method — wet cotton swab rolled over area
  • Double swab technique (preferred): wet swab rehydrates cells → dry swab absorbs them → yields more DNA
  • Control sample: rinse mouth with saline then water → chew paraffin → collect 50–60 ml
  • Preservation: heat bath 10 min (inactivate enzyme) → centrifuge 1500 RPM 3 min → preserve supernatant; no preservative added
Vomitus 2.9.4
  • Medical term: emesis
  • Relevant in poisoning, drug abuse, hanging, sexual abuse
  • Tested for drugs or poisons if administered
  • Rarely found at crime scene; limited evidentiary value on its own
Sweat 2.9.5
  • Collected over a minimum of one week
  • No preservative added
  • Used for workplace drug testing
  • Non-invasive but difficult to collect adequate volume
  • Useful for qualitative analysis only
Bone & Teeth 2.9.6
  • Wash and air-dry completely; roll in brown paper, seal in cotton cloth
  • 30 g minimum in screw-cap plastic container; no formalin ever
  • Preference order: Femur → Tibia → Humerus → Molar teeth → Ribs
  • Send at least two intact bones
  • Completely burnt bone is NOT useful for DNA analysis
  • For identity of deceased: collect intact long bones or molar teeth in duplicate
Nails 2.9.7
  • Grow at 1/3–1/10 the speed of hair; drug levels similar to hair
  • Detectable from 2 weeks post-ingestion; no timeframe determination possible
  • Used as a screening tool; hair used to narrow timeframe
  • Clip overhang as in normal grooming; also scrape under nails
  • Package separately into 4 self-sealing bags: left hand, right hand, left foot, right foot
  • Wipe scissors with alcohol wipe between each nail set
