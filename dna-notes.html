Forensic Medicine · Unit V

Forensic DNA Analysis

A complete, organized study guide covering DNA fingerprinting, typing methods, laboratory procedures, statistical interpretation, and forensic applications — compiled from all lecture sources.

01

What is DNA Fingerprinting?

DNA fingerprinting (also called genetic fingerprinting or DNA profiling) is a technique that identifies individuals on the basis of their unique DNA sequence patterns. The human genome is made up of approximately 3 billion nucleotides, 99.9% identical between any two people. Forensic science exploits the 0.1% variation to distinguish individuals with extremely high certainty.

Core Principle

Every person's DNA is unique (except identical twins). DNA from any cell in the body — blood, semen, saliva, hair root, skin — carries the same unique genetic code and can be used for comparison.

DNA fingerprinting focuses specifically on non-coding intron regions containing highly polymorphic repetitive sequences. Because exons are conserved (they code for proteins), introns show far greater variability and serve as ideal forensic markers.

The Four Fundamental Forensic Questions
  1. 01 Whose DNA is it?
  2. 02 From what body fluid did it originate?
  3. 03 How did it get there?
  4. 04 Have the results been reported in a fair and balanced way?
02

History & Origins

1984
Sir Alec Jeffreys — First DNA Fingerprint

Developed the technique at the University of Leicester while studying inherited variation. He began by examining myoglobin gene sequences in seals, identified a repeating "stutter" sequence (minisatellite), and realized similar sequences in humans could distinguish individuals. First DNA fingerprints were produced using a probe that bound to multiple minisatellite regions.

1983
First Criminal Case

DNA fingerprinting was first applied to criminal casework in the investigation of the 1983 and 1986 rapes and murders of Lynda Mann and Dawn Ashworth in the UK.

1987
First Exoneration & Conviction

Richard Buckland was exonerated through DNA analysis (the first DNA-based exoneration). Colin Pitchfork was subsequently convicted — the first conviction using DNA evidence.

1990
FBI establishes CODIS

The FBI selected 13 independently assorting STR markers to form the core of the Combined DNA Index System (CODIS). Expanded to 20 core loci in 2017.

1995
UK National DNA Database (NDNAD)

Established in April 1995, managed by the Home Office on behalf of UK police forces. Designed to maximise investigative use and identify repeat offenders.

03

DNA Structure & Inheritance

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the genetic blueprint of all living organisms. It consists of four chemical bases — A (adenine), T (thymine), C (cytosine), G (guanine) — attached to a sugar backbone. Two strands pair up (A–T, G–C) and twist into the famous double helix structure.

Genome Size

Each human cell contains approximately 6.5 billion base pairs. The full genome is 3 metres long and packaged into 23 pairs of chromosomes.

Inheritance

Half of a child's DNA comes from the mother, half from the father. Full siblings share on average 50% of their DNA. Identical twins share nearly 100%.

Mutations

Each individual acquires approximately 30–100 new mutations relative to their parents' genomes. This constant influx makes any two genomes essentially unique.

Exception

Identical (monozygotic) twins have essentially identical DNA, with only very rare new somatic mutations distinguishing them. This is the one true limitation of DNA identification.

Locus (pl. Loci)

A specific, unique site along the DNA of a chromosome characterized by a defined sequence of bases. Forensic analysis targets multiple loci simultaneously to build a DNA profile.

04

Nuclear vs. Mitochondrial DNA

Nuclear DNA (nDNA)
Location: Cell nucleus
Size: ~3.2 billion base pairs
Copies/cell: 2 (diploid)
Inheritance: Half from each parent
Use: Primary forensic typing (STR)
Uniqueness: Individual-specific
Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA)
Location: Mitochondria (cytoplasm)
Size: ~16,569 base pairs (circular)
Copies/cell: Hundreds to thousands
Inheritance: Maternal lineage only
Use: Old/degraded samples, hair shafts
Uniqueness: Shared by all maternal relatives
Y Chromosome DNA
Location: Cell nucleus (Y chr.)
Copies/cell: 1 (males only)
Inheritance: Paternal lineage only
Use: Male contributor in mixtures
Uniqueness: Shared by paternal relatives
Note: Rare mutations differ between relatives
⚠ Important Note

Both Y-STR and mtDNA analyses are less discriminating than autosomal STR profiling because they are shared among all relatives in the respective lineage. Far more individuals may share the same profile compared to STR profiling.

05

Coding vs. Non-Coding DNA

The human genome contains both exons (coding sequences that produce proteins) and introns (non-coding sequences between genes). Forensic DNA fingerprinting targets non-coding regions because:

  1. 1 Exons are highly conserved across individuals (they encode proteins necessary for life) → low variability → poor discriminating power.
  2. 2 Introns contain highly polymorphic repetitive sequences (STRs, VNTRs) that differ greatly between individuals → high variability → strong discriminating power.
  3. 3 Non-coding STRs carry little information about ancestry, disease predisposition, or phenotypic traits → important for privacy and ethical reasons.
06

Short Tandem Repeat (STR) Analysis

STR analysis is the primary method of modern forensic DNA typing. STRs are regions in the genome where a short sequence of 1–6 base pairs is repeated multiple times in tandem. The number of repeats varies between individuals, creating a highly polymorphic marker.

Allele

Each version of an STR locus with a specific number of repeats. For example, allele "13" at a given locus means 13 repeating units of the 4-base sequence. An individual carries two alleles per locus (one from each parent), which may be the same (homozygous) or different (heterozygous).

Example: GATA repeat at a single locus
Person 1: [GATA]×8 → Allele 8
Person 2: [GATA]×9 → Allele 9
Person 3: [GATA]×10 → Allele 10
6.1
Why Multiple Loci Are Required

If only one STR locus were tested, many unrelated people would share the same alleles by chance. Analysing multiple independent loci multiplies the rarity of the combined profile exponentially — making the probability of a random match essentially negligible.

Period STR Loci Analysed System / Kit
1995–1999 6 loci SGM (Second Generation Multiplex)
1999–2014 10 loci AmpFlSTR® SGM Plus®
2014–present (E&W, NI) 16 loci DNA-17 multiplexes (ESI17, NGM SElect, ESS Plex SE)
2014–present (Scotland) 23 loci AmpFlSTR® GlobalFiler™
USA (current) 20 core loci CODIS (PowerPlex Fusion 6C, GlobalFiler)
6.2
CODIS Criteria (USA)
  1. 1 Known chromosome location; must assort independently of all other CODIS markers.
  2. 2 Must have multiple alleles in all examined populations.
  3. 3 Alleles must be consistently, reliably, and accurately amplified by PCR.
  4. 4 PCR products must distinguish alleles clearly enough for automated analysis.

CODIS STR markers use the naming convention D#S### — "D" = DNA, the number = chromosome, "S" = single repetitive sequence (found once in genome). Example: D7S820 is on chromosome 7 and contains 5–16 GATA repeats.

6.3
Allele Frequencies & Match Probability

Population databases record how often each allele appears in a given population. The frequency of a specific allele at one locus provides a probability. Multiplying frequencies across all tested loci gives the Random Match Probability (RMP) — the chance that a randomly chosen, unrelated person shares the same full DNA profile. With modern 16–20 locus panels, RMP values routinely reach 1 in 10³⁰ or smaller.

07

Y Chromosome STR Analysis (Y-STR)

Y-STR analysis examines STR loci located only on the male-specific Y chromosome. Because the Y chromosome is transmitted from father to son with little change, all males sharing a direct male ancestor carry essentially the same Y-STR haplotype (differing only through rare mutations).

Primary Use

Detecting male contributor DNA in samples containing predominantly female DNA — most critically in sexual assault cases where male DNA is swamped by the female victim's DNA.

Limitation

Cannot distinguish between paternal male relatives (brothers, father, sons, cousins through paternal line) — they all carry the same Y-STR haplotype.

Inheritance

Passed intact from father to all sons. All males in a patrilineal lineage share the same Y-STR profile (with rare mutation exceptions).

08

Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) Analysis

mtDNA analysis is used when nuclear DNA is unavailable, degraded, or in extremely small amounts. Because there are hundreds to thousands of copies of mtDNA per cell (compared to just 2 of nuclear DNA), it is far more likely to survive in old or damaged samples.

When mtDNA Is Most Useful
  1. 1 Hair shafts without roots (nuclear DNA absent).
  2. 2 Old skeletal remains (bones and teeth).
  3. 3 Samples where nuclear DNA has severely degraded.
  4. 4 Tracing maternal lineage in missing persons or mass disaster identification.

mtDNA changes only ~2–4% every million years through random mutation, allowing maternal lineage tracing across many generations. Forensic scientists analyse the HV1 and HV2 hypervariable regions of the mitochondrial genome, comparing single nucleotide differences between samples and a reference.

Example of mtDNA difference between two unrelated individuals:
Person 1: ACCGGTTGCAAG
Person 2: AGCGGTACCAAG
(underlined positions indicate differences)

All of a mother's children, and all their maternal-line relatives, share the same mtDNA profile. This significantly limits discriminating power compared to autosomal STR profiling.

09

Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism (RFLP)

RFLP was the first DNA fingerprinting method developed by Jeffreys. It uses restriction enzymes (molecular scissors) to cut DNA at specific recognition sequences. Because DNA sequences vary between individuals, enzymes cut at different locations, producing fragments of different lengths — the RFLPs.

1
Cut DNA with restriction endonucleases

Restriction enzymes recognize specific 4–8 base sequences and cut there. Polymorphisms in these sites mean cutting patterns differ between individuals.

2
Gel electrophoresis

Fragments are separated by size through an agarose gel under an electric current. DNA's negative charge causes it to migrate toward the positive electrode; smaller fragments move faster.

3
Southern Blotting

Separated fragments are transferred from gel to nylon membrane. Single stranded DNA is fixed to the membrane by heat or UV light.

4
Hybridization with radioactive probe

Radioactively labelled single-stranded DNA probes complementary to VNTR sequences are added and bind to matching fragments.

5
Autoradiography / X-ray film

Radioactive probe decays and emits light, creating dark bands on X-ray film. The unique banding pattern is the DNA fingerprint.

⚠ Limitation

RFLP requires large amounts of high-quality DNA and is sensitive to degradation. Warm, moist conditions rapidly degrade DNA and render it unsuitable for RFLP. This is why PCR-based STR methods have largely replaced it in modern forensics.

10

VNTRs and Minisatellites

Variable Number Tandem Repeats (VNTRs) are repetitive sequences scattered throughout the non-coding regions of the genome where the repeat unit is typically 10–60 base pairs. They are also called minisatellites in the original Jeffreys system.

VNTRs

Repeat units of 1–100 bp, repeated many times. Everyone inherits VNTRs from both parents but has a unique combination due to crossing over during meiosis.

Minisatellites

Short repetitive sequences of 10–60 bp, occurring at 1,000+ locations throughout the genome. Highly variable between individuals. Used in original Jeffreys DNA fingerprinting.

STRs (Microsatellites)

Smaller repeat units of 1–6 bp, dispersed through chromosomes. Now the standard in forensic science because they can be amplified by PCR even from tiny/degraded samples.

11

Sample Collection & Preservation

11.1
Sources of DNA Evidence
Body Fluids

Blood, semen, saliva (from cigarette, drink can), vaginal fluid, urine.

Cellular Material

Hair roots, skin cells (touch DNA), buccal cells, bone fragments, teeth.

Known Samples

Buccal (mouth) swabs from suspects, victims, family members — scraping inside cheek.

11.2
Collection Methods
  1. 1 Swabbing: Moistened sterile DNA-free swab rubbed over hard surfaces (knives, windows). Often double-swab technique.
  2. 2 Cutting: Stained fabric sections cut out for analysis.
  3. 3 Tape lifts: Surface material collected from clothing collars and similar surfaces.
  4. 4 Intimate samples: Taken by medical practitioners in sexual assault cases (vaginal, anal swabs).
  5. 5 Targeted sampling: Scientist predicts contact areas based on case circumstances (e.g., grip area of a knife handle, knot of a ligature).
11.3
Preservation

Most biological evidence is best preserved dry and/or frozen. These conditions reduce DNA degradation and prevent mould/bacteria growth. Samples are packaged in tamper-evident bags, transported to the forensic laboratory, inspected and signed for on arrival, then generally frozen. Extracted and purified DNA is stored in sealed tubes at 4°C (refrigerator) or −20°C (freezer).

12

PCR Amplification

The Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) is the cornerstone of modern DNA fingerprinting. Developed by Kary Mullis (Nobel Prize, Chemistry 1993), PCR copies a specific target DNA sequence millions of times, making even trace amounts detectable.

Why PCR Is Essential

Forensic samples often contain only nanogram or picogram quantities of DNA — far too little to detect directly. PCR amplifies the target region exponentially so it can be analysed. In 28 cycles, PCR produces over 268 million copies of the target sequence.

12.1
PCR Mechanism — Three Steps Per Cycle
94°C
Denaturation

High temperature breaks hydrogen bonds between complementary bases, separating the double-stranded DNA into two single strands (templates).

60°C
Annealing

Temperature drops; short DNA primers (complementary to flanking sequences of the target STR) bind (anneal) to their complementary positions on the template strands. This defines the exact region to be copied.

72°C
Extension

Taq polymerase enzyme synthesizes a new complementary strand, adding dNTPs (nucleotides) to the 3' end of the primer. The cycle repeats, doubling copies each time.

Exponential amplification: 2ᴺ copies after N cycles
28 cycles → 2²⁸ = 268,435,456 copies
Target input: ≥500 pg DNA is ideal (~80 cells); ≥100 pg minimum
12.2
Multiplex PCR Kits

Modern forensic labs use commercial multiplex PCR kits that simultaneously amplify all required STR loci in a single reaction. Each primer pair carries a fluorescent dye (different colours for loci of similar sizes) allowing automated detection. Examples: PowerPlex® Fusion 6C (Promega), GlobalFiler™ (Thermo Fisher), Investigator ESS (QIAGEN).

12.3
DNA Quantitation (Before PCR)

Before PCR, labs use quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) to measure how much DNA was recovered. This determines: whether there's enough for one or multiple tests; whether to concentrate the sample further; the ratio of human to male DNA; extent of degradation; and degree of PCR inhibition (substances in the sample that block PCR).

13

Electrophoresis & Detection

After PCR, fluorescently labelled amplified STR fragments are separated by size using capillary electrophoresis (CE). DNA fragments migrate through a polymer-filled capillary under an electric field — smaller fragments move faster. A laser excites the fluorescent dyes and detectors record the signal.

Electropherogram

The graphical output of capillary electrophoresis. The horizontal axis shows fragment size (base pairs); the vertical axis shows fluorescence intensity (amount of DNA). Peaks represent alleles at each STR locus. This is the primary document analysed by the forensic scientist.

13.1
Reading the Electropherogram
  1. 1 Gender marker (amelogenin): Males show two different-sized peaks (X and Y); females show one (X only).
  2. 2 Single-source sample: 1–2 peaks per locus. If homozygous (same allele from both parents), one peak of approximately double height.
  3. 3 Mixed sample: 3–4 or more peaks per locus indicate multiple contributors.
  4. 4 Peak height: Approximates DNA quantity at that allele. Used to assess mixture ratios and major/minor contributors.
13.2
Common Artefacts to Recognise
Stutter

Small peaks typically one repeat unit smaller than the true allele, caused by polymerase slippage during PCR. Usually <15% of the true allele peak height.

Pull-up (Bleed-through)

A strong signal in one colour channel causes artefact peaks at the same size position in an adjacent colour channel.

−A peaks

When the polymerase fails to add a single A base to the end of each fragment, a "shoulder" one base smaller than the main peak appears.

Ski Slope Pattern

In degraded DNA, larger fragments give disproportionately low or absent peaks. Smaller fragments amplify normally. This characteristic pattern indicates degradation.

14

DNA Profile Generation — Full Workflow

1
Laboratory Examination

Examine submitted item visually and with chemical tests or alternative light sources to detect and locate body fluids.

2
Sample Recovery

Collect biological material by swabbing, cutting, or tape lift. For sexual assault cases, differential extraction separates sperm DNA from other cellular DNA.

3
DNA Extraction & Purification

Cells are lysed, proteins degraded, and DNA recovered using magnetic beads/silica columns or organic separation. DNA is stored in sealed tubes.

4
DNA Quantitation (qPCR)

Measure the amount and quality of recovered DNA. Determines optimal PCR input and whether low-template protocols are needed.

5
PCR Amplification

Multiplex PCR amplifies all required STR loci simultaneously using commercially validated kits with fluorescently labelled primers.

6
Capillary Electrophoresis & Detection

Amplified fragments are separated by size. Laser excites dyes; detector records fluorescence to generate the electropherogram. ~1 hour.

7
Data Interpretation & Genotyping

Scientist analyses the electropherogram, assigns allele designations to each peak at each locus, distinguishes true alleles from artefacts, and generates the numerical DNA profile.

The DNA profile is expressed as a string of numbers — the count of STR repeats on each allele at each tested locus (e.g., for 16 loci this produces 32 numeric values plus a sex designation). This is what gets compared and stored in databases.

15

Southern Blotting

Southern blotting is the classical technique used in the original RFLP-based DNA fingerprinting. After gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size, the fragments are transferred ("blotted") onto a nylon membrane for hybridization analysis.

1
Transfer to Nylon

Separated DNA fragments are drawn from the agarose gel onto a nylon membrane using capillary action or electric current.

2
Denaturation & Fixation

Chemical treatment breaks hydrogen bonds, separating DNA strands. Single-stranded DNA is cross-linked to the nylon by heat or UV light.

3
Hybridization with Radioactive Probe

Single-stranded radioactively labelled probe DNA (complementary to VNTR sequences) is applied. It binds specifically to matching fragments.

4
Autoradiography

X-ray film is placed against the membrane. Radioactive decay exposes the film, producing dark bands at positions where probe hybridised. The banding pattern = DNA fingerprint.

16

Match Probability & Likelihood Ratio

When a DNA profile from a crime scene matches a suspect, there are three possible explanations:

  1. 1The suspect is the true source of the crime scene material.
  2. 2A second unrelated person with an identical DNA profile is the source.
  3. 3The match is a false positive due to contamination, sample switch, or error.
16.1
Match Probability
Match Probability

The probability that a randomly chosen, unrelated individual would have the same DNA profile as observed at the crime scene. In the UK, the minimum reported value is 1 in 1 billion (capped) even if the actual calculation yields values like 1 in 1 trillion.

The cap exists because: (1) statistical assumptions become difficult to verify at smaller probabilities; and (2) numbers beyond 1 in a billion are practically incomprehensible to a jury. About 1 in 1,000 individuals has an identical twin — this provides a practical upper limit to discriminating power.

16.2
Likelihood Ratio (LR)
Likelihood Ratio

The probability of the observed evidence under the prosecution hypothesis divided by the probability under the defence hypothesis. LR = P(Evidence | Prosecution) / P(Evidence | Defence). In simple cases, LR = 1 / Match Probability. In complex cases multiple competing hypotheses are evaluated. UK cap: 1 billion.

The LR is the internationally preferred method for expressing weight of DNA evidence. A higher LR means the evidence is more consistent with the prosecution's proposition than the defence's.

16.3
Logical Fallacies to Avoid
Prosecutor's Fallacy

Incorrectly equating the match probability with the probability of innocence. A 1 in 3 million match probability does NOT mean a 1 in 3 million chance the suspect is innocent. Guilt depends on all evidence, not just the DNA match.

Defence Lawyer's Fallacy

Arguing that because ~20 people in the UK might share the same 1-in-3-million profile, there's a 1 in 20 chance the suspect is guilty. This ignores all other evidence that identified the suspect.

⚠ Key Point for Exams

A DNA match alone should NEVER be used to imply a suspect's involvement in a crime. DNA evidence must always be evaluated in the full context of the case, including other non-DNA evidence and credible alternative explanations for the DNA's presence.

17

Mixed & Complex DNA Profiles

A complex DNA profile involves one or more of:

  1. 1Low template: Less than optimal DNA quantity (typically <100 pg or ~16 cells).
  2. 2Mixture: DNA from multiple contributors present simultaneously.
  3. 3Degradation: DNA broken into short fragments; larger STRs give disproportionately weak or absent peaks.
  4. 4Inhibition: Chemical substances in the sample interfering with PCR efficiency.
17.1
Interpretation Software — Three Types
Type How It Works Best For Examples
Binary Scientist manually assigns peaks as allele/artefact. No peak height consideration. Simple mixtures (2–3 contributors), no low-template issues. Lab-developed spreadsheets
Discrete Variables Manual peak assignment; software accounts for low-template effects but not peak heights. Up to 4 contributors; some low-template issues. LRmix Studio, Resolve, LikeLTD 4+, LabRetriever
Continuous Variables Software considers ALL peak information including heights, stutter, degradation, and low-template effects using probabilistic modelling. Complex mixtures; 3+ contributors; degraded/low-template samples. STRmix, TrueAllele, LiRA-HT, DNA View Mixture Solution
⚠ Critical Point

Different software programs using different statistical models on the same DNA data can produce different LR values. Reproducibility is expected within each software type, but results can differ more markedly across types. When DNA profiles are complete, most programs will calculate LR > 1 billion (all capped the same). Differences mainly appear with incomplete or low-level profiles.

17.2
Low Template DNA

When input DNA is below optimal, stochastic effects dominate:

  1. 1Allele drop-out: One allele at a locus fails to amplify above the detection threshold, appearing absent.
  2. 2Allele drop-in: Sporadic non-reproducible peaks appear (often from trace contamination).
  3. 3Stochastic variation: Peak heights vary unpredictably between replicates.

Testing replicate samples (2–3 runs) assesses reproducibility. If replicate peak patterns are consistent → sufficient DNA for standard interpretation. If replicates show very different patterns with missing peaks → special validated methods required, or profile must be rejected.

18

Contamination Control

Definition

Contamination = introduction of extraneous DNA (or biological material containing DNA) to a sample after a trained official has control of the crime scene.

Because PCR is extraordinarily sensitive, even single molecules of foreign DNA can amplify and confound results. Contamination can occur at every stage — crime scene, transportation, laboratory processing.

Common Contamination Routes
  1. 1Crime-scene examiner not changing gloves between exhibits.
  2. 2Talking/sneezing without a properly fitted face mask at crime scene.
  3. 3Analyst inadvertently adding their own DNA in the lab.
  4. 4Suspect transported in same vehicle as complainant before sampling.
  5. 5Multiple suspects processed together (should be separate facilities, different medical practitioners).
  6. 6DNA-contaminated swabs, reagents, or consumables used.

All forensic labs maintain elimination databases — DNA profiles of all lab staff, crime scene staff, police officers, and consumable manufacturers — so any contamination can be identified and distinguished from case evidence. Labs must comply with ISO 17025 international quality standards, independently verified by UKAS (UK Accreditation Service).

19

DNA Transfer & Persistence

Understanding transfer is crucial because finding someone's DNA at a crime scene does not automatically mean they were there during the crime. Transfer requires three things: source, opportunity, and mechanism.

19.1
Types of Transfer
Primary Transfer

Person A directly contacts an object → deposits DNA on it. Most straightforward and common scenario.

Secondary Transfer

Person A touches Person B, who then touches the object. A's DNA may be found on the object even though A never touched it directly.

Tertiary Transfer

Person A touches Object 1 → Person B touches Object 1 → Person B touches Object 2. A's DNA may appear on Object 2. Low probability but demonstrated experimentally.

19.2
Factors Affecting Touch DNA Transfer
  1. 1Person-to-person variability (some individuals are "good shedders," others are not).
  2. 2Time elapsed and activities since last hand-washing.
  3. 3Intensity of contact (brief touch vs. firm grip).
  4. 4Surface characteristics — wet/dry, rough/smooth, absorbent/non-absorbent.
Key Scientific Principles (Published Research)
  1. 1It is NOT possible to determine when DNA was deposited.
  2. 2DNA can persist for many months depending on environmental conditions.
  3. 3Secondary (and tertiary) transfer can occur — a person's DNA may be on objects they never touched.
  4. 4Secondary/tertiary transfer without the transferring person's own DNA has been demonstrated only when transfers occur immediately after each other.
  5. 5Each successive transfer involves DNA loss, but profile quality cannot rule out a specific transfer type.

Error Rates in DNA Analysis

Source of Error Frequency (proportion of samples)
Police handling of suspect sample/records < 1 in 2,000
Police handling of crime sample/records < 1 in 50,000
Forensic lab handling of suspect sample < 1 in 10,000
Forensic lab handling of crime sample < 1 in 5,000
Lab error in known sample interpretation* < 1 in 10,000
Lab error in mixed profile interpretation* < 1 in 500

*Each error relates to a single part of the DNA profile only (one of ~34 values in a full profile). For cases going to court, additional quality checks by a second scientist further reduce effective error rates below these submission figures.

Quality is maintained through quarterly checks, "near-miss" scanning of the national database (flagging profiles differing by just one allele), and mandatory dual-scientist review for court cases.

22

Applications of DNA Fingerprinting

Criminal Investigations

Identifying suspects; exonerating the innocent; linking serial crimes; cold case resolution.

Paternity / Kinship Testing

Determining biological parentage; immigration disputes; inheritance cases; identifying family members in mass disasters.

Sexual Assault Cases

Identifying assailants from biological evidence; Y-STR to detect male DNA in predominantly female samples.

Missing Persons / Disaster Victims

Identifying human remains using nuclear STR or mtDNA compared to reference samples from maternal/paternal relatives.

Exoneration (Post-conviction)

Re-examining old evidence with modern sensitive methods to exonerate wrongly convicted individuals.

Non-Human Applications

Plant identification; livestock pedigree authentication; animal species identification; tumour cell analysis; cell line quality control.

Bone Marrow Transplants

Cell chimaerism studies — tracking donor vs. recipient cells after transplantation.

Forensic Genetic Genealogy

High-density SNP typing of cold-case DNA → search open genealogy databases → family tree detective work → narrow to candidate suspect → STR confirmation. Example: GEDmatch (now Verogen).

23

Future Advancements in Forensic DNA

Rapid DNA Systems

Closed, integrated systems generating a full STR profile in 90–100 minutes. Used at police booking stations for arrestee enrollment. Being explored for field use in mass disasters and border crossings.

Next Generation Sequencing (NGS)

Sequences the exact base composition of STR alleles (not just length). This reveals multiple "flavors" of the same length allele — increasing discriminating power significantly. One STR length allele can have 10+ different sequence variants with different population frequencies.

Phenotype Prediction

SNP markers that predict physical traits (skin, hair, and eye colour) from DNA for investigative intelligence. Not yet used as courtroom evidence — currently investigative use only.

Ancestry Estimation

Biogeographical ancestry prediction from SNP panels. Provides investigative leads about geographic origins of an unidentified contributor.

Improved Body Fluid Tests

More accurate chemical/molecular tests for determining the body fluid of origin (blood, semen, saliva, etc.) — enabling scientists to be more definitive about sample type.

Whole Genome Sequencing

Becoming faster and cheaper. Use of different genome regions for human identification in criminal justice not yet fully explored but emerging.

Key Glossary

Allele
One of two or more versions of a genetic sequence at a specific locus. Characterised by a specific number of STR repeats.
Allele Drop-Out
Failure of a detectable allele to produce a signal above threshold, usually due to low template DNA. The allele appears absent in the profile.
Allele Drop-In
Sporadic allele peaks not reproducible across replicates, often from trace contamination. Explained as low-level extraneous DNA.
Amelogenin
Sex-determining marker present in all forensic STR kits. Males show two different-sized peaks (X and Y); females show one (X only).
Capillary Electrophoresis
Method of separating fluorescently labelled PCR products by size through a polymer-filled capillary. The standard detection platform in modern forensic labs.
Degraded DNA
DNA that has broken into short fragments due to aging, heat, moisture, or UV exposure. Larger STR alleles give weak or absent peaks ("ski slope").
Electropherogram
Graphical output of CE analysis. Horizontal = fragment size (base pairs); vertical = fluorescence intensity. Peaks represent alleles at each locus.
Genotype
The genetic make-up at one or more loci. At each STR locus, consists of two alleles (one from each parent) — the genotype.
Haplotype
A group of genes or DNA sequences inherited together from one parent. Y-STR profiles and mtDNA sequences are haplotypes.
Likelihood Ratio (LR)
Probability of evidence under prosecution hypothesis divided by probability under defence hypothesis. Expresses weight of DNA evidence. UK cap: 1 billion.
Locus (Loci)
A unique physical location of a DNA sequence on a chromosome. Multiple loci are analysed to build a DNA profile.
Match Probability
The probability that a randomly chosen unrelated person shares the same DNA profile as the crime-scene sample. UK minimum reported: 1 in 1 billion.
Minisatellite
Repetitive DNA sequences of 10–60 bp occurring at 1,000+ locations. Used in original Jeffreys RFLP-based fingerprinting.
Multiplex PCR
Co-amplification of multiple STR loci simultaneously in a single PCR reaction using multiple primer pairs, each labelled with fluorescent dyes.
PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)
In vitro process that exponentially copies a target DNA region through repeated temperature cycling. Produces billions of copies from a few starting molecules.
Pull-Up (Bleed-Through)
Artefact where strong fluorescent signal in one colour produces false peaks in an adjacent colour channel at the same size position.
RFLP
Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism. Differences in DNA fragment sizes produced by restriction enzyme digestion due to sequence variation between individuals.
STR (Short Tandem Repeat)
Regions of 1–6 bp repeated sequences in tandem. Number of repeats varies between individuals. Primary target of modern forensic DNA profiling.
Stutter
Artefact peak one repeat unit smaller than the true allele, caused by polymerase slippage during PCR. Usually <15% of true allele peak height.
Touch DNA
DNA transferred to or from surfaces via skin contact rather than from a specific body fluid. Amount varies by individual and contact type.
VNTR
Variable Number Tandem Repeat. Larger repeat units (1–100 bp) than STRs. Used in original RFLP fingerprinting; microsatellites/STRs have largely replaced them.
Validation
The process of providing objective evidence that a method, process, or device is fit for its specific intended purpose. Required by the Forensic Science Regulator for all methods used in casework.

Forensic Case Examples

Case 1
Blood on Suspect's Clothing (Body Fluid Attribution)

A complainant was stabbed and a DNA profile was obtained from a large, heavy stain on the suspect's clothing. A presumptive blood test gave a very strong positive result. The mixed DNA profile showed the major contributor matched the complainant and the minor contributor matched the suspect (as the habitual wearer). Conclusion: Reasonable to attribute the major DNA component to the blood staining and the minor component to the wearer's own skin cells.

Case 2
Rape Investigation — Sperm Fraction Evidence

Suspect denied intercourse but claimed to have masturbated two hours prior and only touched the complainant externally. A high vaginal swab revealed significant sperm heads visually. After differential extraction (separating sperm from other cells), the sperm fraction DNA matched the suspect. The non-sperm fraction matched the complainant. Conclusion: The findings are highly improbable if the suspect merely touched the external genitalia after masturbating, and far more probable if full intercourse with ejaculation occurred.

Case 3
Knife in Stabbing — Transfer Assessment

A cleaned knife (no visible blood) was swabbed separately on blade and handle. Blade: weak profile matching complainant. Handle: weak mixed profile with suspect as major contributor. Key evaluation: The complainant's DNA on the blade does not automatically indicate use in the stabbing — it could be from previous handling (e.g., kitchen knife from complainant's own home). Context of where the knife was found is critical to interpretation.

Case 4
Firearm Found at Suspect's Lock-Up — Transfer vs. Direct Contact

Illegal firearm found wrapped in a plastic bag. Trigger swab: low-template profile matching the defendant (no other contributor). Handle/barrel: no profile. Suspect claimed DNA transferred from the bag (which he admitted was his) to the gun. Evaluation: Direct transfer to the trigger is the simplest scenario. The bag-to-gun transfer requires the correct bag area to align with the trigger, passing through the trigger guard — additional steps each requiring specific conditions. Scientist concluded findings more probable if the prosecution scenario (direct contact) is true.

Paternity Example
Determining Biological Father

DNA from mother, child, and two alleged fathers is cut with a restriction enzyme and run on a gel. The child's DNA must be a combination of one allele from the mother and one from the father. If the child shares alleles with Man A that Man B cannot contribute, then Man B is excluded as the father and Man A is identified as the biological father. One mismatch at any locus = complete exclusion.
