Unit One
Forensic Entomology
1.1

Definition & Scope

Forensic Entomology is the application of the study of insects and other arthropods to legal matters, particularly criminal investigations.

Branches & Applications

  • Medicocriminal Forensic Entomology — the main branch; use of arthropods in violent crime investigation (murder, suicide, rape, physical abuse).
  • Death Investigation — establishing the post-mortem interval (PMI), movement of a corpse, manner and cause of death, association of suspects with the death scene.
  • Toxicology via Insects — detection of drugs and toxins through analysis of insect larvae that fed on the corpse.
  • Contraband Trafficking — entomological evidence in smuggling cases.
Key Phrase: "Dead men do tell tales" — insect activity on a corpse reveals a predictable biological timeline that the trained forensic entomologist can read.
1.2

History of Forensic Entomology

  • Sung T'zu (1235 AD) — Yuan Dynasty Chinese mandarin; earliest recorded forensic use of insects. Wrote "Washing Away of Wrongs." Observed that maggots appearing at temples, hairline, rib cage or belly before natural orifices indicates those areas were injured — pointing to cause of death.
  • Dr. Bergeret d'Arbois (1855) — Western world's first forensic entomology case; used entomological knowledge to help Paris police solve the murder of a child.
  • J.P. Megnin (1894) — Published "La Faune des Cadavres," a monograph on identification of blowflies; first systematic reference work; used technique in a case of a long-dead infant.
  • Hall (1948) — Published monograph on blowfly identification; rekindled scientific interest.
  • Pekka Nuorteva of Finland (1970s) — Published series of case histories, reviving modern interest.
  • Keh (1985), Smith (1986), Catts & Goff (1992) — Published modern textbooks and reviews establishing the discipline.
1.3

Ecological Succession on a Corpse

A cadaver is a rich but ephemeral (short-lived) resource. There is intense competition, especially in early stages of decomposition. Insect colonization occurs in a predictable series of stages — different groups are adapted to different decompositional stages.

Exam Key Point: Succession is predictable. This predictability is the foundation of PMI estimation using entomology. Flies prefer fresh flesh; beetles arrive later for tougher material.
1.4

Stages of Decomposition

1.5

Major Insect Groups on Cadavers

Flies (Diptera)

Blowflies, Flesh Flies, House Flies, Cheese Skippers. First arrivals; prefer moist fresh flesh; vital for PMI.

Carrion Beetles (Silphidae)

Arrive after putrefaction begins. Chewing mouthparts; can process tougher material than fly larvae.

Rove Beetles (Staphylinidae)

Early predatory visitors; feed on larvae and eggs of all fly species including predatory fly larvae.

Hister Beetles (Histeridae)

Among first beetles; hide under corpse in daylight; nocturnal hunters of blowfly larvae and pupae.

Hide Beetles (Dermestidae)

Late arrivals; dominant late-stage scavengers feeding on dried skin and tendons.

Scarab Beetles (Scarabaeidae)

Arrive when body is completely dry, alongside dermestids.

1.6

Flies (Diptera) — Detailed

Blowflies — Family Calliphoridae (Blue/Green Bottles)

  • Attractive metallic blue-green color (common names: blue-bottles, green-bottles); also come in non-metallic brown form.
  • Can detect faint traces of decay odour from up to 20 km away.
  • Among the first insects to arrive at a cadaver; prefer fresh, moist flesh.
  • Adult females arrive within minutes; lay approximately 250 eggs in natural openings and wounds.
  • Forensically critical species: Phaenicia (Lucilia) sericata, Phormia regina, Calliphora vomitoria, Cynomyopsis cadaverina.

Flesh Flies — Family Sarcophagidae

  • Most breed in dead animals, dung, or decaying material.
  • Some species are larviparous — deposit live larvae (not eggs) directly into wounds, hence the name.

Common House Flies — Family Muscidae

  • Adults most common at corpses in early stages of decomposition when corpse is moist.
  • Larvae are normally dung feeders; secondary colonizers of corpses.

Cheese Skippers — Family Piophilidae

  • Attracted by the "cheesy" odour from butyric fermentation in later stages.
  • Also pests of cheeses and hams.
  • Forensically notable: found in coffins buried up to 3 m deep and in corpses up to 10 years old.
1.7

Beetles (Coleoptera) — Detailed

Key Distinction from Flies

Critical: Flies prefer fresher meat; beetles arrive after putrefaction begins. Beetles have chewing mouthparts allowing them to process tougher material (skin, cartilage, tendons) that fly larvae cannot manage.
  • Carrion Beetles (Silphidae): Over 200 species; subfamily Necrophorinae are the corpse-feeders. Adults feed on both carrion and fly larvae but cannot survive on carrion alone — require access to maggots.
  • Hister Beetles (Histeridae): First beetles to arrive; hide under corpse by day; active at night preying on maggots. Lay eggs in corpse; larvae feed on blowfly pupae.
  • Sap Beetles (Nitidulidae): Small beetles; feed on tree sap, fungi, fruit juices, and carrion.
  • Rove Beetles (Staphylinidae): Eat fauna on/in the corpse; adults eat larvae and eggs of all fly species; larvae also predatory.
  • Hide Beetles (Dermestidae): Late-arriving specialist scavengers feeding on dried skin and tendons; dominant late-stage.
  • Ham/Checkered Beetles (Cleridae): Common in later decomposition stages; elongate, often metallic/red/yellow; larvae feed on dried fat and pupate inside empty fly pupal cases sealed with silk.
  • Scarab Beetles (Scarabaeidae): Arrive when body is completely dry.
  • Carcass Beetles (Trogidae): Among the last to inhabit; very thick exoskeleton; dark coloration; feed on dried skin and ligaments; larvae live in vertical burrows underneath the carcass.
1.8

Maggot Morphology & Fly Life Cycle

Two Maggot Types

  • Smooth maggots — pioneer flies; purely corpse feeders; arrive first.
  • "Hairy" maggots — secondary flies; feed on corpses and are active predators of smooth maggots. Arrival is later than pioneer maggots because they need existing maggot prey.
Beetle maggots are also generally predatory, consistent with adult beetle behaviour.

Black Blow Fly Life Cycle (Exam Detail)

  • Adult female blowflies arrive within minutes of death.
  • Each female deposits approximately 250 eggs in natural body openings and open wounds.
  • Eggs hatch into 1st instar (stage) maggots within 24 hours.
  • 1st instar → feeds → moults → 2nd instar → feeds for several hours → moults → 3rd instar.
  • Masses of 3rd instar maggots generate heat, raising local temperature by more than 10°C — this must be accounted for in PMI calculations.
  • 3rd instar maggots move away from the body and metamorphose into adult flies (pupation in soil).

General Beetle Life Cycle

  • Female may lay from several dozen to several thousand eggs in her lifetime; singly or in clumps.
  • Larvae feed voraciously (often predatory); larval period can be several years in some species.
  • All beetle larvae go through multiple instars (developmental stages between moults).
  • Beetles are holometabolous: egg → larva → pupa → imago (adult).
  • Adult lifespan extremely variable: weeks to years depending on species.
1.9

Post-Mortem Interval (PMI)

The PMI is the elapsed time from death to the discovery of a cadaver. Flies (especially Calliphoridae and Sarcophagidae) are vital in PMI determination.

Three Premises Underlying PMI Estimation

  • Flies will begin egg-laying (oviposition) as soon as they discover a body.
  • Succession on a corpse is predictable.
  • Insect development is predictable and temperature-dependent.
1.10

Temperature & Insect Development

Each developmental stage requires a certain amount of time. Development rate is temperature-dependent: warmer = faster; cooler = slower. Each species has a specific developmental rate at any given temperature.

Degree-Day / Degree-Hour Accumulation

Because simple time measurement is insufficient, forensic entomologists use Accumulated Degree Days (ADD) or Accumulated Degree Hours (ADH).

Formula for Degree-Day (base 10°C):

DD = (Daily Max – 10) + (Daily Min – 10) ÷ 2

If max or min is less than the threshold (10°C), enter zero.

1.11

PMI Determination Methods

Method 1: Isomegalen Diagram

A graph showing maggot length (in mm) plotted against time/temperature. The investigator uses maggot size and observed temperatures to read off estimated age.

  • Example: An 8 mm maggot, daily max 24°C, daily min 16°C → read development stage directly from the diagram.
  • Best used when temperature records are reliable and the situation is straightforward.

Method 2: Controlled Rearing

  • Collect sample from the crime scene (always collect the largest maggots present).
  • Rear samples under constant laboratory conditions until adults emerge.
  • Determine the ADD/ADH required to complete development after collection.
  • Subtract this value from total required for the species to determine the unknown accumulated amount since oviposition.
  • Count back the days/hours prior to collection needed for the maggot to reach the sampled stage.
Choosing the method: Isomegalen diagrams are quick and suitable for field estimates. Controlled rearing provides more precise PMI when laboratory facilities are available. The situation dictates which is best.
1.12

Crime Scene Protocol — Determining PMI

  • Step 1 — Collect Insect Samples: Critical to collect the largest maggots on the cadaver (even if few). Collect representative samples of all other insects present. Preserve some immediately (in 70–80% ethanol). Rear others to adulthood for species confirmation.
  • Step 2 — Determine Temperature History: Use air temperature data (airport weather readings, etc.). Validate with micro data-loggers placed at the scene. Identify any "windows" when insect access was possible (e.g., body indoors with windows open/closed).
  • Step 3 — Estimate Time of Egg-Laying: Based on species present and their developmental stage, calculate how long they took to develop to that point at the recorded temperatures.
  • Step 4 — Seek Corroborating Evidence: Look for other insect evidence (pupae, pupal cases, shed skins, beetle presence) that corroborates or challenges the PMI estimate.

Unit Two
Causes & Manner of Death
2.1

Definition of Death

  • Oxford Dictionary: The end of life.
  • Chambers 20th Century: The extinction of life.
  • Black Law Dictionary: The cessation of life; the ceasing to exist.
  • Medical/Forensic context: Simply diagnosed as cessation of heartbeat and breathing.
  • Modern consensus: Permanent and irreversible cessation of the tripod of life — nervous, circulatory, and respiratory systems.

Harvard Criteria (4 Criteria Required)

  • Total unawareness of externally applied stimuli.
  • No movements or breathing during a continuous one-hour observation period by physicians.
  • No reflexes (blinking, eye movement, tendon reflexes).
  • Flat electroencephalogram (EEG — brain activity).
Caveats: Severe hypothermia and certain paralyzing drugs can mimic death (no heartbeat detected, no breathing). Patients on life-support add further complexity. A doctor requires a 5-minute period of absent heart sounds before pronouncing life extinct.
2.2

Tests for Death (WHO Criteria)

1. Tests for Stoppage of Nervous System

  • No movement.
  • No response to stimuli.
  • No jerks.
  • No reflexes.
  • Confirmed by EEG (Electroencephalograph — flat trace).

2. Tests for Stoppage of Respiration

  • Inspection, palpation, and auscultation of breathing — repeated for at least 5 minutes.
  • Feather Test: feather/cotton placed at nose — no movement.
  • Mirror Test: mirror placed at nose/mouth — no hazing.
  • Winslow's Test: water bowl placed on chest — observe light reflection for movement.

3. Tests for Stoppage of Circulation

  • Inspection, palpation, and auscultation for heart sounds — repeated for at least 5 minutes.
  • Detection of pulse and BP: absent.
  • Icard's Test: 1 mL of 20% alkaline fluorescein S/C — no colour change spread.
  • Ligature Test (Magnus Test): ligature on finger — no swelling or bluish colour.
  • Diaphanous Test (Finger Web Inspection): transilluminate hand — yellowish and opaque (vs pinkish and translucent in the living).
  • Heat test — no blister or redness on skin.
  • Confirmed by ECG.
  • Earliest and surest sign: Segmentation/trucking of blood in retinal vessels — viewed by ophthalmoscope.
2.3

Cause of Death (COD)

The COD is the disease(s) or injury(ies), or combination thereof, that initiates an uninterrupted chain of events leading to the cessation of life.

Death Certificate Structure (WHO)

Part I — Chain of causation (most recent to oldest):
A. Most recent condition (immediate cause)
B. Due to: older condition
C. Due to: even older condition
D. Due to: Underlying/Proximate COD — what started it all

Part II — Other significant conditions contributing to death but not part of the above chain.

Mechanism of Death (examples)

The immediate physiological derangement causing death:

  • Exsanguination — bleeding to death
  • Cardiac dysrhythmia — irregular heart rhythm
  • Air embolism — gas bubbles blocking vessels
  • Cardiac tamponade — heart compressed by blood
  • Sepsis — overwhelming infection
  • Dehydration — insufficient water

Classic Case Study ("But For" Rule)

A 15-year-old is shot, becomes paraplegic, and dies at 32 of sepsis from a UTI. The underlying COD is the gunshot wound. Certificate reads: Sepsis → UTI → Paraplegia → Remote spinal cord injury → Gunshot wound of back. "But for the gunshot wound sustained 17 years earlier, the person would not have died." — Illustrates the "but for" rule of causation
2.4

Mode of Death

The Mode of Death refers to the system whose failure initiates the process of death.

  • Coma — failure of the nervous system
  • Syncope — failure of the circulatory system
  • Asphyxia — failure of the respiratory system
2.5

Manner of Death (MOD)

The MOD describes the circumstances by which the cause of death occurred. Five legal options:

1. Natural

Solely from natural disease process or multiple diseases.

2. Accident

Unforeseen events involving an external factor.

3. Suicide

Intentional self-harm with intent to end one's life.

4. Homicide

Killing of one human being by another — not necessarily criminal.

5. Undetermined

Insufficient evidence to determine manner; used when evidence supports multiple equally viable manners.

Priority Rule: When multiple CODs with different MODs, non-natural overrides natural. Homicide overrides all others. Accident overrides natural. Therapeutic complication fits after accident.
2.6

Natural Death

Deaths resulting solely from an underlying natural disease. Must be 100% natural — even a 1% non-natural component changes the ruling.

Important Nuances

  • Chronic alcoholism → Natural (recognized as a disease itself; external influence over extended period).
  • Death from malaria, West Nile virus, bacterial meningitis, influenza → Natural.
  • Wasp sting → subsequent bacterial infection → sepsis → death: may be ruled Accidental (bacterial infection is not a typical outcome of a wasp sting).
  • Lab worker needle-stick → hepatitis C → death → Accidental.
2.7

Accidental Death

Unforeseen events involving an external factor. Death may occur soon after or many years later.

Key Issues

  • Recreational drug overdose death: Not natural, not usually homicide. The "unforeseen" element is disputed — most jurisdictions rule it Accidental (or "unclassified" where available) because many drug users do not die, making the odds of death low (unlike Russian roulette).
  • Russian roulette: High propensity of certain death → may reasonably be ruled Suicidal.
  • Predator attacks / allergic reactions to stings: Debated — some argue natural (hypersensitive immune system), others argue accidental (exogenous sting).
  • Acute toxic effects of recreational drugs → Accident.
  • Chronic effects of drug abuse → Natural.
2.8

Suicide

Death resulting from the intentional action of an individual to harm themselves with the intent to end their life.

Key Points

  • A suicide note is not required — only one-quarter to one-third of victims leave notes.
  • In clear cases: method + scene + investigation provides ample evidence of intent (e.g., vacuum hose over tailpipe into car window).
  • Complex cases: stepping on ladder, tying rope, kicking away ladder — appears suicidal but thorough investigation mandatory.
  • Suicide by motor vehicle: Single vehicle vs fixed object (tree, bridge) with no obvious explanation, no evidence of braking, vehicle stopped at crossing waiting for traffic/train.
  • Long-term survival after suicidal act, with eventual death related to that act, can still be ruled Suicide (consistent with ruling homicides years after the event).
Exam scenario: Suicidal person ingests acetaminophen, then while walking is run over by a stolen car with a lacerated heart. The MOD is Homicide — the car caused the actual fatal injury; homicide overrides all other MODs.
2.9

Homicide

The killing of one human being by another. Does not require criminal intent. Can be justifiable (preventing a felony, self-defence) or criminal.

Key Principles

  • Intent not required: Armed robber fires warning shot → ricochets → kills person = Homicide.
  • Intent can matter: Intentionally chasing another vehicle to run it off the road and driver dies = Homicide.
  • Arson deaths: Arsonist may not intend to kill, but fire death = Homicide.
  • "Homicide by heart attack": Victim with severe heart disease dies of fatal arrhythmia triggered by stress of being the victim of a criminal act = Homicide.
  • Death occurring years later from an original injury inflicted by another person: MOD ruling should reflect circumstances of original injury.
2.10

Sudden Unexpected Natural Death

Death that occurs suddenly when a person not known to have any dangerous disease/injury/poisoning is found dead or dies within 24 hours after onset of the terminal illness. Incidence: approximately 10% of all deaths.

Sudden Cardiac Death (SCD): Natural death due to cardiac causes, with abrupt loss of consciousness within 1 hour of onset of acute symptoms.

Causes by System

System% of Sudden DeathsKey Examples
Cardiovascular 40–50% Coronary atherosclerosis, coronary thrombosis, myocardial infarction rupture, aortic rupture, pulmonary embolism, cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, arrhythmia
Respiratory 15–23% Lobar pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, air embolism, bronchial asthma, acute glottic oedema, pneumothorax, lung collapse

Medico-legal significance: In cases of sudden death, it is usually not possible to certify COD from external examination alone. An autopsy is mandatory to exclude unnatural death. A doctor who issues a certificate without autopsy risks being accused of obstructing justice if foul play is later discovered.
2.11

Death Certification

Requirements for a Doctor to Issue a Death Certificate

  • Inspection of the dead body by the doctor personally.
  • Full satisfaction about the fact of death.
  • Must be sure of the cause of death.
  • Must be free from least suspicion of foul play.
  • Must have seen/examined the person as a living patient within 14 days before death.
  • Must be a registered qualified medical practitioner.

Unit Three
Narcoanalysis
3.1

Definition & Etymology

Narcoanalysis (also called the "truth serum" test) is a forensic interrogation technique in which a subject is administered a sedative/narcotic drug to induce a semi-conscious state, purportedly making it more difficult to fabricate answers.

  • Etymology: "Narkc" (Greek) = anaesthesia / torpor + analysis.
  • Coined by: Horseley.
  • "Father of Truth Serum": Dr. Robert Ernest House — Texas obstetrician who first proposed using scopolamine for criminal interrogation.
3.2

History

  • 1920s: First used as a psychotherapeutic measure; drug of choice was scopolamine.
  • 1922: First time used in the United States in a criminal investigation.
  • Later evolved to use sodium amobarbital (Amytal) and sodium thiopental (Pentothal) as safer alternatives to scopolamine.
3.3

Principle

  • Normally, a person can lie because they can use imagination to fabricate.
  • The drug neutralises the subject's imagination by making them semi-conscious.
  • In the semi-conscious state, it becomes difficult to lie — answers are restricted to facts already stored in memory.
  • Efforts are made to obtain "probative truth" — truth that can be verified and used to direct investigation.
In the drug-induced state, the individual will reply to questions with childlike simplicity and childlike honesty, without evasiveness, guile, deceit or fraud, as the answer to a query stored in his mind as memory. — Dr. Robert Ernest House
3.4

Staff Required

  • Physician — overall medical supervision and responsibility.
  • Anaesthetist — induces and maintains the narcotic state; monitors vital signs throughout.
  • Forensic Psychologist — conducts the interview; formulates and asks questions.
  • Videographer — records the entire session for legal purposes.
  • Supporting Nursing Staff — patient care and assistance.
3.5

Drugs & Dosage

DrugChemical NameClass

Dosing Parameters

  • Rate of infusion: <1 cc/min (slow IV infusion).
  • Standard dose: 150–350 mg.
  • Reduced dose in organic brain disease: 75 mg.
  • Dose is highly individualized — varies with willpower, mental attitude, and physique of the subject.
3.6

Procedure of Narco-Analysis

Stage 1 — Pre-Test Interview

  • Subject is fully explained regarding the entire procedure.
  • Informed consent is obtained.
  • Medical history taken; contraindications assessed.

Stage 2 — Pre-Narcotic State

  • Anaesthetist commences IV drug infusion slowly.
  • Maintains the pre-narcotic state throughout — monitoring the subject's level of consciousness.

Stage 3 — Semi-Narcotic State (Active Interrogation)

  • Semi-narcotic state is established; subject appears:
    — Flushed
    — Slowing and slurred speech
    — Nystagmus (involuntary eye movement) observed by testing eye muscle with finger.
  • Forensic psychologist conducts the interview at this stage.
  • Session is videographed in full.

Stage 4 — Recovery

  • Subject is allowed to sleep off the drug naturally.
  • Allowed to wake up on their own.
  • Upon waking, anaesthetist checks the subject and allows coffee or tea.

Stage 5 — Post-Test Interview

  • Memory is checked.
  • Subject is allowed to know what they spoke during the interview.
3.7

Contraindications

  • Airway disease including Upper Respiratory Infection (URI) — barbiturates depress respiratory drive.
  • Severe renal or hepatic disease — impaired drug metabolism and excretion.
  • History of porphyria — barbiturates are a classic trigger.
  • Hypotension — barbiturates cause vasodilatation and further drop in BP.
  • Barbiturate dependence — altered tolerance and response.
  • Psychosis — historical note: Rudolf Hess (tested); psychotic patients may produce unreliable or bizarre responses.
3.8

Arguments in Favour

  • In the drug-induced state, the subject responds with "childlike honesty" — without evasiveness, guile, or fraud (Dr. Robert Ernest House).
  • Every person is required to furnish information regarding offences — societal duty to report crime.
  • Protection against self-incrimination was designed to protect the innocent, not to allow the guilty to evade justice.
  • The framers of the Bill of Rights believed rights of society were paramount to those of individuals in certain contexts.
  • Can be useful as an investigative tool to uncover leads when all other methods have failed.
3.9

Arguments Against

  • It is not 100% accurate — certain subjects have made completely false statements while under the drug.
  • Often unsuccessful in eliciting truth — should not be used to compare with statements given to police before the drug was used.
  • A person can still give a mix of true and false information.
  • Malingerers and chronically evasive/untruthful persons are not reliably affected.
  • It is very difficult to determine the correct dose for a particular individual.
  • Dose varies with willpower, mental attitude, and physique — no universal dosing formula.
  • Some subjects become more talkative but not necessarily more truthful.
  • May elicit confabulation (unconscious fabrication to fill memory gaps).
3.10

Legal Aspects

Critical Exam Points — Legal Position:
  • Narcoanalysis, polygraph tests, and brain-mapping cannot be done without the consent of the individual.
  • If the person consents, information obtained can be used for further investigation (probe).
  • Results are NOT admissible as evidence in court, even if done with consent.
  • The use of such methods is considered illegal and unconstitutional (violates the right against self-incrimination).
  • A suspect cannot be compelled to disclose facts which they can recall from memory and which are likely to incriminate them.
Constitutional Basis: The right against self-incrimination (equivalent to the 5th Amendment concept) protects suspects from being forced to produce testimony against themselves. Narcoanalysis compels mental testimony — hence unconstitutional without genuine free consent.
Summary

How the Three Units Connect

Forensic Entomology → establishes the time and sometimes the location of death through insect succession and PMI estimation. Insects may also reveal toxins/drugs (bridging to narcoanalysis).
Causes & Manner of Death → provides the medical and legal framework for classifying the death discovered through entomological and other forensic investigation. PMI narrows the window; manner classification determines how the criminal justice system proceeds.
Narcoanalysis → an investigative technique used once a suspicious death is identified (homicide or suspected homicide). Applied to suspects or witnesses; subject to the legal constraints shaped by the same constitutional framework that governs death investigations.
